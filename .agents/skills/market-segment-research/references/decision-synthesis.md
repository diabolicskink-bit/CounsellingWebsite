# Market decision synthesis

Use this reference after integrating the market evidence and before writing the
canonical dossier's interpretation and recommendations. Turn the research into
a defensible point of view for the actual owner, service or project.

## Start from the owner's decision

Re-read the applicable product or business direction, current service facts,
owner constraints and decision questions. Identify:

- the outcome the owner is trying to improve;
- the decisions that are actually open;
- capabilities, credentials, capacity, geography, delivery mode, price or
  ethical constraints that narrow the option set;
- current strengths or assets that can be used;
- non-goals or choices already settled; and
- the time horizon for the recommendation.

Do not optimise for an abstract market when the owner cannot or should not
serve it. Do not treat existing site copy as proof that a positioning works.
Use project direction as a constraint on the advice, not as external evidence
that the market agrees.

## Build an inspectable reasoning chain

For every consequential conclusion, make the chain visible:

```text
observed evidence
+ owner context
+ evidence geography and transfer assessment
+ stated assumptions
-> interpretation
-> recommended action
-> expected benefit, risk and validation
```

Cite source IDs beside the observed evidence. Name the relevant project fact or
direction where it affects the recommendation. Label the interpretation as an
inference when the evidence does not directly establish it.

Triangulate different evidence streams without blending their units. A query
sample, public price table and provider taxonomy can jointly shape a
recommendation, but they do not become a demand measurement merely because
they point in a compatible direction.

## Use assumptions deliberately

Use assumptions to make progress when evidence or owner context is missing.
Prefer a clear assumption to vague hedging or an unspoken leap.

For each material assumption, record:

- the assumption itself;
- why it is reasonable given the known context;
- whether it is low-, medium- or high-consequence if wrong;
- which interpretation or recommendation depends on it; and
- the alternate recommendation or change in priority if it proves false.

Examples include assumed service capacity, acceptable geographic reach,
commercial priority, implementation effort, client eligibility, willingness to
publish a dedicated page, or whether a missing demand metric is worth obtaining.

Do not invent market facts, provider performance, audience preferences,
conversion rates, search volumes, budgets or capabilities and call them
assumptions. Use scenarios when more than one assumption is plausible and would
materially change the advice.

## Explain what the market means

Move beyond a compressed findings recap. Explain the mechanisms and
consequences that matter for this owner, such as:

- how discovery is mediated by directories, local search, informational pages,
  professional taxonomies, referrals or national online providers;
- what is target-market reality, what can be learned from broader markets, and
  what needs localisation or a local test before transfer;
- whether visible competitors compete through a dedicated service page, broad
  inclusion language, credential structure, price, format or delivery model;
- which query formulations map cleanly to services and which produce ambiguous
  or mixed-intent results;
- where role boundaries or regulation change what can credibly be claimed;
- which visible conventions create a baseline expectation rather than a useful
  differentiator;
- where the owner has a credible advantage, parity requirement or mismatch;
- which apparent market absences may be true whitespace, weak presentation,
  indexing artefacts or sample limitations; and
- how missing demand evidence should affect confidence, sequencing and
  measurement rather than being mistaken for no demand.

State what the research confirms, changes, weakens and leaves unresolved about
the owner's likely prior view.

## Make specific recommendations

Recommend choices, not just areas to consider. Cover every decision area the
evidence materially affects, which may include:

- segment boundary and geographic focus;
- SEO query families and intent priorities;
- dedicated pages, information architecture and content coverage;
- positioning, differentiation and market language;
- service configuration, delivery mode, offers and packaging;
- price presentation, concessions, entry offers and booking routes;
- directories, partnerships, referral channels and professional signals;
- trust, proof, scope and risk disclosures;
- measurement, analytics and first-party demand capture; and
- research or product experiments.

For each material recommendation, state:

- the action or decision;
- why it follows for this owner;
- the supporting evidence and assumptions;
- expected benefit or decision value;
- important risk, trade-off or dependency;
- confidence as high, medium or low with a short explanation; and
- the validation or trigger for revisiting it.

Include `what not to do` where a tempting action is contradicted by the
evidence, relies on a category error, exceeds current capability or would create
an avoidable trust problem.

Do not write finished visitor-facing copy. It is acceptable to recommend a
message territory, query focus, page purpose, content job, offer change or
decision rule for a downstream specialist to develop.

## Prioritise without fake precision

Use the smallest useful prioritisation scheme. Prefer `Now / Next / Later`,
ordered recommendations, or a compact table with qualitative impact,
confidence, effort and dependency.

Do not calculate a score unless its inputs and weighting have real meaning.
Explain why one action precedes another. Common sequencing logic includes:

- establish the service or capability before promoting it;
- fix category confusion before expanding acquisition;
- capture missing demand evidence before making a costly commitment;
- address a high-confidence discoverability gap before a speculative offer;
- build a shared foundational page before narrower supporting content; and
- run a reversible test before an expensive or identity-defining change.

Distinguish a recommendation that is high priority because evidence is strong
from one that is high priority because learning is cheap and valuable.

## Recommend tests and measurements

Do not use `more research` as a generic ending. Name the smallest useful next
evidence and the decision it would change.

For each proposed test or dataset, state:

- the uncertain assumption or decision it addresses;
- the metric and denominator;
- the geography, period and segment coding;
- the minimum observation window or stopping rule where relevant;
- the decision threshold or interpretation rule; and
- any privacy, access or implementation constraint.

Prefer first-party learning when market tools cannot answer the business
question: aggregate Search Console queries, landing-page engagement, enquiry
topic coding, booking starts and completions, qualified enquiry rate, attendance
or repeat-session signals. Do not collect sensitive free text merely because it
might be analytically interesting.

## Avoid shallow synthesis

Reject these weak patterns:

- repeating the key findings under a heading called `Implications`;
- listing generic possibilities without choosing a direction;
- calling every observed absence an opportunity;
- copying a mature or overseas market pattern without a transfer judgement;
- turning common competitor language into presumed audience preference;
- treating a directory count or visible result set as demand;
- recommending parity with every competitor convention;
- hiding a decisive assumption inside confident prose;
- using `test and learn` without defining the test or decision; and
- handing raw evidence to a downstream agent and expecting it to reconstruct
  the market reasoning.

The synthesis is complete when the owner can see the recommended direction,
understand why it follows, identify the assumptions that matter, and know which
actions or measurements should happen first.
