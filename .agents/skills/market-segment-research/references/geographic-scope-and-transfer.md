# Geographic scope and transfer

Use this reference when the decision concerns a specific place but useful
market learning may come from broader national or international evidence. Build
the local recommendation from multiple geographic layers without confusing
external patterns with local demand, competition or search performance.

## Define the geographic layers

Define only the layers useful to the decision:

| Layer | Meaning | Typical evidence role |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Target decision market | The place where the recommendation must work | Local results, providers, directories, prices, referral routes, availability, regulation, measured demand and current visibility |
| Serviceable market | Other places the owner can genuinely serve through the current delivery model | National online competition, non-local enquiries, broader query opportunity, channel reach and offer eligibility |
| Comparable markets | Places with meaningful similarity in language, regulation, service model, search ecosystem or buyer behaviour | Benchmarks, repeated patterns, alternative provider approaches and cross-city contrasts |
| Learning markets | Larger, more mature or differently developed markets selected for a specific learning question | Query vocabulary, intent families, information architecture, content depth, offer innovation, taxonomies, tools and emerging conventions |

A provider in a learning market is not necessarily a competitor. A national
online provider may be a direct competitor in the target market even when its
office is elsewhere. Define competition through serviceability and substitution,
not office address alone.

## Choose broader markets for a reason

Do not default to the United States merely because it has abundant results.
Choose each comparison or learning market according to the question and record
the reason. Consider:

- shared language and the likelihood that query vocabulary travels;
- comparable professional roles, regulation and permissible claims;
- similar service delivery, payment, insurance or referral structures;
- search-engine and directory ecosystems;
- cultural and demographic relevance;
- market maturity or content depth;
- data/tool availability; and
- whether the owner can serve or only learn from that geography.

Different questions may need different learning markets. The United States may
be useful for discovering search-intent families or mature content structures
while being poor for price, credential, insurance or regulatory comparison.
The United Kingdom, Canada, New Zealand or another Australian city may be more
transferable for a different question.

Stop expanding geography when another market mainly repeats represented
patterns or its structural differences make transfer too speculative.

## Separate search geographies

Record four location dimensions for every consequential search observation:

1. **Decision geography:** where the recommendation must work.
2. **Query geography:** any location explicitly written in the query.
3. **Search-observation geography:** the known or inferred location, language,
   account, device and personalisation context of the search interface.
4. **Result geography:** where the surfaced provider, publisher or directory is
   based and which places it says it serves.

These dimensions may differ. A query containing `Perth` from a generic web
search interface is not the same as a Google search physically performed in
Perth. A US page ranking for a generic English query does not establish that it
ranks in Australia. An interstate online provider can still compete for Perth
clients.

For SEO learning, use separate query sets where useful:

- target-location service queries to observe the local visible landscape;
- broader national or unmodified queries to discover Australian terminology,
  publishers and online providers;
- selected learning-market queries to discover additional intent families,
  modifiers, questions, page models and terminology; and
- validated local follow-up queries to test whether imported concepts appear
  or make linguistic and service sense in the target market.

Do not merge result counts, positions or feature frequencies across search
interfaces or geographies into one ranking table.

## Match questions to geographic layers

Use the closest layer that can answer the claim:

| Question | Strongest evidence layer | How broader evidence may help |
| --- | --- | --- |
| What currently appears for a Perth service query? | Target market search observation | Broader markets can suggest follow-up queries, not replace the Perth observation |
| Which providers can serve Perth clients? | Target plus serviceable market | External learning-only providers should remain outside the competitor count |
| What do Perth or Australian users search for? | Target/national demand tools and first-party data | Overseas demand can reveal candidate terms but cannot quantify Australian demand |
| Which query intents or vocabulary might be missing from the local pass? | National and selected learning markets | Treat imported terms as hypotheses requiring Australian language and intent checks |
| Which content structures or trust explanations are possible? | Comparable and learning markets | Assess regulation, culture, owner capability and target search evidence before recommending transfer |
| What should the service charge or claim? | Target/serviceable market plus owner economics and regulation | Overseas prices and claims are contextual examples, not local benchmarks without normalisation |
| Which directories or referral channels matter locally? | Target/serviceable market | International platforms matter only if used in or structurally informative for the target market |
| What market innovations may emerge later? | Mature learning markets | Present as directional learning or a test, not a forecast of local adoption |

## Assess transferability

For every broader-market pattern used in synthesis, classify it:

- **Directly transferable:** the underlying language, service model, regulation,
  channel and owner capability are sufficiently aligned for local use.
- **Transferable with localisation:** the pattern is useful but terminology,
  examples, trust signals, offer details, regulation or delivery need adaptation.
- **Hypothesis to test:** the pattern is promising but local demand, language,
  fit or feasibility is not established.
- **Context only:** the pattern helps explain the wider market but should not
  drive a target-market action.
- **Not transferable:** structural differences make local application
  misleading or unsafe.

Base the judgement on the dimensions that matter:

- language and search intent;
- regulatory and professional-title compatibility;
- service eligibility and delivery;
- payment, rebate, insurance and price structure;
- cultural meaning and expected trust signals;
- platform or search-ecosystem availability;
- owner credentials, capability, capacity and direction; and
- corroboration in target or serviceable-market evidence.

State the reason, the localisation needed and the consequence if the transfer
assumption is wrong. A repeated pattern across several foreign sites remains a
foreign-market pattern until this assessment is made.

## Integrate broader learning into recommendations

Keep the reasoning chain visible:

```text
broader-market observation
+ target-market evidence
+ owner context
+ transfer assessment
+ stated assumptions
-> local interpretation
-> recommendation, localisation and validation
```

Explain whether the recommendation is based primarily on local evidence,
strengthened by broader evidence, or imported as a reversible hypothesis.
Prioritise low-cost local validation when a potentially valuable pattern has
weak target-market corroboration.

Broader evidence can legitimately support recommendations about:

- additional query and intent families to investigate locally;
- page types, information architecture and content coverage;
- clearer role, scope, consent, eligibility or trust explanations;
- offer, channel, package or conversion-path experiments;
- terminology to test rather than automatically adopt;
- directories, referral models and professional taxonomies to compare; and
- future measurement or market-monitoring questions.

It must not silently supply target-market demand, ranking, competitor counts,
prices, regulation, audience preference or expected performance.

## Avoid geographic category errors

Reject these patterns:

- researching only Perth and concluding that locally uncommon SEO language has
  no strategic value;
- researching only the United States and presenting it as the Perth market;
- treating US keyword volume as Australian or Perth demand;
- copying overseas clinical titles, claims, rebates, insurance language or
  prices into an Australian recommendation;
- counting learning-market providers as local competitors;
- assuming an English phrase carries the same local meaning or search intent;
- combining search positions from different locations or interfaces;
- calling a mature-market content convention a local gap without checking the
  target market and owner fit; and
- dismissing a transferable pattern merely because it has not yet appeared in
  the smaller target sample.

The geographic analysis is complete when the dossier explains what is locally
true, what the owner can learn from elsewhere, why that learning should or
should not transfer, and how any uncertain transfer will be localised or tested.
