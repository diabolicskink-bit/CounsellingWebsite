# Page-flow gates

Use these gates after the copywriter skill has reviewed the public copy. They
govern the staged artifact and the copy's place in the page; they do not repeat
the copywriter's methods for craft, inhabited reader response, sentence
function, model glue, voice, inclusion, claims, research discipline, or
read-aloud review.

Run the relevant gates after developing a section and run all of them again
after assembling the page. Revise when a gate reveals a real problem. Record
the decision or change that follows rather than adding commentary merely to
show that the gate was visited.

## Section ownership and page position

Recover the section's place in the actual visitor journey before judging it.
Check that the workthrough states:

- what the hero and preceding sections have already established;
- the live visitor question at this point;
- the one communication job this section owns;
- what later sections should answer instead; and
- what understanding this section must make possible next.

Then ask:

- Does the recommended copy answer the assigned visitor question?
- Does it remain centred on the section's owned job?
- Has it restarted the page proposition or absorbed a later section?
- Does its level of explanation fit what the visitor has already read?
- If the section were deleted, what necessary page-level understanding would
  disappear?

If the last answer is unclear, strengthen or change the job, combine the
section with another, or remove it. Do not preserve a section merely because a
candidate contains good lines.

## Candidate and synthesis traceability

For substantial section work, confirm that the default three structurally
different angles were developed as distinct editorial compositions. Each
viable candidate should have complete copy and its own inhabited reader response
before synthesis begins. Reuse the page-level pseudoperson rather than creating
a new visitor account for each candidate.

When one candidate is selected intact, record why it serves the section and
page journey better than the alternatives.

When the recommendation is synthesised, confirm that the artifact:

- records the material or editorial move contributed by each source candidate;
- explains why the combination is stronger than selecting one candidate;
- does not require one candidate to remain the base;
- removes seams, repeated orientation, competing endings, and incompatible
  registers created by the combination;
- presents one clean recommended working draft; and
- includes a new inhabited reader response to the synthesised draft rather than
  relying on the responses to its source candidates.

Judge the synthesis as a new composition. Candidate traceability explains the
decision; it does not require preserving every useful passage.

## Coverage and non-duplication

Use the page plan's coverage map and the actual surrounding copy. Check for
repetition of:

- the hero's service identification or main judgement;
- examples, situations, terms, or explanations already used above;
- provider stance or inclusion claims;
- practical facts, links, and calls to action;
- modality explanations owned by another page or section; and
- qualifications added repeatedly because each section was drafted alone.

Distinguish useful recurrence from duplication. A precise phrase may need to
reappear for retrieval or clarity, but each occurrence should perform a new
local job. Remove or relocate material that exists only because it appeared in
a strong candidate.

During assembly, make a page-level pass for repeated claims and examples even
when the wording differs. Semantic repetition is still repetition.

## Handoff and page movement

Read the ending of the preceding section, the complete current section, and the
opening of the next section in sequence. Ask:

- Does the section begin from what the visitor now knows rather than starting
  again?
- Does each movement change, deepen, or apply the visitor's understanding?
- Does the ending make the next useful question possible without answering it
  in advance?
- Does the next opening continue the movement rather than summarising the
  section just completed?
- Would a different order better match the visitor's developing questions?

A handoff need not contain an explicit transition sentence. Prefer an earned
change in question or emphasis to connective prose added solely for smoothness.

## Assembly consistency

When assembling the full page:

- use the recommended section drafts as editable inputs, not immutable blocks;
- verify that each included draft still corresponds to the current page plan
  and stable section ID;
- reread the complete visitor journey from the hero through the final action;
- recheck section proportions, heading rhythm, and changes in register;
- remove repeated stance statements, practical facts, and calls to action;
- resolve open handoff questions in the actual sequence; and
- rerun the copywriter skill's full-page quality review after page-flow edits.

If assembly changes a section materially, update its workthrough or record the
assembly decision so the recommended artifact and assembled copy do not imply
different current directions.

## Artifact authority and assumptions

Check that every artifact clearly records its status and relationship to the
other artifacts:

- the page plan owns page journey and section jobs;
- the section workthrough owns candidate exploration and its recommended
  working draft;
- the assembled page records the selected sequence and assembly changes;
- recommended copy is not described as owner-approved or implemented; and
- cross-links, stable IDs, and filenames resolve to the current artifacts.

Keep evidence, working assumptions, and owner decisions distinct. Existing
research dossiers may be cited as supporting evidence, but this workflow must
not create, update, or maintain them. General knowledge and reasonable visitor
hypotheses may fill an evidence gap only as labelled planning assumptions.

Before public copy is treated as ready, confirm that no assumption has become a
sourced prevalence, clinical, service, or other factual claim. Qualify or omit
unsupported wording, or leave the consequential uncertainty open for separate
resolution.
