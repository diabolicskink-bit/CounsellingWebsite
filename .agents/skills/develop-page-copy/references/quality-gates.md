# Quality gates

Use these gates after drafting a section and again after assembling the full
page. They are lenses, not scores. Revise when a gate reveals a real problem;
do not add prose simply to demonstrate that a check was run.

## Visitor reading

Put the reasoning and editorial annotations out of sight. Name the specific
visitor position you are adopting, including what brought them here and what
they have read immediately before this section. Then read the copy cold from
that visitor's position rather than as its writer, the provider, or a peer
practitioner.

Repeat this for materially different, evidence-supported positions. Apply the
copywriter skill's grounded visitor-reading process; the prompts below are a
compact reinforcement rather than a replacement. For each position, ask:

- **Immediate:** What do I notice first? Can I tell why this section is here?
- **Intellectual:** What can I reasonably understand, distinguish, or expect?
- **Relational:** Am I recognised, instructed, categorised, corrected,
  pressured, or granted room to judge?
- **Sceptical:** What claim, implication, or omission would make me hesitate?
- **Next movement:** What question does the copy now make possible?

Do not invent emotional reactions as facts. Record plausible readings and the
language that produces them.

## Sentence function and deletion

For each sentence or copy movement, name its function. Useful functions include:

- orienting;
- answering;
- distinguishing;
- recognising;
- evidencing;
- setting a boundary;
- changing pace;
- creating a handoff; or
- supporting a next action.

Delete a sentence when its only function is:

- connecting two complete thoughts;
- restating the heading;
- paraphrasing the hero;
- reassuring without adding recognition;
- displaying research coverage; or
- announcing a quality the copy has not demonstrated.

When a paragraph is crowded, remove the least necessary example before adding
a summary sentence.

## Model-glue review

Look for prose that makes a draft smoother while making it less specific.
Examples include:

- universal complexity openings;
- `whether ... or ...` catalogues;
- `there is no right way` assurances;
- `you are not alone` closings;
- `you do not need to have it all figured out` thresholds;
- self-awarded `safe`, `affirming`, or `non-judgemental` claims;
- equal-length modules created for visual neatness; and
- generic invitations to `explore` or `navigate` the issue.

Do not ban a phrase mechanically. Ask whether the surrounding copy earns its
meaning. Prefer a concrete situation or distinction when it can do the work.

## Inclusion and adverse readings

Select reader positions from the brief or research, not a generic demographic
checklist. Test whether the copy:

- makes one configuration, identity, or path feel universal;
- treats one party as the default visitor and another as the obstacle;
- quietly ranks possible outcomes;
- claims the visitor's feelings or private motives;
- infers harm, health, pathology, or safety from a label alone;
- requires specialist vocabulary to be understood; or
- turns a person into a system or case formulation.

Do not solve every adverse reading inside one section. Assign the concern to the
page part that owns it.

## Research and claim integrity

Check that:

- every factual or clinical claim has an appropriate source;
- the source supports the public wording, not merely a nearby idea;
- non-representative evidence has not become `common`, `most`, or `many`;
- public discussion has not become a prevalence claim;
- research categories have not been copied into an issues directory;
- time-sensitive guidance is verified or omitted; and
- an open service fact remains an open decision rather than invented copy.

Use research to sharpen distinctions, vocabulary, coverage, and adverse-reading
tests. Do not make the page defend its audience with statistics.

## Section ownership and handoff

Ask:

- Does this section answer its assigned visitor question?
- Does it add something the hero or previous section has not already said?
- Is it doing work assigned to a later section?
- Does its ending open the next question without summarising the next section?
- If the section were deleted, what necessary understanding would disappear?

If the answer to the last question is unclear, the section needs a stronger job
or should be removed.

## Read-aloud pass

Read only the public copy, without the reasoning around it. Listen for:

- repeated sentence openings;
- list rhythm disguised as prose;
- abstract nouns accumulating without an image or situation;
- over-balanced contrasts;
- a provider speaking to peers rather than a visitor;
- phrases that sound polished but not owned; and
- an ending that resolves too neatly.

Then read the preceding and following sections with it. A sentence that works
in isolation may be redundant in sequence.
