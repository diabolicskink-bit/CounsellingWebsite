# Visual Craft Reference

Use this reference when creating or changing a website's visual direction, composition, layout, typography, colour, imagery, material, or motion.

## 1. Resolved intent

Good visual design is not a particular style. It is the visible coherence of purpose, content, hierarchy, interaction, accessibility, performance, and identity.

Expect each major choice to make meaning clearer, direct attention or action, establish a relationship, create an appropriate emotional register, carry identity, improve confidence, reduce effort, or preserve continuity across states and devices.

Decoration is allowed. Make it part of the art direction and subordinate it to comprehension.

## 2. Art direction

Art direction is the governing idea that coordinates image, layout, typography, colour, material, and motion. It is more specific than a mood and more flexible than a page template.

Build it from the subject's actual world, the visitor's likely state, the change the experience should create, two productive character tensions, and formal consequences for every visual medium.

Weak: “Clean, modern, calm, premium.”

Strong: “Create the feeling of a well-edited field guide: observant, grounded, and quietly expert. Use a stable editorial grid, candid close-range photography, a humanist reading face, restrained mineral colour, fine rules, and almost no automatic motion.”

Test whether the direction survives without the hero image. If not, the identity is asset-dependent rather than systemic.

When direction is open, assemble references from more than one source domain and use each for a named property. Draw from the subject's actual world—places, tools, documents, behaviours, language, materials, and histories—as well as relevant designed work. Do not use a reference merely to authorise a look; explain how the borrowed property serves this brief.

Translate the direction into consequences before implementation: what becomes dominant, what stays quiet, how information groups, where density changes, what material the interface suggests, what imagery proves or explains, and how interaction behaves. If several different directions would produce the same consequences, the direction is not resolved enough.

## 3. Composition

### Focal point

Give each viewport a plausible first read. Establish it through relative scale, contrast, isolation, position, directional image content, or motion. Reduce competing high-contrast events.

### Balance

Balance visual weight, not object count. Use symmetry for formality, calm, ceremony, or static confidence. Use asymmetry for editorial energy, openness, depth, or surprise. Balance a dense region with space, scale, colour, or a smaller high-contrast element.

### Proportion and scale

Create meaningful scale differences. A decisive title, clear supporting level, and quiet metadata usually communicate better than many adjacent sizes. Let the subject determine image-to-text and column proportions.

### Contrast

Use contrast through size, density, type, shape, surface, alignment, saturation, image, motion, or continuity. Prefer one strong contrast over several weak ones. Do not make every section introduce a new visual language.

### Unity and variation

Repeat alignments, type roles, spacing relationships, crop logic, and interaction behaviour. Vary them when meaning or pace changes. Coherence is not sameness.

Inspect the page as one silhouette, not only as individually successful sections. Repeated full-width bands, repeated split propositions, or repeated heading-and-copy modules can make locally polished work globally mechanical. Change the macro composition when the visitor's question, evidence, or task changes—not simply to alternate the layout.

## 4. Grid and alignment

Define the maximum content field, outer margins, columns, gutters, readable spans, persistent anchors, and rules for full-bleed media or grid breaks.

Align related elements to common edges or baselines. Let captions belong visibly to images and controls to the content they affect. Centre short ceremonial content deliberately; avoid centring long reading by habit.

Break the grid to communicate emphasis, transition, energy, or scale, then return to a known anchor. Random offsets simulate movement without creating composition.

## 5. Space and rhythm

Use proximity to express relationships. A heading belongs closer to the content it introduces than to the preceding section. A label belongs closer to its input than another field. Evidence belongs near the claim.

Choose density by audience and task. A utility can be compact; a reflective story can breathe. Avoid both card-soup compression and prestige emptiness.

Shape long pages as spatial beats:

1. orient;
2. expand with substance;
3. change pace for a real shift in meaning;
4. resolve with a decision or next step.

Zoom out or blur the page. Look for deliberate peaks, quiet intervals, and transitions rather than equal horizontal bands. Use a coherent spacing family, then correct optically for type, images, and internal whitespace.

Whitespace is relational, not a prestige effect. Use it to establish ownership, sequence, pause, or emphasis. If removing a large gap does not change comprehension or cadence, the space may be performing taste rather than communication.

## 6. Layout archetypes

| Archetype | Use for | Essential quality |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Editorial narrative | Articles, ideas, case studies, complex services | Reading rhythm, headline/deck, captions, section pacing |
| Fact-first service | High-intent, practical, or anxious visitors | Plain orientation, key facts early, action near decision criteria |
| Split proposition | A claim with meaningful visual proof | The second side demonstrates rather than decorates |
| Index or directory | Libraries, portfolios, programmes, complex navigation | Browsing and categorisation become the visual interaction |
| Immersive visual opening | Art, place, entertainment, highly visual products | Media is the subject; orientation and loading stay clear |
| Utility-first | Search, booking, calculation, application, support | The task appears early with calm instructions and complete states |
| Comparative | Plans, options, before/after, evidence | Shared attributes align and differences remain exact |

Select section forms by meaning: timeline, table, annotated image, ruled list, quotation, step sequence, form, directory, or full-bleed scene. Do not default to a row of cards.

Treat archetypes as starting hypotheses, not complete page recipes. A page may combine forms when the content genuinely changes mode, but every transition should have an informational reason. Do not add the expected hero, feature field, proof band, FAQ, or closing action solely because the category usually contains it.

## 7. Typography

Define display, reading, and utility roles. Add a special voice only for a real data, code, quotation, or cultural need. One versatile family may fulfil several roles. Pair families for functional or expressive contrast, not because a template expects a serif and sans.

Build hierarchy using more than size: weight, width, case, style, colour, spacing, and position. Keep levels decisively separated.

Extreme size contrast can create an immediate focal point while leaving the rest of the page weak. Judge the whole type texture: display silhouette, reading prominence, utility legibility, repeated heading behaviour, and the balance between expressive and practical language. Typography should carry a specific voice and information structure, not serve as the primary evidence that a layout is designed.

For continuous prose:

- use roughly 45–75 characters as an initial measure range, then test the face and language;
- use unitless line height;
- preserve paragraph continuity while enabling scanning;
- avoid fragile manual line breaks;
- test zoom, font preferences, localisation, and content extremes.

Art-direct display lines for meaning and silhouette. Inspect x-height, aperture, width, terminals, stroke contrast, numerals, italics, and punctuation rather than relying on broad category labels.

Load only required fonts and styles. Define compatible fallbacks and control layout shift.

## 8. Colour and material

Assign colour roles before collecting shades: base and alternate canvas, primary and secondary text, action and states, signature accent, border, semantic feedback, and data series when required.

Build hierarchy in greyscale first. Add hue for identity, grouping, state, and emotional temperature. Control the palette by proportion: dominant field, supporting range, and concentrated accent.

Use colour consistently and never as the sole cue. Test contrast, fatigue, imagery interactions, dark contexts, and colour-vision differences.

Choose one material logic. Flat planes, paper, luminous screens, glass, plastic, fabric, ink, or exposed technical grids imply different edges, shadows, textures, and movement. Do not mix materials casually.

Use borders and shadows to explain separation or elevation. Choose radius by character and object type. Texture should affect material feeling without impairing content or performance.

## 9. Imagery

Give imagery a declared role: evidence, explanation, identification, narrative, or atmosphere.

Define photography through subject distance, point of view, light, observed or posed behaviour, colour temperature, crop, aspect ratio, and relationship to text. Authenticity still requires composition and selection.

Define illustration through line weight, geometry, perspective, palette, texture, abstraction, character construction, and type relationship. Avoid mixing unrelated stock families.

Crop with intent. Use close crops for intimacy or detail, wide crops for context, and subject direction to support adjacent content. Supply art-directed sources when one crop cannot survive all viewports.

## 10. Motion

Classify motion as state, spatial, narrative, or atmospheric. Prioritise state and spatial motion. Keep direct feedback immediate. Make automatic and repeated movement subordinate. Avoid the same reveal on every section.

Match motion character to the art direction: crisp and short, soft and calm, elastic and playful, or largely still. Prefer transform and opacity where appropriate. Provide reduced variants for parallax, zoom, and large spatial movement.

## 11. Responsive recomposition

Change form while preserving priority and relationships. Ask what must come first, what must remain adjacent, what needs a new crop, what comparison needs another representation, what action needs persistent access, and what visual idea becomes crowded or expensive.

Choose breakpoints at content pressure, not device folklore. Test narrow phones, landscape, tablets, split-screen, narrow laptops, wide desktops, zoom, and enlarged text.

Mobile may need a linear sequence, earlier practical facts, larger targets, and fewer simultaneous events. Desktop should use its field for comparison, context, or stronger image-text relationships rather than floating an enlarged phone column.

## 12. Visual directions

Use these as coherent systems, not templates.

| Direction | Strong ingredients | Failure to avoid |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Calm and credible | Stable grid, readable humanist type, muted but contrast-rich palette, real people or places, measured space, little motion | Pale wellness sameness, low contrast, excessive emptiness, vague reassurance |
| Warm and human | Tactile colour, observed photography, natural crops, approachable type, modest irregularity | Fake grain, blobs, doodles, and stock smiles over a generic structure |
| Editorial and intelligent | Strong headline/deck, expressive display type, captions, rules, asymmetric grid, image sequence | Random serif italics and collage without editorial substance |
| Refined and luxurious | Few elements, exceptional type and imagery, controlled scale, disciplined palette, quiet transition | Tiny grey text, inaccessible contrast, slow reveals, empty prestige language |
| Bold and energetic | Large scale jumps, expressive type, saturated accent, assertive crop, directional composition, quick motion | Every element shouting and comprehension collapsing |
| Technical and precise | Exposed grid, diagrams, exact labels, crisp rules, restrained monospace, clear states | Generic navy, blue glow, meaningless code texture, jargon density |
| Playful and imaginative | Characterful type, flexible shapes, vivid colour, illustrative world, rewarding microinteraction | Unpredictable controls, excessive bounce, juvenile decoration, poor legibility |
| Raw and austere | Direct copy, sharp edges, visible structure, limited palette, plain controls, meaningful awkwardness | Unfinished work presented as a concept and hostile interaction |
