## Match the source to its role

| Source type | Use it to record | Do not use it alone to claim |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Primary quantitative study | Measured counts, percentages, associations, group comparisons, and instrument results in the studied sample | A universal audience rate or cause outside that population and method |
| Primary qualitative study | Reported themes, situations, meanings, variations, and participant accounts in the studied sample | Prevalence or how common a theme is in the wider audience |
| Systematic or scoping review | The shape, consistency, quality, and gaps of a body of research | A result the underlying studies did not measure consistently |
| Professional guidance or official source | Definitions, classifications, duties, standards, and recommendations made by that body | Audience experience, preference, or outcome |
| Community or advocacy research | Community-framed surveys, documented concerns, language, and service critiques | Independent population estimates when sampling or methods do not support them |
| Public first-person discussion | Lived language, questions, examples, disagreement, and recurrence across reviewed sources | Prevalence, representativeness, diagnosis, cause, or a general client requirement |
| Provider or competitor content | Exact market wording, service categories, claims, page types, and omissions | Audience beliefs, provider competence, good practice, or clinical effectiveness |
| Search result or query feature | Dated result composition, visible terminology, directories, modifiers, and query patterns | Search volume, stable ranking, demand, or what a project should target |
| Owner or practice evidence | Aggregated enquiries, observed service patterns, interview findings, and analytics within the authorised scope | Independent general audience evidence |

Judge whether a source is fit for the exact proposition. Do not treat all sources
as interchangeable support for one broad takeaway.

## Research formal evidence

Search by combining the audience with the experience or question being studied:

- therapy, counselling, psychotherapy, healthcare, provider, or help-seeking;
- experience, barrier, disclosure, stigma, trust, alliance, satisfaction, or
  service access;
- qualitative study, survey, review, guideline, or community report;
- relevant country, service model, demographic, or time period.

Prefer the primary paper when reporting a percentage or study-specific finding.
Read beyond the abstract when possible. Capture:

- full citation and publication date;
- study purpose or research question;
- population, inclusion criteria, setting, geography, and recruitment;
- sample size overall and for the reported result;
- method, instrument, comparison group, and time frame;
- exact count, percentage, effect, association, theme, or conclusion used;
- missing data or denominator when relevant;
- limitations that change what the result can support.

### Report quantitative findings precisely

- State `n` and the relevant denominator with a percentage when the source
  provides them.
- Identify whether the figure describes the whole sample, respondents to one
  item, or a subgroup.
- Preserve the study's time frame: lifetime, previous year, current care, and so
  on are not interchangeable.
- Distinguish association from cause and statistical non-significance from proof
  of no difference.
- Do not merge percentages from differently defined populations.
- Do not turn a convenience-sample rate into a population estimate.

Example structure:

> In Kolmes et al.'s internet survey of 175 BDSM-identified clients who had used
> psychological services, X respondents (Y% of those answering the item) reported
> [result]. The survey was self-selected and retrospective. [F##]

### Report qualitative findings precisely

Name the sample and method, then report the themes or categories the researchers
identified. Include variation, negative cases, and the number of participants or
cases when the paper provides it. Do not convert a qualitative theme into "most
clients" unless the study reports and supports that count.

Example structure:

> Lantto and Lundberg interviewed 27 Swedish BDSM practitioners with therapy
> experience. Participants described negative responses including [reported
> categories] and positive responses including [reported categories]. [F##]

Do not discard an older source solely because it is old when it directly answers
the question. Record its date and setting so downstream users can judge it.

## Research public web discussion

Use only publicly accessible material. Search several types of source where
possible:

- topic-specific forums and community sites;
- broad discussion platforms;
- first-person blogs and essays;
- public Q&A pages and comment threads;
- public reviews or service discussions;
- community organisation resources that quote or summarise participants.

Vary the query framing to find positive, negative, neutral, unresolved, and
contradictory accounts. Record:

- the number and types of sources reviewed;
- the date range and geographic skew where visible;
- which themes appeared in which source IDs;
- how many independent sources contained a theme when feasible;
- concrete contexts in which the theme appeared;
- counterexamples or disagreement.

A count of reviewed threads is a property of the research sample, not a rate
among clients. Write "the concern appeared in 6 of 13 reviewed sources," not
"nearly half of kink clients are concerned."

One vivid account is a source entry or counterexample, not a recurring finding.
Do not use public discussion to determine what a page should say.

## Research search and market evidence

Separate the observation from any later SEO or content decision. Record:

- the exact query, directory, page set, or feature checked;
- date, geography, personalisation state, and method;
- number of results or pages sampled when counted;
- result categories and service types;
- exact visible labels or wording;
- repeated features, with counts when feasible;
- sampled absences and technical limitations.

Good research statement:

> In a 16 July 2026 sample of X Australian provider pages returned for the listed
> queries, Y used `kink-aware`, Z used `sex-positive`, and N described a specific
> training source. [S##-S##]

Not a research statement:

> Visitors need proof of competence, so the page should foreground training.

Provider claims establish what providers say. Directory taxonomies establish
how a directory categorises listings. Neither establishes competence or audience
preference. An unreturned page does not establish lack of demand or a content
opportunity.

## Handle owner and practice evidence

Record owner-supplied material only when it is audience evidence, such as
aggregated enquiries, authorised interview notes, de-identified service patterns,
or analytics.

- Use only the authorised scope.
- Aggregate and de-identify sensitive material.
- Do not reproduce client stories or recognisable detail combinations.
- State the observation base and period where known.
- Distinguish practice experience from population evidence.
- Keep site copy, route state, positioning, design, and implementation out of
  the dossier.

## Report findings at the correct level

The main dossier should contain readable cross-source research, not an annotated
bibliography and not analyst strategy.

For each major topic:

1. State the finding indicated by the relevant body of evidence.
2. Cite the supporting source IDs.
3. Pull out the percentages, counts, ranges, sample sizes, comparisons, or
   qualitative categories that make the finding concrete.
4. Keep routine population, method, and limitation detail in the source
   register; repeat it inline only when it materially changes the claim.
5. Preserve disagreement and differences that prevent a combined conclusion.

Not every point needs a statistic, and not every sentence needs to name a study.
The test is whether the prose communicates what the research actually found
with enough detail to be useful and checkable.

Avoid converting evidence into unsupported abstractions.

| Avoid | Report instead |
| --- | --- |
| "Provider labels do not settle competence." | List the labels found, where they appeared, how the sources defined them, and whether common criteria were identified. |
| "Visitors need concrete evidence." | Report any study or public sources that directly asked people how they assessed providers; otherwise make no claim. |
| "Useful care demonstrates calm competence." | Name the study sample and report the therapist responses participants described as positive. |
| "The communication task is to explain the boundary." | Report the community-overlap situations and policies present in the reviewed evidence. |
| "This is a differentiating SEO opportunity." | Report the sampled query results and any counted absence; leave opportunity assessment to an SEO task. |

Do not add sections titled `Implications`, `Recommendations`, `Communication
priorities`, `What this means for the page`, or `Assumptions to avoid` to an
audience dossier.

## Combine sources without flattening them

Cross-source reporting is useful when the sources answer the same question.

- When quantitative definitions and samples are comparable, report the
  source-specific figures and an attributed range if useful.
- When they are not comparable, report the figures side by side and explain the
  difference; do not calculate a pooled audience percentage.
- State when several qualitative studies report similar categories, then give
  concrete examples from the strongest studies and retain full method detail in
  the source register.
- Keep public recurrence separate from formal findings even when themes overlap.
- Record contradictions rather than averaging them into a generic midpoint.
- Distinguish an absence of evidence from evidence that something is absent.
- Use confidence labels only if they describe evidence quality, not strategic
  importance.

Start the synthesis with a `Key findings` section that makes the strongest
documented results easy to scan before the detailed evidence. Use as many
source-bound findings as the evidence warrants, without a fixed minimum or
maximum, and include headline figures, sample sizes, source-set counts,
comparisons, or qualitative categories where available. Preserve subgroup and
setting distinctions. The section should read like a concise research summary,
not strategy, implications, or recommendations. Retain the fuller context,
variation, and limitations in the detailed sections below.

## Protect people and sensitive information

- Use only publicly accessible material; do not bypass access controls or enter
  private groups for research.
- Paraphrase public posts rather than reproducing distinctive wording.
- Omit usernames, profile links, exact dates, locations, and unusual detail when
  they could make a sensitive account traceable.
- Avoid long quotations and unnecessary exposure of painful experiences.
- Do not infer a person's diagnosis, identity, motivation, or clinical history.
- Treat slurs, reclaimed language, and contested labels as contextual evidence,
  not default vocabulary for public copy.
- Prefer theme-level citations to a collection of identifiable anecdotes.

## Record sources efficiently

Use stable IDs:

- `F01`, `F02`, and so on for formal or authoritative evidence;
- `W01`, `W02`, and so on for public web discussion;
- `S01`, `S02`, and so on for search and market evidence;
- `P01`, `P02`, and so on for owner or practice knowledge when recorded.

Keep full source details in the source register. Cite IDs beside findings rather
than repeating URLs throughout the body.

For formal sources, capture citation, source type, population, sample, geography,
method, date, exact reported result used, limits, URL, and checked date. For
public discussion, capture platform or source, broad de-identified context,
contribution to the reviewed themes, limits, URL, and checked date. For search
evidence, capture the exact query or source, geography, date, sampling method,
and observation.
