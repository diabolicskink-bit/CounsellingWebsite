# Research approach

Use this reference when planning or conducting audience research and when
turning source material into a dossier. The aim is extensive audience
understanding for downstream copywriting and page development, not a formal
literature review or an auditable research paper.

## Research widely

Combine formal sources with public material in which people describe their own
experiences, questions, language, disagreements, and contexts. Formal evidence
can supply scale, comparison, studied categories, and findings that individual
accounts cannot. Forums, blogs, community spaces, Q&A, comments, reviews,
first-person essays, and similar public sources can supply texture and angles
that formal studies may not ask about.

Begin with the audience boundary and a few open research questions. Follow
useful leads as they emerge instead of forcing every audience through a standard
topic checklist. Vary searches and source types, look for materially different
contexts and counterexamples, and keep exploring after the first plausible
story has formed. Stop when additional searching mainly repeats the range
already represented or the task's practical limits have been reached.

## Retain useful formal detail

When a study contributes a concrete result, keep the population, sample size,
setting, comparison, percentage or count, and limitation that materially affect
how that result should be understood. Put that detail beside the finding in the
dossier body rather than expanding the source register.

Check the original source before using an exact figure, quotation, or unusually
consequential claim. Do not otherwise impose a source-checking quota, screening
log, confidence framework, or study-by-study appraisal. Do not calculate a
combined percentage from differently defined populations or imply causation
from an association.

## Learn from public experience

Use only publicly accessible material. Read across independent discussions and
formats where possible. Treat a single account as an example, not a recurring
pattern, and treat recurrence within reviewed sources as a feature of that
source set rather than a population rate. Preserve positive, negative, mixed,
uncertain, and contradictory accounts when they are present.

Paraphrase sensitive material. Omit usernames, profile links, distinctive
wording, and unusual combinations of detail that could make a person easy to
trace. Do not infer a person's diagnosis, identity, motivation, or clinical
history beyond what the source says.

## Synthesize for audience understanding

Combine two complementary forms of writing:

- source-shaped reporting that preserves concrete situations, language,
  numbers, and differences; and
- cross-source synthesis that describes recurring experiences, expressed
  feelings, tensions, variation, and disagreements across the material.

Use descriptive headings that arise from the evidence. Do not turn findings
into an average persona, a universal inner life, or a fixed list of audience
requirements. Keep communication, copy, design, positioning, and service
recommendations for downstream work.

## Record sources lightly

Use stable IDs:

- `F01`, `F02`, and so on for formal or authoritative evidence;
- `W01`, `W02`, and so on for public first-person material; and
- `P01`, `P02`, and so on for authorised owner or practice evidence.

Use one source table with only `ID`, `Source`, and `Description`. Link the source
where possible. Make the description just detailed enough to recognise what the
source is and why it appears in the dossier. Cite the IDs beside material
findings in the body.
