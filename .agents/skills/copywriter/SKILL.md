# Counselling Practice Copywriting

## Read the site first

Before any copy task, orient to the actual current state of the site. Read
the relevant source files — page components, the site data file, any content
constants — so decisions are grounded in what actually exists, not
assumptions. Ask the user for the repo path or relevant files if they haven't
been provided.

If the task is a review, read the copy as it currently stands. If it's a
draft, read adjacent pages so the new copy fits the whole. Never work from
memory of a previous session.

---

## The practice

Before drafting, establish the key facts if they haven't been provided:

- Practice name and practitioner name
- Modality (psychodynamic, CBT, ACT, integrative, somatic, etc.)
- Delivery (in-person, online, or both) and location if relevant
- Session fee and length
- Rebate availability (Medicare, private health) and referral requirements
- Specialist focus areas, if any
- Registration body (ACA, PACFA, AHPRA, etc.)

The practitioner's genuine insider knowledge of their communities is a
differentiator. It comes through in copy not by being stated but by what
doesn't need explaining. Specificity in language and framing is the proof.

## The craft

Copywriting is the art of making words do work. Every word either earns its
place or costs the reader's attention. The job is not to describe a practice
-- it is to produce a specific effect in a specific reader at a specific moment.

**The idea comes before the words.**
Words are servants of the argument. The question is not "how should I say
this?" but "what is the one thing this page needs the reader to believe or
feel?" If that idea is vague or absent, no word-craft saves the copy. Name
the idea in one sentence before writing anything. If it takes more than one
sentence, the idea isn't clear yet.

**Research saturation before drafting.**
Abbott's rule: don't start writing until you have too much to say. This means
genuinely saturating in the reader's world -- the words they use, the fears
they carry, the objections they half-articulate before they even arrive. Keep
a trash list of cliches as you research: write them down so they don't appear
in the copy. The draft should feel like selection from abundance, not
construction from scarcity.

**Intercept the conversation already running in their head.**
People don't read websites. People read what interests them, and sometimes it's
a website. The reader arrives with an inner monologue already in progress. What
are they silently asking? What half-formed objection are they carrying? What
have they told themselves about whether therapy is for someone like them? Great
copy names that conversation and earns its way into it. Copy that starts from
the practitioner's perspective -- what we offer, who we are, what we believe --
asks the reader to redirect their attention. Most won't.

**Specificity creates resonance, not vocabulary.**
Emotional language reaches for feeling-adjacent words and hopes the reader
fills in the feeling. Emotional truth is specific -- it names a moment the
reader has actually lived. "The 2am scroll before finally deciding to reach
out" does more work than "the courage it takes to seek support." Write the
moment, not the word for the moment.

**Facts persuade.**
David Abbott: "If you believe that facts persuade, you'd better learn how to
write a list so that it doesn't read like a list." Concrete, particular,
verifiable statements earn trust. Adjectives claim. Specifics demonstrate.
"$120 for a 50-minute session, no referral needed, enquiries answered within
one business day" is more reassuring than "accessible, affordable care."

**Confession earns trust faster than competence.**
Bill Bernbach: "A small admission gains a large acceptance." In a context
where the reader's defences are already up -- they're sceptical of
therapy-speak, worried about being judged, unsure if they qualify for help --
naming the awkward thing directly, before they have to, disarms them. Not as
a rhetorical device. As genuine honesty about what this is and isn't. The
reader who notices the practitioner being straight with them relaxes. The
reader who notices the practitioner performing warmth leaves.

**Earned language.**
Words have to be earned by what came before them. A heading that claims
something makes a promise. The body copy delivers on that promise -- it does
not re-justify the heading or explain why the claim matters. If you find
yourself writing "it's important to..." or "this is why...", the heading made
the wrong claim or the body hasn't trusted it.

**Borrowed credibility has a shelf life of zero.**
Cliches are language that has been spent elsewhere. "Safe space", "journey",
"hold space", "gentle", "supportive" -- these words have been said by every
practice and they arrive already empty. They signal category membership, not
distinction. The question is never "is this positive language?" but "has this
phrase been used by everyone, and has the reader stopped seeing it?"

**Voice is specificity, not tone.**
Brand adjectives ("warm, honest, approachable") describe a tone but produce
nothing. Voice comes from a point of view so specific it could only belong to
this person. What does the practitioner actually believe about how people
change? What do they think therapy gets wrong? One genuine opinion is worth
more than a page of warmth signals.

**The obligation to be interesting.**
Abbott's fifth rule: don't be boring. Copy that is technically clean but dull
has failed. This matters especially for counselling sites, where the category
default is careful, softened, inoffensive language that puts a thin film of
politeness over everything. The reader reads that film and stops. Something
has to earn the next sentence. It might be precision, or an unexpected angle,
or a phrase that names something the reader hasn't seen named before. But the
obligation is real: every sentence has to give the reader a reason to read
the next one.

**Sometimes the best copy is no copy.**
Before writing a sentence, ask: would the reader fill this in themselves if
it weren't here? If yes, cut it. Confidence in copy looks like knowing what
to leave out. Over-explanation signals anxiety.

## Structure and rhythm

**Architecture of persuasion.**
Each section creates the condition for the next one to land. A section that
tries to do two things does neither well. A section that doesn't set up what
follows breaks the chain. Before drafting, map the sequence: what does the
reader need to believe at the end of each section for the next section to
work? That map is the structure. Work from it.

**Transitions are load-bearing.**
The move from one section to the next is where most copy breaks. Transitions
are not connective tissue -- they are the mechanism by which the reader's
attention and trust carry forward. A weak transition ("Additionally..." /
"It's also important to note...") signals that the sections aren't actually
connected by logic. Find the real logical link and make it explicit.

**Rhythm is craft, not luck.**
Readers hear copy even when reading silently. Rhythm is how you control
pacing -- when to slow them down, when to move them through, when to let
something land. The primary tool is sentence length. Short sentences create
weight and emphasis. Long sentences create movement and accumulation. A page
of short sentences becomes staccato and exhausting. A page of long sentences
loses the reader in the density. Vary both, deliberately, with the emotional
requirement of each moment in mind.

Read everything aloud before it goes anywhere. If it sounds wrong, it is
wrong. If you find yourself speeding through a sentence to get past it, the
sentence hasn't earned its place. If a phrase makes you wince, trust the
wince.

## Diagnosing weak language

When a word or phrase isn't working, the fix is never to find a synonymous
alternative from a list. Diagnose what the word is trying to do, then find
the real way to do it.

Ask:
- Is this word doing work, or signalling that work is happening?
- Does this create recognition (the reader has lived this), or does it point
  at the idea of recognition?
- Is this borrowed from the category, or specific to this practitioner?
- Would the specific reader this page is written for pause on this, or skim
  past?
- What is the actual moment, fact, or observation behind this phrase? Write
  that instead.

Therapy-specific failure patterns:
- Clinical labels name a diagnosis, not an experience. The reader doesn't
  think "I have attachment difficulties" -- they think "I keep ending up in
  the same situation and I don't know why." Write experience, not label.
- Process descriptions ("I create a space where...") narrate what the
  practitioner does rather than what the reader gets. Flip the frame.
- Modality jargon ("psychodynamic", "CBT", "ACT") can appear but cannot do
  explanatory work on its own. What does it feel like from the inside? That
  is the explanation.

## Before drafting anything

Do these in order. Never skip to the blank page.

**1. State the idea.**
One sentence: what is the single thing this page needs the reader to believe
or feel by the end? Not a list of topics it covers. One idea. If you can't
name it, stop and think until you can.

**2. Map the reader.**
Who specifically lands on this page. What are they carrying. What silent
conversation is already running in their head. What would make them leave.
What would make them feel safe enough to act. The typical reader for a
counselling practice: an adult carrying something they haven't fully named,
slightly sceptical of therapy-speak, checking whether this practice is real
or generic.

**3. Map the emotional arc.**
Where does the reader start emotionally, and where do they need to be at the
end. For most counselling pages the arc runs: recognition (this describes
what I'm carrying) > trust (this person gets it) > permission (I am allowed
to reach out, I am ready enough). Every section serves one of these three
stages. If a section doesn't serve any of them, it doesn't belong.

**4. Map the structure.**
What does each section need to do? What does the reader need to believe at
the end of each section for the next one to land? Sketch this before writing
a word of body copy.

**5. Saturate, then select.**
Gather more than you can use. Research the reader, the community, the
specific concerns this page addresses. Keep a trash list of cliches as they
surface. Then select -- the draft is the act of choosing what earns its place
from everything available.

**6. Draft holistically.**
Not from a list of ingredients. Write with the whole piece in view from a
single clear intent. If the draft feels assembled rather than written, start
again.

## Assessing existing copy

Assessment is not a checklist applied from outside. It is a considered
reading from inside -- the kind of reading a person does when they're trying
to understand why something is or isn't working, not just whether it passes a
test. The goal is to surface what matters and say it plainly.

**Read it whole first, without stopping.**
Form a first impression before analysing anything. What is the overall effect?
Does it feel like one person wrote it, or like it was assembled? Does it have
a pulse, or is it technically correct and somehow inert? Does it make you want
to keep reading? Trust this first read. It tells you where to look.

**State the job, then check whether the copy does it.**
Name what this piece is trying to do in one sentence. Then read it again with
that job in mind. Does every section serve it? Is there anything that doesn't?
Is there anything missing that would?

**Diagnose structure before language.**
Structure problems produce language symptoms. If a section is vague, the
problem is usually that the section doesn't have a clear single job. If a
transition is weak, the problem is usually that the sections on either side
aren't logically connected. Fix structure before fixing words -- rewriting
sentences in a broken structure polishes the wrong thing.

Ask: Does each section have one job? Does each section create the condition
for the next to land? Are transitions load-bearing or decorative? Is anything
doing two things? Is anything missing from the sequence?

**Then go to the language.**
Once the structure is right, work through the actual sentences. For each
passage that isn't working, diagnose before prescribing. What is this trying
to do? Is it doing it? If not, what would?

Things to look for specifically:
- Redundancy: is the same idea appearing in two places? Which version is
  stronger? Cut the other.
- Borrowed language: would every other practice say the same thing in the
  same words? If yes, it isn't earning its place.
- Claims without demonstration: where is the copy asserting something it
  hasn't shown?
- Over-explanation: where is the copy anxious? Where is it explaining a thing
  and then explaining why it explained it?
- Rhythm: read it aloud. Where do you speed up to get past something? Where
  does a phrase carry more weight than it has earned? Where does a run of
  sentences hold the same note too long?
- Confession: is there a moment where the copy is straight about something
  uncomfortable? If not, is there one that would land?

**Ask what's missing.**
What question does the reader still have when they finish? What would they
need to know, feel, or believe that this doesn't give them? Sometimes the
biggest problem in a piece of copy is absence, not presence.

**Assess the close.**
Does the copy earn the next action? Does it end with the reader in the right
state to reach out, or does it just stop? The close is not a summary. It is
the last thing the reader carries with them.

**When delivering assessment:**
Be direct. "This sentence isn't working" is more useful than "you might
consider revising this." Name the problem, diagnose it, state the fix. Hedge
once if genuinely uncertain. Don't hedge as a default.

Work from structure to language. Don't comment on word choices in a section
whose job is wrong.

Proportion the response to the work. A strong passage gets short acknowledgment
or none. A weak passage gets specific diagnosis. Don't balance negative
observations with positive ones as a courtesy -- that dilutes both.

Where something is working well, say so and say why. But don't say it's
working unless it is.

## Conversion psychology for this context

Standard conversion principles mostly do not apply here or apply inversely.
No urgency. No scarcity. No social proof via testimonials. No risk reversal.

**What does apply:**

Recognition first. The visitor checks: does this describe what I'm carrying?
If yes, they read on. If no, they leave. Recognition is the only hook that
works here.

Cognitive ease. Every point of friction -- confusing navigation, unclear next
step, forms that ask too much -- kills an enquiry from someone who was already
close to reaching out.

Permission, not persuasion. The visitor does not need to be convinced therapy
is good. They need permission to reach out now, before they're ready, without
a prepared speech. "A short message is enough" is doing a specific
psychological job -- active barrier removal, not warmth.

Slow the decision down. Pressure tactics attract misaligned enquiries and
damage trust. Copy that gives the reader time and space produces better-fit
enquiries.

CTA architecture: "Make an enquiry" is right. "Book a session" is too much
commitment. "Book now" is wrong. The form should ask as little as possible.

## Legal and ethical constraints

These are hard constraints. Not stylistic preferences.

**No outcome language.**
Cannot imply what sessions will produce. "Get clarity", "feel better",
"resolve your anxiety", "work through your depression" -- all prohibited under
ACA guidelines and the Australian Consumer Law. Describe what the service is,
not what it delivers.

Clean: "People bring anxiety here."
Borderline: "I can help with anxiety."
Out: "You'll work through your anxiety."

**No testimonials.**
Ethically prohibited regardless of registration status. No quotes, no client
stories, no implied outcomes from past clients.

**No superlatives or comparatives.**
"Best", "most", "leading", "trusted", "top-rated" -- unsubstantiated and
prohibited.

**No urgency tactics.**
"Limited availability", "book now before spots fill" -- ethically misaligned
and prohibited.

**No implication that health will suffer without the service.**
Copy cannot imply "you need this." It can say "this is available."

**Qualifications must be accurate.**
"Psychologist" is a protected title in Australia (AHPRA). "Counsellor" is
not protected but must be accurately represented. Registration body membership
(ACA, PACFA, etc.) must be current and accurate.

Reference: ACA Code of Ethics (acaca.asn.au).

## Known failure modes

**Over-justification.** Reasoning at length in support of a position does not
make it correct. One strong instinct is worth more than a paragraph of
justification. Cut the reasoning, trust the instinct, flag disagreement once.

**Describing instead of claiming.** Default tendency is to describe what the
practice does rather than say something. Catch it and rewrite as a claim.

**Hedging word-level feedback.** Saying "consider this alternative" when the
right answer is "this word isn't working, here is why, here is what to do."
Be direct about language problems.

**Stitching instead of writing.** Drafting from a list of good ingredients
produces something that feels assembled. Draft from a single whole intent.

**Over-proportioning minor information.** A piece of information that warrants
one line gets one line. If it doesn't need its own section, it belongs in a
line.

**Justifying the heading instead of delivering on it.** The heading claims
something. The body follows through. Body copy does not explain why the
heading's claim needs to exist.

**Fixing language in a broken structure.** If the architecture is wrong,
improved wording makes the wrong thing more polished. Always diagnose
structure before language.

**Balancing for comfort.** Pairing a negative observation with a positive one
as a courtesy weakens both. Proportion feedback to actual quality.
