# Angle prompts

Use these prompts to find a useful direction before drafting substantial copy.
They are options, not a checklist or page template.

## Start with the visitor's situation

- What may have happened recently that made this page relevant now?
- What is the visitor trying to decide, not merely learn?
- What might they need to confirm before continuing?
- What language are they likely to use when searching or asking someone about
  this?
- What would make the page feel irrelevant, generic, patronising, or unsafe?

Do not invent an intimate inner monologue. Keep any hypothesis proportionate
to available evidence and mark it as a hypothesis.

## Consider distinct frames

### Decision and fit

Help the visitor judge whether the service, practitioner, format, or boundary
fits what they are looking for.

Ask:

- What information would make that decision easier?
- What would a good-fit or poor-fit distinction clarify?
- What should remain the visitor's decision rather than become persuasion?

### Recognition

Name a situation, pattern, or tension the intended visitor may recognise.

Ask:

- Which concrete experience is relevant to this page?
- Is it supported by the known audience, or merely plausible-sounding?
- Can it be described without diagnosing, dramatising, or claiming universality?

### Practical clarity

Reduce uncertainty with concrete information.

Ask:

- What happens, where, when, for how long, at what cost, and with what next
  step?
- Which practical fact carries more trust than an adjective would?
- Which detail needs owner confirmation before publication?

### Misconception or misplaced assumption

Address an assumption that may prevent the visitor from understanding the
service or recognising themselves in it.

Ask:

- What might people reasonably misunderstand here?
- Is correcting it central enough to lead the section?
- Can the correction be made without arguing with or caricaturing the reader?

### Boundary or limitation

Use a real boundary to make the service more intelligible.

Ask:

- What does this service not assume, promise, provide, or decide for someone?
- What alternative or next step is useful at the boundary?
- Does stating the limitation improve understanding, or merely perform honesty?

### Specialist fluency

Demonstrate knowledge through the distinctions and language used.

Ask:

- Which terms can be used without translating the visitor's life into generic
  language?
- Which same-looking situations need to be distinguished?
- Where should familiarity be paired with curiosity about individual meaning?

### Practitioner point of view

Use a genuine professional judgement or observation when it helps explain the
work.

Ask:

- What does the practitioner actually notice, ask, or take seriously?
- What common simplification do they avoid?
- Can the point be supported without turning disagreement into a provocative
  brand position?

### Experience of the process

Explain the service from the prospective client's side rather than listing a
modality or practitioner action.

Ask:

- What might a session involve in observable terms?
- What choices, questions, pace, or boundaries might the client encounter?
- What must remain uncertain because therapy is individual rather than a fixed
  sequence?

### Lowering the threshold

Remove unnecessary effort from the next step.

Ask:

- What does the visitor not need to prepare, decide, disclose, or commit to yet?
- What is the smallest accurate next action?
- Does the wording provide useful permission without promising emotional ease?

## Compare before choosing

For each plausible angle, note:

- the central question it answers;
- the facts or evidence available to support it;
- the section or page structure it suggests;
- what it would leave out or push into the background;
- the main risk, such as overclaiming, generic reassurance, excess explanation,
  or search-language distortion.

Reject options that differ only in tone or phrasing. Choose or present angles
that would genuinely change what the copy says and how the page is organised.

Once a direction is chosen, use only the supporting ideas it needs. Do not
blend the entire exploration into the draft.
