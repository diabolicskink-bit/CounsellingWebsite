# Model-glue review

Run this pass before finalising any new page, substantial section, or
substantial rewrite. Also use it when a short passage is accurate but still
feels generated, over-explained, or interchangeable.

Inspect rhetorical mechanisms and clusters, not isolated vocabulary. Any
phrase below may be the clearest choice once; the problem is automatic use,
vague use, or a repeated job that the direct proposition could perform better.

| Mechanism | Common signal | What may be happening | Test and repair |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Significance announcement | `that matters`, `what matters`, `important because`, `meaningful because` | The prose tells the reader to value a point before offering a broad consequence. | Delete the announcement and make the consequence the main clause. Keep it only when the act of ranking priorities is the point. |
| Interpretive bridge | `this means`, `in practice`, `what this looks like`, `in other words`, `at its core` | The model completes a statement with a second, smoother explanation. | Start with the meaning itself or merge the sentences. Keep the bridge when it marks a necessary change of register or corrects a real misunderstanding. |
| Counselling abstraction | `pattern`, `dynamic`, `experience`, `process`, `space`, `support`, `context`, `part`, or `thing` without a clear referent | A broad noun creates psychological or professional texture while leaving the proposition thin. | Name what repeats, happens, changes, conflicts, or is chosen. Keep the abstract term when the reader and speaker use it precisely and its referent is already clear. |
| Abstract cause | An abstract subject `shapes`, `affects`, `influences`, or `impacts` another abstraction | The sentence sounds consequential without showing the relationship. | Ask what acts on what, and how. State the observable relationship or remove the causal claim. |
| Modal accumulation | Several uses of `may`, `might`, `can`, `could`, `often`, or `sometimes` in a short passage | Caution has become a general surface style rather than a precise expression of uncertainty. | Classify each statement as fact, possibility, hypothesis, invitation, or unknown. Keep only the qualification its status requires. |
| Automatic benefit conversion | `which can help`, `allowing you to`, `so you can`, `making it easier to` after each fact or feature | Every detail is being translated into a positive client outcome. | Delete the benefit tail. Restore it only when it adds a decision-changing consequence that can be stated proportionately. |
| Vague demonstrative chain | Consecutive sentences led by `this`, `that`, `it`, `these`, or `those` | Pronouns and smooth transitions conceal repetition or an unclear subject. | Name the referent, combine the sentences, or cut the second sentence. |
| Safe explanatory choreography | Statement → significance bridge → counselling interpretation → possible benefit | A fluent four-part sequence is completing itself regardless of the amount of thought available. | Choose the strongest proposition and the one development it actually needs. Break the sequence rather than merely varying its wording. |

Use these transformations during the pass:

1. Strike the introductory bridge and read the remaining clause as a direct
   sentence.
2. Circle each broad noun and name its referent. If no referent is available,
   question the proposition rather than finding a synonym.
3. Underline modal verbs and assign each statement a status. Do not hedge a
   fact, present a possibility as a fact, or stack caution without purpose.
4. Remove each client-benefit ending. Restore only the ones that change
   understanding or choice.
5. Trace every `this`, `that`, `it`, `these`, or `those` to an unambiguous noun.
6. Compare the first few words and logical movement of consecutive sentences.
   Repair repeated reasoning, not merely repeated openings.

For example, `That distinction matters because it changes the questions asked`
usually becomes `That distinction changes the questions asked`. If `these
patterns can shape relationships` cannot be rewritten to name what repeats and
what changes, the idea is not yet specific enough to publish. These are editing
tests, not preferred public wording.

Do not replace flagged phrases mechanically, introduce random variety, or make
the copy conspicuously blunt. Repair the thin thought, unnecessary bridge,
unclear status, or repeated reasoning that produced the signal.
