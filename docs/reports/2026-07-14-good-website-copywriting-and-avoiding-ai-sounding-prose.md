# Good Website Copywriting in 2026: Clarity, Persuasion, Voice, and Avoiding Generic AI Prose

**Research date:** 14 July 2026  
**Scope:** Independent research into strong public-facing website copy, including content strategy, persuasive writing, voice, trust, accessibility, inclusion, search, professional-service and health-adjacent communication, weak copy patterns, and the current features associated with generic AI prose.  
**Relationship to this repository:** This is a companion research report to [Good Website Design in 2026](2026-07-14-good-website-design-and-anti-ai-tropes-research.md). It was researched independently and does not use the repository's copywriter skill or its public-copy rules as a method or source. It is supporting evidence, not project direction or approved public wording.  
**Intended use:** A reference for briefing, drafting, critique, editing, and content testing. It is not a universal voice guide, a legal opinion, a promise that one formula will improve conversion, or a detector of AI authorship.

## Executive summary

Good website copy helps a particular reader understand something, decide something, or do something. It earns attention by being relevant. It earns trust through accurate detail, proportionate claims, visible limits, and a next step that matches the reader's situation.

The strongest conclusions from the research are:

1. **Start with the reader's decision, not the organisation's preferred description of itself.** The useful question is not “What do we want to say?” but “What does this person need to know or resolve here?”
2. **Plain language and distinctive voice are compatible.** Plain language removes avoidable decoding work. Voice comes from judgement, selection, cadence, point of view, and specific knowledge, not from harder words.
3. **Specificity does several jobs at once.** It explains the offer, shows familiarity with the subject, makes claims testable, reduces uncertainty, and makes prose harder to transfer unchanged to a competitor.
4. **A claim needs a basis.** Australian Competition and Consumer Commission guidance says claims about services should be accurate, truthful, based on reasonable grounds, and capable of proof. Fine print cannot repair a misleading overall impression.
5. **Professional-service copy should support a choice rather than manufacture certainty.** Benefits matter, but promised feelings and outcomes can become misleading. Explain what is offered, what the process involves, who it may be relevant to, what is uncertain, and what the next contact permits the reader to find out.
6. **Warmth is usually carried by conduct and detail.** A clear answer, a candid limitation, an ordinary sentence, or an invitation that preserves choice is more credible than announcing that a service is “safe”, “caring”, “welcoming”, or “non-judgemental”.
7. **Web copy needs an information hierarchy.** Descriptive titles, frontloaded paragraphs, meaningful headings, short coherent sections, and specific links support scanning without reducing every thought to a fragment or card.
8. **Accessibility is part of the writing.** Common words, explained specialist terms, literal instructions, meaningful link text, useful headings, clear errors, and inclusive assumptions help many readers, including people using assistive technology or reading under stress.
9. **Search clarity and human clarity largely point in the same direction.** Google recommends original, useful, people-first content and warns against keyword stuffing, scaled low-value pages, and extensive automation without added value. A precise service name in a useful heading is stronger than a paragraph of keyword variants.
10. **Copywriting formulas are prompts, not laws.** AIDA, PAS, feature-benefit tables, and similar devices can expose missing information. They should not force every reader through a dramatic problem-agitation arc or assume a fixed route from attention to action.
11. **“AI-sounding” describes a convergence pattern, not provenance.** Current studies identify clusters such as overrepresented vocabulary, predictable sentence structures, excessive formality, generic introductions, upbeat summary conclusions, weak specificity, and safe treatment of the subject. No single word, punctuation mark, or grammatical feature proves AI authorship.
12. **The strongest anti-generic test is transferability.** If changing the organisation name and a few nouns makes the copy suitable for another business, the writing probably contains category language rather than specific knowledge.
13. **Do not simulate humanity with mistakes.** Random slang, contractions, typos, sentence fragments, or em dashes do not create authorship. The better remedy is to preserve source material, make real choices, include verified detail, edit locally, and allow sentences to end when their work is done.
14. **Test comprehension and decision support, not elegance alone.** Ask representative readers what the page offers, who it is for, what happens next, what remains unclear, and where they would look for a practical answer. Readability scores can flag surface complexity but cannot measure understanding, relevance, cohesion, or trust.

The practical standard proposed in this report is:

> Good website copy gives the reader the right information at the point of decision, in language they can understand, with enough specificity and candour to judge the offer for themselves.

## 1. Research method and confidence

This report separates durable guidance from current observations about generative writing.

### Layer A: standards, regulators, and operational guidance

These sources support the most dependable requirements and checks:

- the W3C Web Accessibility Initiative on headings, link text, instructions, text alternatives, and clear content;
- the Australian Government Style Manual on plain and inclusive language;
- the Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care on written health information and consumer testing;
- the ACCC on service claims and misleading impressions;
- Google Search Central on people-first content, generative AI, spam, and descriptive search content;
- GOV.UK and other government content-design manuals on user needs, structure, interface copy, and testing.

These organisations write for different contexts. Government instructions are not a ready-made brand voice, and health-information guidance is not a substitute for clinical or legal advice. Their methods are useful because they turn clarity, access, accuracy, and task success into observable questions.

### Layer B: peer-reviewed and empirical research

Research used here includes:

- field and experimental studies of concrete language in service communication;
- a recent meta-analysis of freedom-threatening language and psychological reactance;
- usability research on concise, scannable, objective web writing;
- corpus and annotation studies of language associated with large language models;
- experiments on AI-assisted creativity and homogenisation;
- evaluations of human and automated AI-text detection.

The domains matter. Findings from academic abstracts, news-style articles, customer-service exchanges, short stories, or social advertisements cannot be transferred wholesale to counselling-service pages. This report uses those findings to identify risks and questions, not universal word bans.

### Layer C: current practice and cultural observation

Public content guides from Mailchimp, Microsoft, Shopify, and similar organisations show how mature teams operationalise voice, tone, consistency, and microcopy. They are practitioner references, not independent proof that one house style works for every audience.

Lists of “AI words” and social-media claims about punctuation are treated as cultural signals only when better evidence is unavailable. They should never be used to accuse an author or reject otherwise good wording.

### Confidence summary

| Finding | Confidence | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reader needs, clear hierarchy, plain language, meaningful headings and links improve access and task success | High | Convergent standards, operational guidance, and long-running usability evidence |
| Concrete, relevant detail can strengthen perceived listening and service satisfaction | Moderate to high | Field data plus controlled experiments, though not from counselling websites |
| Forceful, freedom-threatening wording can create reactance | High for the general effect; context-dependent in size | Recent meta-analysis with heterogeneous effects |
| Accurate, supportable claims and a non-misleading overall impression are necessary | High | Current Australian regulator guidance |

| Certain words and structures are overrepresented in some LLM outputs | Moderate | Several corpus and annotation studies, mostly in academic and non-fiction genres |
| A reader can reliably determine AI authorship from style alone | Low as a general claim | Performance varies sharply by reader experience, genre, model, prompt, and study |
| Removing a list of “AI words” makes copy good or human | Very low | Quality problems operate at content, structure, evidence, and judgement levels |

## 2. What good copywriting should mean

“Copywriting” is often reduced to words that sell. That definition is too narrow for a public website, especially a professional or health-adjacent service.

Website copy may need to:

- identify the service and the person or organisation providing it;
- help a reader recognise whether the page is relevant;
- explain an approach, process, distinction, or practical arrangement;
- provide evidence and establish limits;
- reduce uncertainty without pretending uncertainty can be removed;
- support comparison;
- help someone complete a form or recover from an error;
- make a next step clear;
- make the page findable through ordinary search language.

Persuasion remains legitimate. A practice can explain why its service may be worth considering and invite contact. The quality test is whether persuasion is achieved through relevant information and a fair presentation of the choice, or through pressure, omission, inflated certainty, and emotional manipulation.

| Quality | The copy succeeds when… | Common failure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Relevant | It answers a real question or supports a real decision. | It begins with the organisation's aspirations, history, or adjectives. |
| Clear | The reader can identify the subject, meaning, and next step. | The central offer is hidden behind a slogan or abstraction. |
| Specific | It uses concrete facts, examples, distinctions, and boundaries. | Broad words such as “holistic”, “tailored”, and “transformative” carry the argument. |
| Credible | Claims are proportionate, supportable, and consistent with the service. | The prose announces trust, safety, expertise, or outcomes without showing a basis. |
| Useful | Practical information appears where it affects a decision. | The copy creates atmosphere while leaving process, cost, limits, or contact unclear. |
| Accessible | Language, structure, links, instructions, and errors work for a wide range of readers. | Shortness or visual neatness is mistaken for comprehension. |
| Inclusive | The copy avoids unnecessary assumptions and uses community-relevant language with care. | Generic welcome language sits on top of stereotyped examples or default assumptions. |
| Persuasive | The reader can see why the offer may matter and what evidence supports it. | The copy agitates fear, invents urgency, or overstates the likely result. |
| Distinctive | The selection of detail and way of explaining it belong to this provider. | A competitor can use the same page after changing a few nouns. |
| Search-clear | People and search systems can identify the page's topic naturally. | Keywords are repeated, locations are stacked, or several thin pages say the same thing. |
| Maintainable | Facts, decisions, ownership, and review dates can be checked. | Copy slowly contradicts itself across pages or keeps outdated promises. |

Good copy can be quiet, playful, formal, spare, technical, intimate, or highly editorial. No surface style guarantees quality. The underlying standard is resolved intent supported by accurate material.

## 3. Four distinctions that prevent bad advice

### 3.1 Plain is not bland

Plain language uses words and structures the intended reader can understand without avoidable effort. It can still contain specialist vocabulary, humour, unusual observation, strong rhythm, and personality.

The [Australian Government Style Manual](https://www.stylemanual.gov.au/writing-and-designing-content/clear-language-and-writing-style/plain-language-and-word-choice) recommends ordinary words, explained specialist terms, expanded acronyms, and language people actually use. The [Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care](https://www.safetyandquality.gov.au/sites/default/files/resources/attachments/fact-sheet-4-health-literacy-writing-health-information-for-consumers.pdf) makes an important distinction: plain language is understandable on first reading, but it is not necessarily casual or patronising.

Blandness comes from a lack of selection and judgement. “We provide tailored solutions for your unique journey” is easy to decode, yet it says almost nothing.

### 3.2 Concise is not short at any cost

Concise copy contains what the reader needs and little that competes with it. Sometimes the concise answer is a sentence. Sometimes a high-consideration service requires several paragraphs, an example, a limitation, and practical detail.

Cut duplicated reassurance, throat-clearing, filler transitions, empty adjectives, and conclusions that merely repeat the page. Do not cut the fact that changes a decision.

### 3.3 Persuasive is not coercive

Persuasion explains relevance, value, evidence, and a reasonable next step. Coercion threatens autonomy, hides alternatives, amplifies fear, or makes inaction seem shameful.

A recent [meta-analysis of message effects on psychological reactance](https://academic.oup.com/hcr/article/52/1/38/8178818) found that high freedom-threatening language produced more anger and negative thoughts than lower-threat language, while those reactions were negatively associated with persuasion outcomes. Effects varied by context, so the finding is not “never use an imperative”. A button labelled “Send enquiry” is clear. A paragraph insisting that a distressed reader “must act now before things get worse” carries a different kind of pressure.

### 3.4 AI-like is not the same as AI-authored

A polished human writer can use language associated with AI. A generated draft can be heavily edited into precise, original prose. Templates, corporate review, SEO habits, educational conventions, and risk-averse committees all produced generic writing before current language models existed.

Use “AI-sounding” as shorthand for a cluster of generic, predictable decisions. Do not use it as an authorship verdict.

## 4. Fourteen durable principles

### 4.1 Give every page and section a job

Before drafting, complete this sentence:

> This page helps [reader in a real situation] understand or decide [specific thing], so they can [appropriate next step or informed non-action].

Then identify:

- what may have brought the reader here;
- what they are comparing or uncertain about;
- what information changes the decision;
- what claims need proof;
- what the organisation cannot responsibly promise;
- what action is useful now, if any.

The GOV.UK content-design tradition grounds content in evidenced user needs rather than stakeholder preference. The [Office for National Statistics user-needs guidance](https://service-manual.ons.gov.uk/content/writing-for-users/user-needs) also warns against defining the need as a predetermined solution. “The user needs a long About page” is not a user need. “The reader needs enough information about the practitioner to judge whether to make contact” leaves room for a better content shape.

### 4.2 Earn attention with relevance

An opening should quickly establish what the page is about and why it may matter to the intended reader. It does not have to shout, pose a dramatic question, announce an emotional truth, or imitate an advertisement.

Useful openings often do one of the following:

- state the service or subject plainly;
- name a real decision or circumstance;
- offer a specific distinction;
- answer the most likely practical question;
- make a precise observation that the rest of the page can support.

“In today's fast-paced world, navigating life's challenges can feel overwhelming” delays the subject and could introduce thousands of unrelated sites. The sentence creates mood without earning it.

### 4.3 Shape the information before polishing sentences

Good line editing cannot rescue the wrong content order. Decide what needs to be known first, what belongs together, what requires evidence, and where a practical question naturally occurs.

For a high-consideration service, a useful sequence may be:

1. identify the service and page subject;
2. address the reader's main uncertainty;
3. explain how the service or approach works;
4. supply relevant proof, constraints, and practical detail;
5. offer the next appropriate step.

That is not a mandatory page template. A fees page, practitioner profile, inclusion page, article, and contact form have different jobs. Repeating one “hero, benefits, process, testimonials, FAQ, CTA” skeleton across all of them produces structural sameness even when every sentence is competent.

### 4.4 Support scanning and committed reading

Many readers scan to locate relevant material before deciding to read it. Others will read closely when the decision matters. Copy should support both behaviours.

The [W3C writing guidance](https://www.w3.org/WAI/tips/writing/) recommends informative page titles, meaningful headings, clear instructions, concise content, and link text that describes its destination. Government content guidance similarly recommends frontloading useful information and using descriptive headings.

Use:

- a page title that names the subject;
- an opening that carries useful information;
- headings that form a meaningful outline;
- paragraphs with a clear point;
- lists for genuinely parallel items or steps;
- descriptive links;
- emphasis only when it helps retrieval.

Avoid manufacturing scannability by turning each sentence into a fragment, bold label, card, or isolated one-line paragraph. Excessive fragmentation interrupts reasoning and has become a common generated-marketing rhythm.

### 4.5 Prefer plain language, but preserve exact language where it matters

Use familiar verbs and concrete nouns. Explain an unfamiliar term when the reader needs it. Keep the specialist term when it is accurate, useful, and part of the audience's language.

Plain-language editing should remove avoidable friction, not erase the vocabulary people use to understand themselves or find a relevant service. A technical or community-specific word can be more respectful and exact than a vague euphemism.

Useful checks:

- Could an ordinary verb replace a nominalisation such as “facilitate the development of”?
- Is an acronym used often enough to justify introducing it?
- Does an idiom create ambiguity for readers or translation tools?
- Does the sentence require the reader to remember too many nested ideas?
- Is the same thing given several synonyms for style, making it harder to follow?

### 4.6 Use concrete detail to show attention

Concrete language names perceptible actions, objects, times, conditions, or examples. Abstract language names broad qualities and concepts.

In a series of field studies and experiments, [Packard and Berger](https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article/47/5/787/5873524) found that customers were more satisfied, and sometimes spent more, after service employees used relevant concrete language. Experimental evidence suggested that concrete wording made employees seem more attentive and better able to understand the customer's needs. The effect weakened when the response itself was irrelevant, which is a useful warning: detail only helps when it belongs.

For website copy, compare:

- abstract: “I offer a personalised, holistic approach.”
- more concrete: “The first conversation covers what has brought you to counselling, what you want help with, and any questions you have about how I work.”

The second sentence is hypothetical rather than proposed copy for this site. Its advantage is structural: actions replace self-awarded qualities.

### 4.7 Connect service facts to consequences without promising outcomes

The familiar instruction to “sell benefits, not features” is incomplete. Readers of a professional-service site need both.

A fact without a consequence may be meaningless: “Sessions are 50 minutes.” A benefit without a fact can become fantasy: “Finally achieve the life you deserve.” Strong copy connects the two carefully: what is offered, what it allows, and what remains uncertain.

For high-consideration or health-adjacent services:

- name the arrangement or method;
- explain what a client will actually encounter;
- state why that may be useful;
- avoid predicting the reader's feelings, suitability, or result;
- provide limits and alternatives where they affect the decision.

### 4.8 Build trust through supportable claims and visible limits

The [ACCC's current guidance on false or misleading claims](https://www.accc.gov.au/consumers/advertising-and-promotions/false-or-misleading-claims) applies to websites and services. It says claims should be true, accurate, based on reasonable grounds, and capable of proof. The overall impression matters; a qualification cannot contradict the main message, and omitting an important fact can sometimes mislead.

Treat these as evidence prompts:

| Claim type | Ask before publishing |
| --- | --- |
| Credential | Is the title current, exact, and presented without implying a different registration or scope? |
| Experience | What period, setting, or work supports the statement? |
| Outcome | Is the result guaranteed, typical, possible, or only an aspiration? Is that distinction visible? |
| Suitability | Who decides, on what information, and when? |
| Safety or inclusion | What conduct, knowledge, process, and recourse support the claim? |
| Availability | Is the information current, and who updates it? |
| Price or “free” offer | Are conditions, duration, and exclusions clear in the main message? |
| Testimonial | Is it genuine, authorised, representative, and permissible in the relevant professional context? |

Candour can be persuasive. “This service is not the right option for…” may help the appropriate reader trust the rest of the page.

### 4.9 Let voice come from judgement

Voice is the recurring character of the writing. Tone is how that voice adjusts to the situation. A home-page introduction, form error, fee explanation, and sensitive service page should not all perform the same emotional register.

Mature practitioner guides make this distinction explicitly. [Mailchimp's public guide](https://styleguide.mailchimp.com/voice-and-tone/) keeps a recognisable voice while changing tone according to the reader's state and puts clarity ahead of entertainment. [Microsoft's writing guidance](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/apps/design/style/writing-style) similarly frames its voice as warm, relaxed, crisp, and clear. These are examples of operating systems for writing, not voices to copy.

A useful voice brief uses tensions and evidence:

- direct, without sounding abrupt;
- knowledgeable, without performing authority;
- warm, without assuming intimacy;
- candid, without becoming cold;
- distinctive in observation, restrained in decoration.

Then define what those tensions change in practice. A list of adjectives alone gives a writer or model too much room to return category defaults.

### 4.10 Vary cadence for meaning, not camouflage

Good prose has movement. Sentence length, clause shape, punctuation, and paragraph density should respond to the thought.

Current AI-text research has found predictable structures in some generated non-fiction: recurring contrast constructions, repeated lists of three, uniformly complex sentences, and highly regular paragraph forms. The remedy is not a mathematical quota of short and long sentences. It is to let each sentence do the amount of work the idea requires.

During editing:

- combine clipped sentences that are one thought;
- divide a sentence whose qualifications hide its point;
- remove a transition when the relationship is already clear;
- check whether consecutive paragraphs begin the same way;
- stop after the useful final fact instead of adding a moral or uplifting summary;
- read the draft aloud and notice where natural emphasis disagrees with the syntax.

An em dash, semicolon, contraction, fragment, or three-item list can be entirely appropriate. Repetition without purpose is the issue.

### 4.11 Create warmth without performing empathy

Service copy often tries to reassure a reader by describing their internal state:

> You have been carrying this alone for too long, and you are ready to reclaim the life you deserve.

That sentence assigns a history, feeling, readiness, and desired future to a person the writer has never met. It may feel accurate to some readers and alienating to others.

More credible warmth can come from:

- acknowledging that people arrive with different levels of certainty;
- making room for questions;
- explaining what a first contact involves;
- stating what the reader does not need to prepare;
- using ordinary language for a difficult subject;
- naming a limit without blame;
- allowing the reader to decide whether the service sounds relevant.

Avoid making “empathetic” a surface style. Accuracy about another person's experience is more important than emotional intensity.

### 4.12 Preserve autonomy in calls to action

A call to action should state the next useful action and, where necessary, what follows. It should not turn every page into a countdown to conversion.

Good CTA copy is:

- specific: “View fees” is clearer than “Discover more”;
- proportionate: “Ask a question” may suit an uncertain reader better than “Start your transformation”;
- accurate: the label predicts the destination or consequence;
- available where the decision arises;
- paired with essential context, such as response time or commitment, when confirmed and relevant.

Imperative grammar is not inherently coercive. Interface labels usually need direct verbs. Pressure comes from the surrounding message, loss framing, repetition, urgency, and suppression of alternatives.

### 4.13 Write accessibly and inclusively

The [W3C cognitive-accessibility guidance](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG2/supplemental/objectives/o3-clear-content/) recommends common words, short sentences, simple tense, short blocks, unambiguous content, literal language, and clear formatting. These measures are particularly important for some readers, but they also help people who are tired, distressed, distracted, reading in another language, or unfamiliar with the subject.

The [Australian Government inclusive-language guidance](https://www.stylemanual.gov.au/accessible-and-inclusive-content/inclusive-language) emphasises culturally appropriate, respectful language and attention to people's preferences. Inclusive writing is not achieved by adding a welcome statement while leaving default assumptions untouched.

Review assumptions about:

- gender, pronouns, titles, and bodies;
- partners, monogamy, marriage, or household structure;
- culture, faith, family, and support networks;
- disability and neurodivergence;
- literacy, device, privacy, money, and time;
- what “normal”, “healthy”, or “successful” is allowed to mean.

Use meaningful headings and link text, label form fields persistently, explain errors in words, and avoid instructions that depend only on visual position such as “use the box on the right”.

### 4.14 Make copy findable, original, and maintainable

Google's [people-first content guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/creating-helpful-content) asks whether content serves an intended audience, demonstrates first-hand knowledge or expertise, helps the reader achieve a goal, and adds original value. For topics that can affect health or well-being, Google says its systems give more weight to signals aligned with strong experience, expertise, authority, and trust.

Google's [SEO starter guide](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/seo-starter-guide) recommends unique, descriptive titles; readable, well-organised, original text; useful headings; current information; and descriptive links. Its [spam policies](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/essentials/spam-policies) identify keyword stuffing and scaled, low-value content as abuse. Its [generative AI guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/using-gen-ai-content) focuses on accuracy, quality, relevance, added value, and appropriate context rather than banning a production tool.

Practical implications:

- use the ordinary name of the service and subject where it helps identification;
- give each page a distinct job;
- write titles and metadata that accurately summarise that page;
- use specialist and location language only when factually and strategically appropriate;
- avoid paragraphs built from near-synonymous keyword variations;
- do not publish multiple thin pages that differ mainly by a place name or topic noun;
- assign ownership for facts that change;
- review fees, availability, credentials, process details, links, and claims on a schedule.

## 5. Persuasion without a universal formula

### 5.1 What classic formulas are good for

AIDA (attention, interest, desire, action), PAS (problem, agitation, solution), before-after-bridge, feature-advantage-benefit, and similar structures can be useful checklists. They may reveal that a draft has no clear opening, has failed to explain relevance, or ends without a next step.

They are weak as universal models of how people decide. Critical reviews of advertising hierarchy models have long questioned their assumed linear sequence and empirical support. A reader may begin with action (“I need a counsellor this week”), then compare evidence, price, and fit. Another may read several pages, leave, ask someone they trust, and return later. Copy should support the real decision rather than force a theatrical sequence.

### 5.2 A better planning model: question, answer, basis, next step

For each section, identify:

1. **Question:** What is the reader trying to find out here?
2. **Answer:** What is the clearest useful response?
3. **Basis:** What fact, example, distinction, or boundary supports it?
4. **Next step:** What should the reader read, decide, or do now?

Not every section needs all four in equal measure. The model keeps attention on information and proof rather than a mandatory emotional arc.

### 5.3 Ethical problem framing

Naming a problem helps readers recognise relevance. Agitation often goes further by intensifying fear, shame, loss, or urgency. That can be inappropriate where the audience may be distressed or vulnerable.

Before using a problem statement, ask:

- Is this experience supported by research or direct audience evidence?
- Have we made one possible experience sound universal?
- Does the wording clarify the problem or merely raise emotional temperature?
- Are we implying that the service is the only sensible answer?
- Does the next step preserve choice?

### 5.4 Proof has to match the claim

Different claims need different evidence:

- a process claim needs an accurate description of process;
- a credential claim needs a verifiable qualification or membership;
- an outcome claim needs appropriate evidence and careful scope;
- a “people like you choose us” claim needs valid, ethical audience evidence;
- a testimonial only establishes one person's reported experience, not a guaranteed result;
- a specialist-knowledge claim is often better demonstrated through precise distinctions and language than asserted.

### 5.5 Conversion is not the only success measure

More enquiries can be the wrong outcome if the copy creates confusion about service scope or attracts people for whom the service is unsuitable. Useful measures may include:

- comprehension of the offer;
- ability to find fees, process, or boundaries;
- quality and appropriateness of enquiries;
- form completion and error recovery;
- fewer repeated pre-enquiry questions;
- fewer avoidable mismatches;
- search visits that reach relevant pages;
- reader-reported confidence in making a decision, including a decision not to proceed.

## 6. A field guide to weak website copy

These patterns are diagnostic prompts, not automatic bans.

| Pattern | Typical manifestation | Why it fails | Better direction |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Organisation-first opening | “Founded on a passion for excellence, we…” | Delays the reason the reader came. | Lead with the service, subject, or decision; use history where it establishes something relevant. |
| The category-cloud hero | “Compassionate support for a brighter tomorrow.” | Names a mood rather than an offer. | Identify who provides what, for whom, and in what context. |
| Adjective credentials | “Expert, trusted, leading, personalised.” | The organisation awards itself qualities the reader cannot verify. | Provide credentials, experience, method, process, or examples. |
| Abstract benefit fog | “Unlock clarity, confidence, and lasting transformation.” | Predicts broad outcomes without a mechanism or boundary. | Explain what happens and what it may help the reader explore or decide. |
| Performed empathy | “We know exactly how you feel.” | Claims knowledge of an individual experience. | Show attention through accurate language, options, and useful detail. |
| Mind-reading | “You are exhausted from pretending everything is fine.” | Invents the reader's inner life and can create false intimacy. | Name situations conditionally or describe the service directly. |
| Jargon as authority | “A holistic, integrative, client-centred therapeutic paradigm.” | Labels replace an explanation. | Keep the accurate term, then explain what changes in the work. |
| Feature dump | A list of modalities, memberships, tools, and session types without interpretation. | The reader cannot tell which facts matter to their decision. | Connect each relevant fact to its practical meaning. |
| Benefit-only fantasy | A future-state montage with no service detail. | Removes the basis on which the reader could judge the claim. | Pair possible value with a concrete process and careful uncertainty. |
| Forced pain agitation | Repeated warnings about what happens if the reader does not act. | Raises threat and may exploit vulnerability. | State the concern accurately and offer a proportionate choice. |
| Universal reassurance | “There is nothing to worry about.” | Dismisses legitimate concern and may be false. | Explain the relevant process, safeguard, limit, or next step. |
| Defensive overexplaining | Long policy-like paragraphs answering objections no reader raised. | Increases cognitive load and can create new anxiety. | State the normal arrangement, relevant exception, and practical alternative. |
| Faux candour | “Let's be honest…” followed by a standard sales claim. | Performs directness without taking a real position or risk. | Say the candid thing itself. |
| Vague difference claim | “We do things differently.” | Requires the reader to accept an unsupported comparison. | Name the actual difference and its consequence. |
| Decorative metaphor | Journeys, chapters, tapestries, storms, light, space, and flourishing applied throughout. | Atmosphere replaces subject knowledge and can feel imposed. | Use metaphor only when it clarifies a real relationship and fits the speaker. |
| Copywriter cleverness | Puns, mystery headings, and surprising reversals on practical pages. | Makes retrieval depend on decoding the writer's performance. | Put character in a line that remains informative. |
| Generic CTA | “Learn more”, “Discover”, “Begin your journey”. | Does not predict the destination or commitment. | Name the action: view fees, read about the approach, ask a question, send an enquiry. |
| SEO paragraph | Repeated service, suburb, city, therapist, and counselling variants. | Sounds unnatural and may meet Google's definition of keyword stuffing. | Use the most useful terms in titles, openings, headings, links, and natural explanations. |
| FAQ landfill | Generic questions invented to hold keywords or repeat the page. | Adds volume without resolving new uncertainty. | Include consequential questions not answered better in the main flow. |
| Tidy final moral | “Ultimately, taking the first step is the beginning of a brighter future.” | Adds no information and pressures the page into uplift. | End with the last useful fact or a clear, proportionate action. |

## 7. What “AI-sounding” prose is and is not

### 7.1 The evidence is real but domain-limited

Several studies have found detectable patterns in particular kinds of generated text:

- A large analysis of biomedical abstracts found abrupt increases in certain style words after widespread LLM adoption. The authors estimated that at least 13.5% of 2024 abstracts in their dataset had been processed with LLMs, while emphasising this was a lower-bound estimate for that corpus. See [Kobak, González-Márquez, Horvát, and Lause](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.07016).
- Juzek and Ward identified 21 words that rose sharply in scientific English and were overused by tested ChatGPT versions. They found mixed evidence about why the overrepresentation occurred. Their work concerns scientific abstracts, not all prose. See [“Why Does ChatGPT ‘Delve’ So Much?”](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.11385).
- A corpus comparison of generated and human-authored social-science writing reported overuse of infrequent academic vocabulary, nominalisation, limited subordination, and syntactic and semantic homogeneity in the tested output. See [Tudino and Qin](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0024384124001694).
- In a 2025 ACL study of 300 paired non-fiction articles, the majority vote of five annotators who frequently used LLMs for writing misclassified one article. Their explanations commonly mentioned vocabulary, predictable sentence structure, grammatical regularity, limited originality, unnatural quotations, weak flow, generic openings, and upbeat summary conclusions. The study used five “expert” annotators, American publication sources, texts under 1,000 words, and specific models and prompts. It does not establish a universal detector for marketing copy. See [Russell, Karpinska, and Iyyer](https://aclanthology.org/2025.acl-long.267/).

The last study is especially useful because it distinguishes experienced from inexperienced judgement. Its non-expert annotators performed around chance and often relied on false assumptions, such as treating neutral tone or any unusual word as evidence of AI.

### 7.2 Detection is context-dependent

Other research shows why confident authorship claims are risky:

- In a 2025 study of German thesis excerpts, 63 lecturers recognised AI text only slightly better than chance overall, and professional-level generated excerpts were particularly difficult to identify. See [Fiedler and Döpke](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1477388025000131).
- A 2025 [NIST pilot evaluation](https://www.nist.gov/publications/2024-nist-genai-pilot-study-text-text-evaluation-overview-and-results) found large performance variation among generators and discriminators: some generators deceived most tested systems while some discriminators detected nearly all tested generators.
- Research published in *Patterns* found that several detectors disproportionately misclassified writing by non-native English writers and that simple rewriting could evade detection. See [Liang and colleagues](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.patter.2023.100779).

Therefore:

- do not use an AI detector score as proof of authorship;
- do not penalise a writer for a single stylistic feature;
- do not equate formal, plain, neutral, non-native, autistic, or carefully edited prose with AI;
- review the quality and basis of the copy directly.

### 7.3 Why generic AI prose converges

Current evidence and writing practice suggest several causes:

1. **Broad prompts activate broad conventions.** “Write warm, professional website copy” contains no particular speaker, situation, evidence, or choice.
2. **The model is rewarded for producing an acceptable complete answer.** It fills gaps with familiar introductions, balanced lists, safe qualifications, and a conclusion.
3. **Models often expand sparse inputs.** Five factual bullets become 500 words, so unsupported volume is created from transition, abstraction, repetition, and mood.
4. **Generation smooths differences.** An experiment published in *Science Advances* found that access to generative ideas improved average story evaluations, especially for less-creative writers, while making the set of stories more similar. That is a creative-writing experiment, but it demonstrates the convergence trade-off. See [Doshi and Hauser](https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.adn5290).
5. **AI suggestions can shift cultural expression.** A 2025 CHI study found that writing suggestions from Western-centric models homogenised some writing toward Western styles and reduced cultural nuance in its experimental setting. See [Homogenization of AI Writing Assistance](https://doi.org/10.1145/3706598.3713564).
6. **Writers accept polished first drafts too early.** Grammatical finish can disguise missing evidence, weak thinking, and a voice assembled from category averages.
7. **Whole-draft rewriting replaces local judgement.** Repeated “make it more human” passes can change every sentence while resolving none of the content decisions.

### 7.4 No single tell is reliable

The following are not proof of AI use:

- an em dash;
- correct grammar;
- a neutral tone;
- contractions or their absence;
- a list of three;
- a sentence beginning with “And” or “But”;
- words such as “delve”, “important”, “nuance”, “support”, or “community”;
- headings, bullet lists, or bold labels;
- one polished contrast such as “not X, but Y”.

Any of these can become part of a pattern. Removing them mechanically can also damage a good draft. The diagnostic question is whether the wording is accurate, useful, natural for the speaker, and justified by the material.

## 8. A field guide to current AI-copy tropes

These patterns are time-sensitive. Models, prompts, and reader expectations will change.

| Trope cluster | Typical manifestation | Why it feels generated | Better intervention |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Universal throat-clearing | “In today's fast-paced and ever-changing world…” | Starts from a generic era claim instead of the page subject. | Begin with the service, question, or concrete observation. |
| Inflated verb stack | unlock, elevate, empower, navigate, embrace, transform, foster | Movement is asserted while the action remains abstract. | Name what a person or service actually does. |
| Importance inflation | crucial, pivotal, paramount, essential, profound | The adjective tells the reader how much to care. | Supply the consequence or evidence. |
| Abstract noun fog | wellbeing, growth, connection, clarity, transformation, resilience without context | Positive concepts accumulate without a scene, action, or boundary. | Tie the concept to a recognisable decision or example. |
| The contrast machine | “It is not just X; it is Y.” “More than X, it is Y.” | Repeated rhetorical balance creates polish without new substance. | State the stronger proposition directly, or keep the contrast only where it resolves a real misconception. |
| Triad compulsion | Three benefits, three adjectives, three clauses, three takeaways in every section. | Uniform completeness makes unlike material share the same shape. | Use the number of items the evidence contains. |
| Symmetrical sections | Every heading receives the same paragraph length, list, and final reassurance. | The template dictates content volume and hierarchy. | Let important or complex subjects occupy different space. |
| Bold-label list | Every bullet begins with **Concept:** followed by a sentence. | Common generated formatting packages prose without deciding whether items are parallel. | Use a plain list, short subsections, or prose according to the relationship. |
| Performed balance | Every claim is followed by an equal counterclaim and a frictionless synthesis. | Avoids taking a useful position and can hide trade-offs. | State what is known, what is uncertain, and what judgement follows. |
| Relentless positivity | challenges become opportunities; every conclusion points to hope, growth, or a brighter future. | Emotional uplift is applied regardless of subject. | Allow difficulty, uncertainty, limitation, and ordinary endings to remain. |
| Synthetic empathy | “We understand how deeply difficult this journey can be.” | Claims intimacy from no evidence and uses emotional register as a trust shortcut. | Demonstrate attention through accurate language and process. |
| Semantic echo | The same idea is restated in slightly different words across a paragraph. | Sparse input has been expanded rather than developed. | Keep the clearest version and add a fact, example, or nothing. |
| Explainer reflex | A common term is immediately defined, contextualised, and summarised. | Treats all readers as generic novices and pads the page. | Explain only what the intended reader may need. |
| Generic scene-setting | An atmospheric opening unrelated to the provider's real experience. | Simulates reportage or intimacy without evidence. | Use a verifiable scene or omit it. |
| Fabricated specificity | Plausible quotes, statistics, names, credentials, or process details supplied to make prose vivid. | Specificity is cosmetically added rather than sourced. | Use only verified material; mark missing facts. |
| The universal reader | “Whether you are X, Y, or Z, we are here for you.” | Tries to include everyone and reveals no useful service boundary. | Name the actual scope and allow the reader to assess relevance. |
| Summary conclusion | “In conclusion… Ultimately… The future is bright…” | Closes the shape of the answer rather than the page's real job. | End with the last useful fact, limitation, or action. |
| Journey CTA | “Ready to take the first step on your journey?” | Combines metaphor, assumed readiness, and sales rhythm. | Label the real action and explain its consequence. |
| Humaniser noise | Injected slang, random fragments, typos, anecdotes, or punctuation quirks. | Simulates a statistical idea of humanity and may weaken credibility. | Restore the actual speaker's choices and source material. |

### The strongest tell: replaceability

Remove the logo and replace the core nouns with those of another provider. If the copy still works unchanged, ask:

- Which sentence could only have been written from this provider's knowledge or decisions?
- Which claim is supported by a fact on the page?
- What has the writer chosen to leave out?
- Where is a real boundary or trade-off visible?
- Does the content change shape when the reader's question changes?
- Is there any observation more specific than the desired brand adjectives?

## 9. Worked examples

These are hypothetical illustrations of the research principles. They are not proposed public copy for Vive Counselling and should not be treated as confirmed practice facts.

### 9.1 Hero copy

**Weak**

> Empowering you to navigate life's challenges and unlock a more authentic, fulfilling future.

**Why it is weak**

The service is unnamed. “Empowering”, “navigate”, “authentic”, “fulfilling”, and “future” can be transferred to coaching, education, fitness, finance, or healthcare. The sentence promises a direction of outcome without describing the offer.

**Stronger shape**

> Counselling for adults who want to understand recurring difficulties in their relationships, mood, or sense of themselves.

This version names a service, audience, and possible subjects. A real draft would still need to be checked against confirmed scope and the intended page.

### 9.2 Empathy

**Weak**

> We know how frightening it can feel to finally ask for help after carrying everything alone.

**Why it is weak**

It assigns fear, delay, isolation, and a help-seeking narrative to the reader.

**Stronger shape**

> You do not need a finished explanation of what is happening before you make an enquiry. The first conversation can include questions about the service and whether it sounds relevant to what you want help with.

The warmth comes from reducing a practical uncertainty. The wording remains hypothetical until service facts are confirmed.

### 9.3 Approach description

**Weak**

> My holistic, client-centred framework creates a safe and transformative space for deep healing.

**Why it is weak**

Every important word is a label or promised quality. The reader still does not know what the practitioner pays attention to or how sessions work.

**Stronger shape**

> I pay attention to what is difficult now and to patterns that have developed over time, including how you protect yourself, relate to other people, and respond when something feels risky or uncertain.

This shows an approach through attention and questions. It would need factual and clinical review before use.

### 9.4 Inclusion

**Weak**

> We celebrate every identity and provide a completely safe, judgement-free space where you can always be your authentic self.

**Why it is weak**

It makes absolute promises about safety, judgement, and another person's experience. “Every identity” avoids showing relevant knowledge.

**Stronger shape**

> You can use the terms you normally use for your identity, relationships, or practices. Familiar language does not remove the need for questions; the same term can mean different things to different people.

The second version demonstrates a working stance rather than awarding the service an inclusion badge. It remains a hypothetical research example.

### 9.5 Practical information

**Weak**

> We offer flexible and affordable options designed around your unique needs. Contact us to learn more.

**Why it is weak**

“Flexible” and “affordable” are comparative claims without a reference point. The reader has to make contact for information that may belong on the page.

**Stronger shape**

> Sessions cost [confirmed fee] and last [confirmed duration]. [Confirmed payment, cancellation, or delivery detail.] If you are unsure whether an arrangement will work for you, you can ask in an enquiry.

Placeholders expose the missing facts rather than allowing generic reassurance to hide them.

### 9.6 Call to action

**Weak**

> Don't wait to become the best version of yourself. Start your healing journey today.

**Why it is weak**

It creates urgency, defines an ideal self, predicts healing, and hides the actual action.

**Stronger shape**

> Send an enquiry  
> Ask about availability and arrange an initial conversation.

The label predicts the action. The supporting line should only be used if those details are confirmed.

### 9.7 SEO copy

**Weak**

> Looking for a Perth counsellor, Perth therapist, anxiety counsellor Perth or online counselling Australia? Our Perth counselling services provide counselling therapy for adults.

**Why it is weak**

It is written around query variations rather than meaning and may trigger Google's keyword-stuffing concerns.

**Stronger shape**

> Adult counselling in Perth  
> [A natural, accurate opening that explains the service and subjects of the page.]

One precise phrase in a useful title can do more identification work than a paragraph of repetitions. Location and delivery wording must remain factually and strategically current.

### 9.8 Ending

**Weak**

> Ultimately, counselling is not just about overcoming challenges; it is about embracing growth, fostering resilience, and stepping into a brighter, more authentic future.

**Why it is weak**

It combines the contrast machine, a triad, abstract benefits, and an uplifting conclusion. It adds no practical information.

**Stronger shape**

> If you have a question that the website has not answered, include it in your enquiry.

The page ends by resolving a remaining practical need. Some pages may end better on the preceding factual sentence with no CTA at all.

## 10. A practical anti-generic writing process

### Step 1: collect source material before drafting

Gather:

- confirmed service facts;
- practitioner or subject-matter interviews;
- audience questions from real enquiries, search data, or user research;
- exact terms used by the provider and relevant communities;
- evidence for claims;
- practical arrangements and boundaries;
- existing approved wording, if any;
- known unknowns and owners for verification.

Do not ask a model to make sparse inputs sound complete. Missing facts should remain visible.

### Step 2: write a decision brief

Record:

- who reaches the page and why;
- the decision or uncertainty it should support;
- what the reader must be able to find;
- the page's strongest evidence;
- what must not be implied;
- the appropriate next step;
- useful search vocabulary;
- tone tensions and contextual risks.

### Step 3: map questions to answers

Build the page from likely reader questions. For every proposed section, write its one-sentence job. Remove any section whose only justification is convention, page length, or a place to hold keywords.

### Step 4: draft the factual version

Write the plainest accurate version first. Use actual nouns and verbs. Leave markers such as `[confirm fee]`, `[evidence needed]`, and `[owner decision]` rather than inventing connective material.

### Step 5: add consequence and character locally

Explain why a fact matters. Introduce voice where it improves recognition, understanding, or relationship. Do not “apply personality” evenly to every line. Practical details often need to stay practical.

### Step 6: edit in separate passes

1. **Purpose:** Does the page do its stated job?
2. **Coverage:** Are the decision-changing facts present?
3. **Structure:** Is information in the order a reader needs it?
4. **Accuracy:** Can every fact and implication be supported?
5. **Specificity:** Which adjectives or abstractions can become evidence, action, or example?
6. **Voice:** Does the wording sound like an identifiable speaker in this situation?
7. **Pressure:** Does any line threaten autonomy, agitate fear, or promise too much?
8. **Accessibility:** Are language, headings, links, instructions, and errors understandable?
9. **Search:** Is the service and page subject naturally identifiable?
10. **Cadence:** Does the draft sound natural aloud? Are structures repeated without purpose?
11. **Ending:** Does the final line add information or a useful action?

Separating passes reduces the temptation to solve a factual weakness with prettier phrasing.

### Step 7: run an anti-generic review

Highlight:

- sentences transferable to a competitor;
- claims with no visible basis;
- three or more abstract positive nouns in one sentence;
- repeated contrast structures and triads;
- mind-reading and universal “you” statements;
- paragraphs that restate rather than develop;
- headings that could sit on any page;
- conclusions that only tidy or uplift;
- terms from current AI-trope lists.

Revise the underlying problem. Do not delete a word merely because it appears on a list.

### Step 8: read it aloud as the named speaker

Reading aloud catches breathless clauses, false intimacy, sales cadence, accidental rhyme, repeated sentence openings, and phrases that look polished but would be implausible in conversation.

Ask: would this person say this sentence to this reader in this situation? Public copy can be more composed than speech; it should not require a fictional corporate persona.

### Step 9: test with people

Show the page without explaining it. Ask participants to:

- identify the service and provider;
- describe who the page seems for;
- find a practical detail;
- explain the approach in their own words;
- say what they think would happen after the CTA;
- identify anything that feels like a promise;
- mark wording that feels vague, pressuring, clinical, patronising, or implausibly intimate;
- name the unanswered question that would affect their decision.

Observe the task rather than asking only “Do you like the copy?” Preference does not reveal comprehension.

### Step 10: maintain the content

Record fact owners and review triggers. Revisit copy when services, fees, credentials, availability, policies, forms, search positioning, or audience evidence changes. Cross-page contradiction is a writing failure even when every page reads well alone.

## 11. Using generative AI without surrendering the writing

Generative tools can help with labour and contrast. They should not be the source of service facts, professional claims, or the provider's point of view.

### Useful roles

- cluster verified audience questions;
- identify duplication and unsupported claims;
- generate structurally different outlines from a detailed brief;
- test whether headings make sense out of context;
- flag jargon, long sentences, ambiguous pronouns, and undefined acronyms;
- create edge-case variants for forms and errors;
- compare a draft against documented voice constraints;
- produce a literal summary so the writer can see whether the intended meaning survived;
- suggest questions for a subject-matter interview;
- check consistency across a set of confirmed facts.

### High-risk roles

- writing from an industry label and a few brand adjectives;
- inventing audience pain points, testimonials, statistics, or quotes;
- expanding copy to an arbitrary word count;
- generating mental-health or legal claims without authoritative review;
- deciding what the practitioner believes or how they speak;
- mass-producing location or specialist pages;
- “humanising” a complete draft through random style changes;
- applying one prompt to every page regardless of purpose.

### A source-bound prompt template

```text
PAGE JOB
[The decision or task this page must support.]

READER CONTEXT
[What is known from research; do not invent private thoughts or feelings.]

CONFIRMED SOURCE MATERIAL
- [Facts]
- [Exact terms]
- [Evidence]
- [Approved wording]

UNKNOWNS
- [Questions that must remain visible]

CLAIM BOUNDARIES
- [What the copy must not promise or imply]

VOICE CONSTRAINTS
- [Tensions with operational examples]
- [Speaker and relationship]

SEARCH LANGUAGE
- [Natural terms that accurately identify the page]

TASK
[Outline, critique, rewrite one section, find repetition, etc.]

OUTPUT RULES
- Do not add facts, examples, quotes, outcomes, credentials, or practical details.
- Mark missing information rather than completing it.
- Explain the reason for each structural recommendation.
- Prefer one accurate sentence over several paraphrases.
- Keep source wording unchanged where explicitly required.
```

The template reduces ungrounded completion. It cannot supply judgement, fact checking, community consultation, or final editorial responsibility.

## 12. Review scorecard

Use the score to organise discussion. Accuracy, accessibility, and misleading claims are gates, not points that can be offset by strong style.

| Area | Weight | Review questions |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Page purpose and reader decision | 15 | Is the page's job clear? Does it answer the question that brought the intended reader here? |
| Coverage and practical usefulness | 15 | Are the decision-changing facts present at the point they matter? What useful question remains unanswered? |
| Accuracy and claim discipline | 15 | Can facts and implications be verified? Are uncertainty, conditions, and limits visible? |
| Structure and retrieval | 10 | Do title, opening, headings, order, paragraphs, and links help readers find what they need? |
| Specificity and evidence | 10 | Does the page show concrete knowledge, actions, examples, distinctions, and boundaries? |
| Clarity and accessibility | 10 | Is the language understandable without being patronising? Are specialist terms, instructions, links, and errors handled accessibly? |
| Voice and authorship | 8 | Does the copy express recognisable judgement? Would the named speaker plausibly say it? |
| Tone, autonomy, and inclusion | 7 | Does tone fit the reader's context? Does the copy avoid mind-reading, stereotypes, pressure, and unnecessary assumptions? |
| Action and interface copy | 5 | Are actions specific, proportionate, predictable, and supported by relevant context? |
| Search clarity and originality | 5 | Can people and search systems identify the subject naturally? Does the page add value rather than repeat category copy? |

### Automatic review flags

Stop and resolve the issue if:

- a service fact, fee, credential, availability statement, quote, or statistic is unverified;
- the overall impression overstates an outcome, suitability, safety, inclusion, or professional status;
- an important condition appears only in fine print or distant copy;
- a testimonial is invented, altered beyond its permission, or used without relevant ethical review;
- the copy assigns a vulnerable reader a feeling, history, diagnosis, or readiness as fact;
- urgent or fear-based wording pressures the reader without a legitimate safety reason;
- a form action does something different from what its label predicts;
- essential information depends on jargon, metaphor, visual position, colour, or an image;
- several pages repeat near-identical copy to target keywords or locations;
- a readability or AI-detector score is used as a substitute for human review;
- the page could be relabelled for a competitor without meaningful editing;
- AI-generated material has not been checked against primary sources and confirmed practice facts.

## 13. Testing programme

### Before drafting

- Interview the provider or subject-matter expert for language, distinctions, examples, and boundaries.
- Review real enquiries, search terms, analytics, support questions, and referral conversations where lawfully and ethically available.
- Audit competitors for repeated claims and missing practical information, not for phrases to imitate.
- Identify claims that require evidence or professional review.
- Define what a successful reader decision looks like.

### During structure work

- Give participants headings alone and ask what they expect under each one.
- Run a five-second orientation check: what is this page, who provides it, and what can be done here?
- Ask people to locate key information without reading from the top.
- Compare genuinely different information orders, not cosmetic headline variants.
- Test at narrow widths and with realistic text expansion so visual truncation does not change meaning.

### During drafting

- Ask readers to paraphrase each important section.
- Use highlighter testing: mark clear/useful, unclear, and unconvincing passages in different colours.
- Ask what each claim makes them expect in practice.
- Check whether conditional wording is understood as conditional.
- Read interface and error copy in the actual flow, not in a spreadsheet alone.
- Have an independent reviewer verify facts against source records.

### Before publication

- Read every public page for cross-site contradictions.
- Review title, metadata, visible heading, internal link text, and structured facts together.
- Test keyboard and screen-reader navigation of headings, links, labels, errors, and status messages.
- Confirm that important details remain understandable without images or layout cues.
- Check that privacy, response, cost, and commitment expectations are accurate in the enquiry flow.
- Run spelling, grammar, link, and terminology checks, then review every automated suggestion rather than accepting in bulk.

### After publication

- Analyse search queries and onsite paths for mismatched expectations.
- Track repeated questions that indicate missing or unclear copy.
- Review form abandonment alongside error messages and field requirements.
- Ask suitable new clients or readers what the site led them to expect.
- Re-test when a substantial page, service fact, or audience position changes.
- Review AI-trope observations periodically; cultural tells age quickly.

### Use readability scores cautiously

Readability formulas can flag long sentences and long words. They cannot tell whether a reader understands the subject, whether a familiar specialist term is clearer than its replacement, whether the page is coherent, or whether the right information is present.

The US [Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality](https://www.ahrq.gov/talkingquality/resources/writing/tip6.html) warns that grade-level formulas do not measure comprehension, organisation, reader interest, or the ability to use information. A 2024 randomised [health-information readability study](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11606-024-09200-z) likewise notes that formula scores omit word familiarity, prior experience, and cohesion. Use them as a prompt for inspection, then test with readers.

## 14. Key conclusions

1. **Good copy is resolved usefulness.** It helps a real reader understand, decide, or act with less unnecessary effort.
2. **The brief determines the writing.** Audience context, page job, evidence, boundaries, and the next decision should exist before a polished draft.
3. **Plain language creates room for voice.** Clarity handles the basic work; specific observation and judgement make the copy recognisable.
4. **Specificity is the centre of credible service writing.** Concrete actions, examples, terms, process, and limits are stronger than self-awarded adjectives.
5. **Facts and consequences belong together.** Readers need enough service detail to judge relevance and enough explanation to understand why the detail matters.
6. **Claims must remain proportionate to evidence.** Copy should not predict suitability, safety, emotional response, or outcomes beyond what the provider can support.
7. **Warmth is practical.** Candour, ordinary language, room for questions, and a clear next step often carry more warmth than reassurance labels.
8. **Autonomy improves persuasion.** A clear invitation can be direct without manufacturing urgency or narrowing the reader's freedom.
9. **Structure is part of the copy.** Titles, headings, order, paragraph shape, links, labels, and error messages determine whether words can be found and used.
10. **Accessibility and inclusion require more than politeness.** They depend on understandable language, meaningful structure, relevant terms, tested assumptions, and usable interaction copy.
11. **SEO should clarify the page.** Natural subject language, original value, descriptive metadata, and useful internal links serve people and search systems better than repetition.
12. **Formulas are diagnostic aids.** They can reveal missing attention, relevance, evidence, or action, but should not force every reader through the same emotional sequence.
13. **AI-sounding prose is a cluster, not a fingerprint.** Predictable structures, overrepresented vocabulary, generic completeness, weak specificity, and compulsory uplift can create the impression. None proves authorship alone.
14. **Word bans cannot create authorship.** Preserve real source material, decisions, cultural language, uncertainty, and local edits. Do not add mistakes to simulate a person.
15. **The most useful anti-generic test is transferability.** Copy that belongs to a provider contains knowledge, choices, and boundaries a category template could not infer.
16. **Human review must examine meaning.** Detector scores, readability grades, and automated style checks cannot replace factual verification, editorial judgement, accessibility review, and content testing.

### Web reading, voice, and content practice

- [Morkes and Nielsen: Concise, Scannable, and Objective Web Writing](https://deceptive.design/articles/concise-scannable-and-objective-how-to-write-for-the-web/) — an influential 1997 usability study. Its direction remains useful, but its percentages should not be treated as current universal conversion benchmarks.
- [Mailchimp Content Style Guide: Voice and Tone](https://styleguide.mailchimp.com/voice-and-tone/) — a mature practitioner example separating relatively stable voice from context-sensitive tone and prioritising clarity over entertainment.
- [Microsoft: Writing Style](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/apps/design/style/writing-style) — practitioner guidance on warm, relaxed, crisp, and clear interface language.
- [Shopify: Content](https://shopify.dev/docs/apps/design/content) — voice, context-sensitive tone, accessibility, readability, and avoiding duplicated interface copy.

### Persuasion, specificity, and claims

- [Packard and Berger: How Concrete Language Shapes Customer Satisfaction](https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article/47/5/787/5873524) — field data and experiments linking relevant concrete service language with perceived listening, satisfaction, intention, and purchasing behaviour.
- [Message Effects on Psychological Reactance: Meta-analyses](https://academic.oup.com/hcr/article/52/1/38/8178818) — pooled evidence that high freedom-threatening wording increases anger and negative thoughts, with context-dependent effect sizes.
- [Szabó and Mitev: When Online Advertising Backfires](https://doi.org/10.1108/JRIM-09-2024-0422) — a 2025 social-advertising experiment on imperative CTA messages and reactance; useful but not directly generalisable to all website buttons.
- [ACCC: False or Misleading Claims](https://www.accc.gov.au/consumers/advertising-and-promotions/false-or-misleading-claims) — current Australian guidance on proof, overall impression, omission, fine print, prices, benefits, future claims, testimonials, and service advertising.

### Search and generative content

- [Google Search Central: Creating Helpful, Reliable, People-First Content](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/creating-helpful-content) — intended audience, original value, expertise, trust, health-related YMYL topics, and the “who, how, and why” self-assessment.
- [Google Search Central: SEO Starter Guide](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/seo-starter-guide) — descriptive titles, useful and original content, headings, links, metadata, and maintenance.
- [Google Search Central: Generative AI Content](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/using-gen-ai-content) — accuracy, quality, relevance, context, and the risk of scaled generation without value.
- [Google Search Central: Spam Policies](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/essentials/spam-policies) — keyword stuffing, scaled content abuse, doorway pages, hidden text, and other manipulative practices.

### AI language, detection, and homogenisation

- [Russell, Karpinska, and Iyyer: People Who Frequently Use ChatGPT for Writing Tasks Are Accurate and Robust Detectors of AI-Generated Text](https://aclanthology.org/2025.acl-long.267/) — 2025 ACL study of 300 non-fiction articles, experienced and inexperienced annotators, detector comparisons, and a taxonomy of stylistic clues. Findings are limited to the tested genres, people, models, and prompts.
- [Juzek and Ward: Why Does ChatGPT “Delve” So Much?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.11385) — corpus method identifying overrepresented words in scientific abstracts and mixed evidence about their cause.
- [Kobak and colleagues: Delving into LLM-Assisted Writing in Biomedical Publications Through Excess Vocabulary](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.07016) — large-scale vocabulary analysis of more than 15 million biomedical abstracts.
- [Tudino and Qin: A Corpus-Driven Comparative Analysis of AI in Academic Discourse](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0024384124001694) — comparison of generated and human-authored social-science prose, including vocabulary, nominalisation, syntax, and semantic homogeneity.
- [Fiedler and Döpke: Do Humans Identify AI-Generated Text Better Than Machines?](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1477388025000131) — 2025 survey experiment showing that lecturers and detectors were only slightly better than chance on German thesis excerpts overall.
- [Liang and colleagues: GPT Detectors Are Biased Against Non-Native English Writers](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.patter.2023.100779) — evidence of false positives and evasion weaknesses in several detectors.
- [NIST: 2024 GenAI Pilot Study, Text-to-Text Evaluation](https://www.nist.gov/publications/2024-nist-genai-pilot-study-text-text-evaluation-overview-and-results) — benchmark showing large variation among generator and discriminator systems.
- [Doshi and Hauser: Generative AI Enhances Individual Creativity but Reduces the Collective Diversity of Novel Content](https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.adn5290) — controlled story-writing experiment illustrating quality gains alongside output convergence.
- [Moon and colleagues: AI Suggestions Homogenize Writing Toward Western Styles and Diminish Cultural Nuances](https://doi.org/10.1145/3706598.3713564) — 2025 CHI research on style homogenisation and cultural nuance in AI-assisted writing.
- [Li and colleagues: How Does Disclosure of AI Assistance Affect Perceptions of Writing?](https://aclanthology.org/2024.emnlp-main.279/) — experimental evidence that disclosed content-generation assistance can reduce average quality ratings, with effects moderated by reader characteristics. This concerns perception of disclosed assistance, not the intrinsic quality of all AI-assisted copy.
