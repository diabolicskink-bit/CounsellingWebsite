# Directories, demand and market structure: Australian kink-aware counselling

Observed: 17 July 2026  
Geography: Australia, with attention to Perth and Western Australia and to services available nationally online  
Status: retained working evidence for the Australian kink-aware counselling dossier; not a canonical dossier or source register

## Scope and interpretation

This file investigates how Australian and Australia-accessible directories classify adult kink-aware counselling or nearby services, what those classifications do and do not establish, which intermediaries shape discovery, how professional and regulatory structures overlap, and whether any defensible demand measure is available.

The working segment is adult counselling or psychotherapy in which a person can discuss consensual kink, BDSM, fetish interests, power exchange or participation in kink communities without the interest being automatically pathologised. Directory labels such as “kink-friendly”, “kink allied”, “aware” and “knowledgeable” are recorded exactly where possible. They are directory taxonomies, not universal definitions, protected professional titles, evidence of therapeutic fit, or a common competence standard.

The following were deliberately not treated as demand measures:

- directory result or listing counts;
- apparent provider abundance;
- autocomplete or related-search suggestions;
- prevalence estimates for kink interests or behaviour;
- anecdotes, testimonials or reviews;
- unlabelled search-result estimates.

Those signals can describe discoverability, classification or supply-side visibility, but they cannot establish how many Australian adults are seeking, enquiring about, booking or paying for kink-aware counselling.

## Findings at a glance

- The most explicit kink-specific directory taxonomies are supplied by Kink Aware Professionals, Psychology Today Australia and Victorian Inclusive Practitioners. Their classification and verification models differ substantially.
- Kink Aware Professionals supplies the clearest awareness ladder—Friendly, Aware and Knowledgeable—but those levels are selected by the listed professional. The directory says inclusion is not an endorsement and that NCSF does not verify or screen listed professionals.
- Psychology Today uses the public filter “Sex-Positive & Kink-Friendly”. Its Perth result page directly displayed “Show 146 Therapists”, but the result set included online-only and “Serves Area” profiles outside Perth. The number is therefore a dynamic count of directory profiles selected by the platform’s location/category logic, not a count of Perth premises, distinct practitioners, available appointments or demonstrated kink competence.
- Kink Aware Professionals did not yield a reproducible current census. Same-day accessible representations displayed Australia totals of 25 and 24, and Western Australia totals of 4 and 3. Duplicate or stale-looking records were also visible. These observations should not be converted into a supply estimate.
- Australian sexology associations provide more substantial training/accreditation frameworks for psychosexual therapy or sexuality education, but neither the Society of Australian Sexologists nor ASSERT presented a kink-specific practitioner filter. Their curricula or concern lists include sexual subcultures, fetishes or related material, which is relevant but does not prove kink-affirming competence for every member.
- Mainstream counselling/psychotherapy directories validate broader professional standing. ACA visibly includes “Sex Therapy”, “LGBTIQA+” and “Transgender affirming care” among interests but no visible kink/BDSM interest. PACFA exposes broad issue, therapy, telehealth and membership filters, but its dynamic issue taxonomy did not render in the research environment and no kink label could be verified.
- Adjacent directories intermingle different roles. Come Back to Bed’s Non-Monogamy Allied Clinician Directory includes psychologists, counsellors, social workers and a sex/intimacy coach. The SSEAA directory spans sex coaching, bodywork, workshops, professional training and embodied counselling. These services should not be represented as clinically equivalent.
- Exact demand for kink-aware counselling in Australia or Perth remains unmeasured with the evidence available. No authorised first-party analytics, search-console, enquiry, booking or revenue export was present. Keyword Planner metrics were account-gated, and a Google Trends series could not be retrieved. The closest Australian industry survey category combines “Sex & sexuality / Gender / Identity”, so it cannot isolate kink.

## Method

### Source selection

Priority was given to:

1. directory owners’ live category, result, terms, submission and membership pages;
2. Australian professional associations’ directory and accreditation pages;
3. Australian Government and Ahpra material describing current professional or regulatory structure;
4. an Australian counselling workforce survey with stated sample and fieldwork;
5. official explanations of search-demand tools and their limitations.

All live pages were observed on 17 July 2026 unless a separate publication or fieldwork date is stated. A page’s observation date is not the date on which its underlying profiles, membership data or result index were refreshed.

### Count rule

Counts are reported only when the source interface directly displayed a number and its unit could be described. Profiles were not hand-counted from long pages, pages with missing JavaScript output, pagination, or embedded tables. Where a directory returned conflicting numbers during the same session, every directly observed representation is retained and the count is marked non-reproducible.

### Access limitations

The in-app browser was unavailable in the research environment, so sources were inspected through live web retrieval and text extraction. Dynamic interfaces did not always render filters, result totals, embedded Airtable data or JavaScript-generated lists. Google Trends’ interactive query data could not be inspected; a read-only request to its public explore endpoint returned HTTP 429. This is an access failure, not evidence of zero searches or “insufficient data”.

No canonical source IDs were assigned here. The source list at the end is local working evidence for later integration.

## Detailed directory observations

### Kink Aware Professionals

KAP is a service of the US-based National Coalition for Sexual Freedom with worldwide coverage. Its home page describes a list of psychotherapeutic, medical, legal and other professionals who state they are knowledgeable about and sensitive to diverse sexuality. The examples include leather, BDSM, fetish, polyamory and “the Lifestyle”.

Its terms define the awareness ladder:

- **Friendly**: minimal general knowledge, open to learning, and does not automatically treat kink or non-monogamy as pathological;
- **Aware**: specific knowledge of important concepts and practices plus experience with more than one or two clients who identify with kink or non-monogamy;
- **Knowledgeable**: more advanced knowledge and an affirmative approach to care.

Every listed professional must state a belief that kink and non-monogamy can be healthy and that consensual adult sexuality can be healthy when practised safely and responsibly. These definitions are more informative than an unexplained “kink-friendly” badge. They remain declarations made in a submission form rather than an independent assessment.

The submission workflow asks an applicant to create an account, select one or more business genres, supply business and practitioner details, education/certifications, tags, address, internet mode, payment/insurance information and an awareness level, accept the terms, and await administrative approval. The how-to page says approval is usually within 72 hours. Administrative approval is not the same as professional screening: the home page explicitly says inclusion is without verification or screening by NCSF.

The category descriptions also require careful role separation:

- Licensed Mental Health Professionals covers psychiatrists, psychologists, counsellors and therapists described as appropriately educated, licensed or certified in their fields.
- Doctors is a separate clinical category.
- Health and Wellness (non M.D.) covers nutrition, doulas, midwifery, massage, sex education, body work and other services with whatever education, licensing, insurance or bonding is appropriate to the service.
- Life Coaches is separate.
- Continuing Education is an educational/training category.

KAP’s terms say it does not endorse, support, recommend or warrant a professional, including one described as “KAP Certified”. It reserves discretion to refuse or remove a listing and may remove one that is unresponsive or lacks required qualifications, but this does not override the stated absence of NCSF verification/screening.

#### Count and data-quality warning

The live Australia and Western Australia routes did not return a stable total during the observation session:

- the Australia route displayed “Australia (25)” in one representation and 24 in another;
- the Western Australia route displayed 4 in one representation and 3 in another;
- a sorted Australia route also displayed 24;
- Australia exposed pagination/Next;
- at least one practitioner appeared in two Australia records, and one record’s displayed state/postcode combination was inconsistent.

These are direct interface observations of listing records. The conflict may reflect caching, indexing, modification timing or other dynamic behaviour; its cause was not established. No single number should be carried forward as “the number of kink-aware therapists in Australia/WA”.

### Psychology Today Australia

Psychology Today places “Sex-Positive & Kink-Friendly” under “Gender and Sexuality”, alongside Bisexual, Gay, Lesbian, LGBTQ+, Open Relationships Non-Monogamy and Transgender. The Perth route’s editorial heading uses “Sex-Positive, Kink Allied Psychologists and Therapists in Perth, WA.”

Visible search controls included:

- gender;
- in-person, online and available;
- issues, including Sex Therapy;
- therapy types;
- age;
- payment/insurance;
- provider types: Psychologists, Counsellors and Psychotherapists, and Social Workers;
- ethnicity, language, faith, price and sliding scale.

The Perth page directly displayed “Show 146 Therapists” with pages 1–8 and Next. However, visible records included “Online Only” and “Serves Area” entries and an interstate example. The platform’s count therefore has the following unit:

> directory profiles returned by Psychology Today’s current category, location, service-area, availability and ranking logic

It is not a count of Perth-based consulting rooms, WA-registered practitioners, currently accepting practitioners, unique practices, session capacity, or providers whose kink competence has been independently assessed.

The category explainer says this kind of therapist may offer a non-judgemental or identity-affirming context and may work with sexual identity, consensual non-monogamy, shame, power dynamics, communication, self-acceptance, anxiety or depression. It also says special training is generally not required, notes limited programmes/unofficial accreditations, and advises visitors to ask about education, licensing and experience. That is useful consumer guidance, but it confirms the category is not a formal specialty credential.

The Sussex directory terms effective 23 March 2026 say:

- professional information can be self-reported and/or gathered from verified third parties;
- the directory takes measures to verify information but does not guarantee credentials, licences, expertise, accuracy, completeness or quality;
- professionals are responsible for keeping profiles current and for representing applicable licence/certification, location and in-person/online status;
- listing visibility and order can be determined by relevance, completeness, user preferences and engagement, and order is not an endorsement;
- professional membership is paid.

No public category-specific validation requirement for “Sex-Positive & Kink-Friendly” was found.

### Society of Australian Sexologists

The SAS accredited directory has the strongest visible structured distinction between psychosexual therapist, sexuality educator, supervisor and mentor roles. It also lets a visitor filter by state branch and professional registration/body, helping distinguish sexology accreditation from a practitioner’s underlying profession.

For Provisional Psychosexual Therapist accreditation, SAS describes requirements including 200 client-contact hours, 50 supervision hours (including 30 individual), 72 hours of psychosexual psychotherapy, 216 hours of sexological education and 12 hours of sexuality-attitudes/values work, plus registration with an accepted professional association, indemnity insurance and police/working-with-children checks. The curriculum includes socio-cultural material on sexual subcultures and atypical behaviours including paraphilias/fetishes. Clinical Psychosexual Therapist requires substantially more practice, including 1,000 client-contact hours, with sex-therapy orientation after the first 200, and further supervision.

This provides credible adjacent competence evidence. It does not show that every accredited member is affirmative toward consensual kink, nor did the directory expose a kink-specific filter. The directory itself advises users that member disciplines vary and to ask whether a practitioner is qualified and competent for the specific issue.

The JavaScript search returned a blank “Found:” field in text extraction, so neither a national nor WA count was recorded.

### ASSERT

ASSERT describes itself as a national not-for-profit representing counsellors, psychologists, doctors, nurses, psychotherapists, social workers and other health professionals in sexology. Its public sex-therapy scope includes “Cross dressing” and “Fetishes”, alongside desire, arousal, pain, identity, trauma, communication and relationship concerns.

It states that Australian sex therapy is not government-regulated and says its accredited sex-therapist and sex-educator members must meet criteria, show high-level skills, undertake ongoing supervision and further education, and follow codes of conduct. The membership workflow requires an application with qualifications and experience and review by the accreditation membership committee. The public membership categories distinguish Sexuality Educator, Sexuality Researcher, Sexual Therapist, General Professional and Student membership.

Therapists are browsed through state pages. The WA page did not display an aggregate result count. “Fetishes” is an explicit concern in ASSERT’s sex-therapy explanation, but the directory did not offer an explicit kink-aware filter or awareness ladder.

### Victorian Inclusive Practitioners

Victorian Inclusive Practitioners is a volunteer-run, Victoria-focused Airtable list. “Kink” is an exact selectable item under both “Trained to see” and “Lived Experience”. Other options include non-monogamy, sex work, sexual and gender identities, neurodiversity, disability, migration and racial/cultural identities.

This is an important distinction between claimed training and optional lived experience, but neither field alone guarantees a particular method or level of competence. The operator says:

- inclusion is voluntary;
- applicants should have significant expertise or lived experience;
- practitioners are responsible for the accuracy of their answers;
- adequate professional indemnity and professional-association membership with a code and complaint process are expected;
- alternative coaching, bodywork or sexual-healing providers without formal qualifications or a professional body may not qualify;
- the list does not confer ACCC/Ahpra “specialist” status or endorsement;
- complaints are referred to external professional bodies rather than investigated by the list.

The embed can be filtered, grouped or sorted, but it did not expose an aggregate count to the accessible research representation. The operator also warns that details can become stale between seasonal updates.

### Come Back to Bed

The Non-Monogamy Allied Clinician Directory is arranged by state/territory plus “Online Only”. Records expose role, practice, free-text relevant interests, online-session availability, and Medicare/private-health information. Kink appears inconsistently in free text rather than through a standard definition or level.

The directory says providers self-identify as open to or passionate about working with non-monogamous relationships and disclaims professional recommendation or responsibility. A public Google Form accepts Australian non-monogamy-allied clinician submissions, but no public kink-specific verification method was visible.

Role labels include psychologists, a clinical psychologist, counsellors, social workers and a sex/intimacy coach. The page is evidence of a discovery network across adjacent services, not evidence that all entries share a clinical registration, therapeutic scope or kink competency standard.

### ACA and PACFA

ACA and PACFA are useful controls because they separate broader professional registration from niche identity/issue claims.

ACA’s directory visibly offers “Sex Therapy”, “LGBTIQA+” and “Transgender affirming care”, but no kink/BDSM filter was visible. ACA registration levels set education, supervision and client-contact requirements; ongoing registration requires professional development, supervision and, where applicable, insurance. The ACA/ PACFA Australian Register of Counsellors and Psychotherapists is a voluntary self-regulation register. These structures can validate general counselling standing, not niche competence.

PACFA’s search description and advanced page expose proximity, language, telehealth, therapy type/issues, private-health, NDIS, membership and practitioner-status controls. Its dynamic issue options did not render during observation, so the presence or absence of a kink filter could not be established. PACFA’s practice categories and annual supervision/CPD/insurance requirements are broader professional evidence only.

### SSEAA: adjacent somatic education, coaching and bodywork

SSEAA should be kept in the market map because people seeking sexuality support may encounter it, but its directory is not a counselling directory.

Its taxonomy explicitly separates workshops/courses, professional training, presentations, sex coaching and body work. It describes:

- Somatic Sex Coaching as a fully clothed coaching modality without required practitioner-client touch;
- Sexological Bodywork as somatic sex education that may use touch, coaching and teaching, and explicitly says practitioners are educators rather than a new type of massage therapist;
- Embodied Counselling as a somatic counselling approach; SSEAA describes minimum training of 220 hours, 42 client hours and 14 supervision hours for that pathway.

Professional membership requires an approved training pathway, ongoing supervision, professional development, nomination, ethics agreement and an annual fee. SSEAA says professional members carry professional-indemnity and public-liability insurance and describes directory members as pre-checked. Its referral page also distinguishes its somatic/hands-on educational work from medical, psychological and sex-therapy services.

The directory’s visible filters did not include “kink”. It does include polyamorous/open relationships and keyword search. No aggregate result total was displayed, and the broad international list was not hand-counted.

## Official and industry market structure

### National registration versus self-regulated and niche credentials

Ahpra’s public register searches the professions governed through National Boards. The visible profession list includes psychology and medicine but does not present counselling, psychotherapy, sexology or sex therapy as National Board professions. This observation should be used narrowly: an individual sex therapist may also be registered as a psychologist, medical practitioner, nurse, occupational therapist or another Ahpra profession, while a counsellor or psychotherapist may instead hold ACA/PACFA registration.

The Australian Government published the National Standards for Counsellors and Psychotherapists on 13 October 2025. They cover education/training, professional practice, ethics, competency, diversity/inclusion and quality assurance. A later consultation on regulatory models opened on 2 March and closed on 20 March 2026; its page describes the work as early-stage exploration of how the standards might be implemented. As observed in July 2026, the standards therefore should not be misrepresented as a completed statutory registration scheme.

The relevant credential layers are:

1. **Ahpra-registered profession**, where applicable—for example psychologist or medical practitioner.
2. **Counselling/psychotherapy registration or membership**—for example ACA or PACFA, with qualifications, practice, ethics, supervision, CPD and complaint requirements.
3. **Sexology or psychosexual accreditation**—for example SAS or ASSERT, adding sexuality-specific education, practice and supervision requirements.
4. **Directory awareness or identity label**—for example KAP’s Friendly/Aware/Knowledgeable, Psychology Today’s Sex-Positive & Kink-Friendly, or Victorian Inclusive Practitioners’ Kink fields.

These layers can overlap, but none can be inferred from another. A kink directory listing does not establish general professional registration. General registration does not establish kink competence. Sexology accreditation does not automatically establish a kink-affirming stance.

## Demand evidence

### Demand conclusion

No defensible measure of Australian or Perth demand for adult kink-aware counselling was obtained.

This means the evidence does not currently support a numeric claim about:

- monthly Australian searches for a kink-aware counsellor or therapist;
- Perth/WA search volume;
- enquiries or bookings caused by kink-related needs;
- conversion, attendance, repeat sessions or revenue for this segment;
- unmet demand or waiting-time pressure;
- willingness to pay.

It would also be incorrect to infer “low demand” from the absence of a usable metric. The result is **unmeasured**, not zero.

### ACA Workforce Census context

The ACA Workforce Census was prepared by Survey Matters and published in December 2024. Fieldwork ran from 30 September to 27 October 2024 through an online questionnaire. The report states 2,242 responses and a 21% response rate; relevant analyses use their own denominators.

Two values are potentially relevant but must remain adjacent context:

- 12% selected the combined “Sex & sexuality / Gender / Identity” specialisation category among practising counsellors answering that item (n=1,318);
- 4% selected that combined category among the top three most common client presentations (n=1,918).

Private-practice and employed subgroups also differed, but subdivision does not solve the category-matching problem. The item does not reveal how much relates to kink, whether the work is affirmative, whether clients sought a kink-aware provider, or where clients were located. It is evidence that a broad sexuality/gender/identity category appears in Australian counsellors’ work, not evidence of kink-aware counselling demand.

### What would make demand measurable

The strongest next evidence would be an authorised, privacy-safe first-party dataset with a defined period and geography, such as:

- Search Console query rows for a pre-registered term set, with clicks, impressions, CTR and position;
- GA4 landing-page and conversion events joined only at aggregate level;
- enquiry or booking form topic tags with a kink-aware option and an “other” path, accompanied by a clear denominator;
- internal-site search terms;
- booking starts, completed bookings, attendance and repeat-session aggregates;
- an exported Keyword Planner historical-metrics table with Australia/WA targeting, match logic, dates and units.

Any future analysis should preserve zero versus missing data, report the observation window, record query variants, keep Australia and Perth/WA separate, and avoid storing free-text client disclosures unless necessary and appropriately governed.

## Limitations and unresolved questions

- Directory profiles and totals are dynamic. Observation does not establish the underlying index refresh date or current availability.
- KAP totals were internally inconsistent during the same session. They should not be normalised to a preferred number.
- Psychology Today’s “146 Therapists” is reproducible only as the directly displayed result at observation time; service-area and online logic make the geographic unit broader than Perth premises.
- JavaScript or embedded-data limits prevented aggregate counts for SAS, Victorian Inclusive Practitioners and some general directories. Hand-counting was rejected because it would create a brittle, non-source-displayed estimate.
- Free-text labels in Come Back to Bed do not form a controlled kink taxonomy.
- Public directory inclusion and membership pages do not reveal every private review or moderation practice.
- Search-demand tooling did not yield a usable metric. No claim about the segment’s magnitude, direction of change or unmet demand is supported.
- Regulatory status can change. The 2026 Department consultation establishes policy work at that date, not the eventual implementation outcome.
- This pass did not assess practitioner-by-practitioner claims, therapeutic quality, outcomes, appointment availability or pricing.
