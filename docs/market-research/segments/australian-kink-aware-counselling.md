# Australian kink-aware counselling market-research dossier

- **Segment boundary:** Australian adult counselling, psychology,
  psychotherapy, psychosexual therapy, sexology and relationship services that
  publicly name kink, BDSM, fetish, D/s, power exchange or a close equivalent.
- **Geography and delivery:** Australia, with specific attention to Perth and
  Western Australia and to services offered nationally online.
- **Observed:** 17 July 2026.
- **Operation:** New dossier.
- **Status:** Canonical supporting market research. It is not market strategy,
  approved public copy, audience research, a provider recommendation or a
  competence assessment.

## Executive synthesis

The visible service market is not one uniform category. It contains overlapping
layers of general psychology and counselling, psychosexual therapy and
sexology, relationship and multi-person work, online national delivery, and
adjacent coaching, education, professional training and experiential services.
The same provider can occupy several layers. Public role labels and credential
structures are therefore material to any comparison; `kink-friendly therapist`
cannot safely be treated as one standardised provider type. [C01-C23; T01-T09]

Six exact-query samples returned 72 page-level results. Thirty-five were direct
provider pages, 23 were directory pages, three were adjacent coaching pages and
11 were irrelevant or generic. The 35 direct-page appearances represented 23
distinct owned provider domains after domain deduplication. These figures
describe one fixed-depth search sample, not demand, stable ranking or total
supply. [Q01-Q06]

The delivery market is both local and national. The purposive provider sample
contains 19 direct provider brands, seven based in Perth or regional Western
Australia. Six explicitly described unqualified Australia-wide online reach;
one additional provider described online reach across Australia while excluding
Victoria. Local searches can nevertheless look directory-heavy: nine of the
first 12 results for `kink friendly therapist Perth` were directory pages from
only two domains. [Q05; C01-C19]

Provider terminology is not standardised. The observed language included
`kink-friendly`, `kink-aware`, `kink allied`, `kink informed`, `kink/BDSM
savvy`, `sex-positive/kink allied`, `kink-identified` and `Kink & BDSM
Counselling`. In the direct-provider sample, only two of 19 brands gave the
topic a dedicated first-party destination; most placed it in an inclusion list,
clinician biography, relationship page, FAQ or broader service description.
[C01-C19]

No defensible measure of Australian or Perth demand for kink-aware counselling
was obtained. There was no authorised first-party query, enquiry, booking or
revenue dataset; Keyword Planner metrics were account-gated; and no usable
Google Trends series could be retrieved. Directory totals and search-result
composition are supply or visibility evidence, not demand evidence. The correct
finding is **unmeasured**, not zero or low. [M04; T01-T02]

## Research boundary and questions

### Included

- Publicly visible adult-facing services connecting kink, BDSM, fetish, D/s or
  power exchange with counselling, psychology, psychotherapy, psychosexual
  therapy, sexology or relationship work.
- Australian in-person services and services explicitly available online to
  Australian adults.
- First-party provider pages, exact-query observations, relevant directories,
  professional taxonomies, public offer and fee information, and adjacent
  coaching, education or training where search language overlaps.

### Kept separate or excluded

- Coaching, somatic education, professional training, workshops and
  experiential BDSM services are adjacent supply, not direct clinical or
  counselling supply.
- Directory awareness labels are directory taxonomies, not protected titles or
  independently demonstrated competence.
- Kink prevalence, community experiences, preferences, reviews and testimonials
  are not service demand and are outside this market dossier.
- The dossier does not audit Vive Counselling, choose a positioning, recommend
  keywords, determine prices, assess therapeutic quality or make public-copy
  decisions.

### Research questions

1. Which query formulations visibly surface direct providers, directories and
   adjacent offers in Australia and Perth?
2. Which provider and offer segments appear, and where do their role boundaries
   differ?
3. How do providers publicly describe kink awareness, service configuration,
   delivery and price?
4. Which directories and professional structures shape discoverability, and
   what do their labels or counts actually establish?
5. Is there defensible evidence of search, enquiry, booking or paid demand?

## Method and evidence quality

### Query sample

The search pass used a general web-search interface on 17 July 2026. For each
of six exact queries, the first 12 numeric general-web entries returned by the
interface were retained and classified, giving 72 page-level observations.
Multiple pages from one domain were retained at this stage because the unit was
a returned page, then domains were deduplicated separately.

The interface did not expose the underlying search engine, searcher's precise
location, account or login state, device, personalisation, language, SafeSearch,
ads, map pack, autocomplete, People Also Ask, related searches or dependable
result totals. Result order is therefore an observation from this interface and
date, not a reproducible Google ranking.

### Provider sample

The direct-provider sample contains 19 distinct public-facing brands or
practices. Multiple pages and clinicians were consolidated into one brand.
Inclusion required explicit first-party kink/BDSM/fetish/D/s or close-equivalent
language connected to an eligible service. Sampling deliberately preserved
variety across Perth/WA and national online supply, professional roles, practice
configuration, page presentation and public price disclosure. It stopped when
the six exact queries and focused directory follow-ups mostly repeated sampled
patterns or returned directory-only, inaccessible or ineligible leads.

Four overlapping but non-equivalent coaching, training, workshop or
experiential brands were recorded separately. Provider roles, credentials,
affiliations, availability and clinical claims remain provider self-description
unless another source is explicitly named.

### Comparison rules

- Search pages, provider brands, directory records and practitioners are
  different units and are never added together.
- `Not visible` means only that the item did not appear on the sampled pages.
- Public fees are transcribed with their displayed duration, configuration,
  concession, rebate, surcharge or inconsistency. No average or headline market
  range is calculated because the bases are not comparable.
- A directory count is reported only when the interface directly displayed it.
  Long or JavaScript-generated lists were not hand-counted.
- Search visibility, listing totals, provider supply and demand remain separate
  evidence streams.

### Visible language and page forms

- Provider labels varied across friendly, aware, allied, informed,
  knowledgeable, savvy, sex-positive and identity-specific formulations.
- `Kink` and `BDSM` were more common than explicit D/s or power-exchange
  language in the sampled direct-service pages. The D/s query itself was the
  least precise of the six query samples. [Q04; C10]
- Kink language frequently appeared alongside consensual or ethical
  non-monogamy/polyamory, LGBTQIA+ inclusion, sex-work inclusion and
  neurodivergence. This is a provider-presentation pattern, not evidence about
  every client's identity or every provider's equivalent competence across the
  named areas. [C01-C19]
- Dedicated issue pages were uncommon in the 19-brand sample: Progressive
  Therapeutic Collective used a dedicated kink/BDSM counselling page, while
  SHIPS Psychology used a dedicated but broader page for erotically marginalised
  communities. [C10; C13]

## Direct-provider supply sample

### Cross-sample observations

- **19 brands** met the first-party inclusion rule; **7** were based in Perth or
  regional WA.
- **6 of 19** explicitly described unqualified national online reach. A seventh
  offered online sessions across Australia with an explicit Victoria exclusion.
- **2 of 19** used a dedicated first-party destination for the topic; the other
  17 placed the language in broader content.
- **14 of 19** disclosed at least one public price; **12 of 19** paired at least
  one price with a duration.
- Public configurations extended beyond an individual/couple binary: several
  providers named polycules, throuples, groups, multiple simultaneous partners,
  or sessions for three or four people.
- Two sampled brands contained first-party fee conflicts. Corina Johns
  Psychology displayed $200 and $190 for the same stated duration on different
  pages. Progressive Therapeutic Collective displayed $105 and $115 financial
  accessibility prices on different pages. Pleasure Centred Sexology also
  displayed different Coby fees on its homepage and fee page during verification.
  These are recorded as page inconsistencies, not normalised. [C01; C10; C14]

### Credential and taxonomy layers

The visible market contains several layers that can coexist but cannot be
inferred from one another:

1. Ahpra registration in a governed profession, such as psychology, where
   applicable. [M01]
2. Counselling or psychotherapy association registration, such as ACA or
   PACFA. [T07-T08]
3. Sexology or psychosexual accreditation, such as SAS or ASSERT. [T03-T04]
4. A provider's public role and service claim. [C01-C23]
5. A directory's awareness or inclusion label, such as KAP, Psychology Today or
   Victorian Inclusive Practitioners. [T01-T02; T05]

The Australian Government published National Standards for Counsellors and
Psychotherapists in October 2025 and consulted on regulatory implementation
options in March 2026. At the observation date this policy work should not be
represented as a completed statutory registration scheme. [M02-M03]

## Demand evidence

### Current conclusion

No `D##` source is assigned because no source yielded a query-specific,
geographically defined search, enquiry, booking or paid-demand measure for this
segment.

| Evidence route | Result | Why it is not a demand measure here |
| --- | --- | --- |
| First-party analytics and enquiries | No authorised Search Console, GA4, internal-search, enquiry-topic, booking or revenue export was available | Implementation or analytics code is not traffic, enquiry or conversion evidence. |
| Google Ads Keyword Planner | No metric retrieved | Historical metrics required gated account/campaign access; no account or campaign was created. |
| Google Trends | No usable Australian series retrieved | The interactive data did not render and a public request returned HTTP 429. This is an access failure, not a zero or insufficient-data finding. |
| ACA 2024 Workforce Census [M04] | Broad adjacent category only: 12% selected `Sex & sexuality / Gender / Identity` as a specialisation (n=1,318); 4% selected it among top-three client presentations (n=1,918) | The category combines sex, sexuality, gender and identity and cannot isolate kink, adult clients, affirmative care, Perth, search demand or paid demand. |
| Directory counts [T01-T02] | KAP displayed conflicting Australia/WA totals; Psychology Today displayed 146 profiles for its Perth route | Profiles measure supply-side listings selected by directory logic, not searches, enquiries, bookings, attendance, repeat sessions or spend. |
| Six exact-query results [Q01-Q06] | Search composition and visible page types only | A result set can show discoverability but not how often people search or whether they become clients. |

The absence of a usable demand metric does not justify a claim of low demand.
Demand would become measurable through a privacy-safe, defined-period source
such as Search Console query exports, aggregate enquiry or booking topic data,
or an exported Keyword Planner table with recorded match logic, geography,
dates and units.

## Variations, contradictions and unresolved questions

- **Supply count remains unknown.** The provider sample is purposive; KAP totals
  conflicted; Psychology Today's location logic includes service-area and online
  profiles; and several directories exposed no aggregate count.
- **Availability remains unknown.** A public listing or booking link does not
  establish that a suitable appointment could be completed.
- **Competence labels differ.** Friendly, aware, allied, informed and
  knowledgeable are not interchangeable, and only KAP supplied a visible
  tiered definition. KAP also says the listing is not verified or endorsed.
- **Professional roles overlap.** Psychology, counselling, psychotherapy,
  psychosexual therapy, sexology, coaching, somatic education and experiential
  services appear in the same search landscape but have different regulatory,
  training and scope structures.
- **Geography is blurred by telehealth.** A Perth query can return national or
  interstate online providers, while an `online` service page may omit the
  states it can serve.
- **Price comparison is unsafe without normalisation.** Durations,
  configurations, clinician level, concession, rebate, tax, surcharge and
  multi-person bases differ; several pages were internally inconsistent.
- **D/s-specific visibility was weak in this pass.** The D/s query returned
  mostly generic relationship material. This could reflect query phrasing,
  search indexing or a genuine presentation gap, but demand cannot be inferred.
- **Audience conclusions are out of scope.** Repeated provider associations
  with queer/trans inclusion, non-monogamy, sex work and neurodivergence do not
  establish the composition or preferences of Australian kink communities.
