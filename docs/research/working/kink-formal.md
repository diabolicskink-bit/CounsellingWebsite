# Formal evidence packet: kink clients in counselling

**Status:** Working formal-evidence packet for later dossier integration  
**Evidence checked:** 2026-07-16  
**Finding IDs:** Existing stable IDs `F01`-`F15` retained; new sources assigned `F16`-`F33`  
**Geographic frame:** Adults in a Perth-based counselling context with online service across Australia. Direct Australian evidence is limited to an old national population survey, a small rural Tasmanian mental-health study, and a 2020 community-recruited healthcare report.

## Research coverage and boundaries

This packet records formal evidence about adults whose kink, BDSM interests, practices, roles, relationships, or community participation may be central, peripheral, or unrelated to counselling. It covers definitions and heterogeneity; reasons kink enters counselling; therapy and provider experiences; access and disclosure; pathologisation; the separate question of coercion or consent violations; healthcare evidence where it helps explain disclosure but is not counselling evidence; Australian evidence; intersections; and evidence gaps.

`Kink` is used as a broad umbrella in much of the literature. `BDSM` usually refers to overlapping practices or interests involving bondage/discipline, dominance/submission, and sadism/masochism. The studies do not support treating kink, BDSM, fetish interests, leather, consensual non-monogamy, role identity, community membership, fantasy, and behaviour as interchangeable. Study authors also use inconsistent diagnostic language. This packet preserves their terms while keeping consensual kink distinct from paraphilic disorder classifications.

This is a research record, not clinical, legal, copy, website, service-design, or search-strategy guidance. Convenience-sample percentages are not population estimates. Cross-sectional associations are not causal effects. Client reports are not direct observations of clinicians. Provider self-rated competence is not demonstrated skill. Healthcare findings are labelled and are not used as substitutes for counselling evidence.

## Proposed key findings

1. **Kink is heterogeneous in form, meaning, salience, and identity.** A systematic scoping review found highly variable definitions and measures across 60 empirical publications [F02]. A grounded-theory study found that BDSM could be a temporary role, an everyday identity, or a position along a continuum rather than an either/or category [F27]. Current mixed-method community research also shows participation and its meanings vary by gender, sexuality, age, race, education, and degree of community involvement [F29-F30].

2. **Kink is often not the reason for counselling.** In the largest direct client survey, 74.9% said the presenting issue was unrelated to BDSM, 11% tangentially related, and 12% expressly related [F06]. Experienced US psychotherapists similarly described BDSM as usually background or indirectly relevant, with relationship concerns common [F09]. In rural Tasmania, 95.2% of survey participants had used a mental-health professional, but only 7.1% had done so for a kink issue [F16]. These are selected samples, not a population distribution of counselling reasons.

3. **Client-reported therapy experiences are mixed, not uniformly negative.** Kolmes and colleagues recorded 118 incidents of biased or inadequate care and 113 examples of sensitive or culturally aware care among 175 therapy-experienced respondents [F06]. Hoff and Sprott identified termination, prejudice, neutral care, knowledgeable/supportive care, and non-disclosure among 60 therapy-experienced people in 32 couples [F05]. Swedish clients likewise described both negative and positive approaches [F08].

4. **The most consistent harmful pattern is misattribution: treating kink itself as pathology, trauma evidence, abuse, or the explanation for an unrelated problem.** This appears across the older client survey [F06], the US/German couple study [F05], a five-person UK-based study [F07], and 27 Swedish client interviews [F08]. Negative experiences also included requiring cessation, assuming family or partner abuse, visible therapist discomfort, topic avoidance, over-focus, interrogation, misrepresented expertise, and clients having to educate clinicians [F05-F08].

5. **Disclosure is selective and shaped by both relevance and anticipated consequences.** In the Kolmes sample, 65.1% disclosed BDSM and about one-third did not [F06]. The UK-based qualitative study framed self-censorship as protection against stigma, medicalisation, and conflation with abuse or self-harm [F07]. In rural Tasmania, roughly four in five did not disclose to a mental-health professional; irrelevance and fear of stigma both appeared [F16]. A broader US disclosure study found disclosure decisions varied by relationship, perceived risk, honesty, and stigma [F28].

6. **Australian evidence reproduces the disclosure problem but is too limited for Australian prevalence claims.** A representative 2001-2002 survey found 1.8% of sexually active Australians aged 16-59 reported past-year BDSM and did not identify greater unhappiness, anxiety, sexual difficulty, or sexual coercion [F12]. A small rural Tasmanian study of people selected for pre-existing mental-health conditions found 90.5% had never seen a kink-aware mental-health professional and nearly 83% did not disclose [F16]. A 2020 grey-literature report of 118 community-recruited Australian practitioners found 70.2% disclosure to a current mental-health worker versus 36.0% to a doctor, while 95.5% had concealed from at least one healthcare professional [F17]. These samples answer different questions and cannot be pooled.

7. **Current diagnostic systems do not classify consensual kink alone as disorder.** DSM-5 distinguishes a paraphilia from a paraphilic disorder and requires distress/impairment or harm/risk to others for disorder status [F03]. ICD-11 likewise reserves its consenting-individual category for marked distress not explained merely by rejection or fear of rejection, or significant injury/death risk, and separates non-consensual interests [F04]. These are classification rules, not evidence that every kink context is healthy or consensual.

8. **The broad psychopathology stereotype is not supported by the strongest mapped evidence.** The 60-publication review found little support for broad psychopathology models [F02]. A national Australian survey found no greater psychological distress among BDSM participants and lower distress among participating men in univariate analysis [F12]. A highly powered Spanish replication (`N=1,907` recruited; published analyses use a slightly smaller complete sample) found the same psychological structure across practitioners and non-practitioners and mean differences that did not fit a deficit profile [F26]. Cross-sectional, self-selected studies cannot prove that kink causes wellbeing.

9. **The formal evidence for a problem of indiscriminate “over-affirmation” is notably absent.** No located client study measured therapists overlooking coercion, abuse, distress, or dangerous practice because they were too affirming. The boundary is nevertheless empirically important: consent norms were stronger on some measures among BDSM community members, but not on every measure [F20], and consent violations were reported in self-selected alt-sex/kink samples [F21-F22]. The evidence supports keeping consensual kink, accidental injury, coercion, abuse, and clinically significant distress as separate assessment questions; it does not establish the frequency of therapist over-affirmation.

10. **Strong consent norms do not prove universal safety or consent.** In a sexually diverse online sample, BDSM community members considered explicit consent discussions more common and less sexually disruptive than majority participants, but groups did not differ in how strongly they believed consent should be discussed or robustly in recent-event communication [F20]. In an international alt-sex survey, 26% reported an alt-sex-context consent violation [F21]. A US analysis found 767 of 2,888 kink practitioners reported such a violation and formal reporting varied by gender, sexual orientation, and injury [F22]. The consent-violation papers draw from overlapping survey infrastructure and are not independent prevalence replications.

11. **Healthcare evidence shows disclosure can affect access and communication, but it is not counselling evidence.** In a San Francisco Bay Area qualitative study, only 38% of participants with a primary-care provider had disclosed kink and fear of stigma was prominent [F18]. A 2024 online survey found 38% reported at least one healthcare-discrimination experience, 21% withheld a concern, and 32% concealed injuries [F11]. A 2025 Chilean survey found 29.5% reported any discrimination and 11.8% healthcare discrimination, with higher reports among non-binary/queer and bisexual participants [F23]. These studies concern healthcare, use selected samples, and do not establish therapy effects.

12. **Provider research shows uneven knowledge and confidence, including positive attitudes alongside preparation gaps.** In 766 US therapists, 76% had treated at least one BDSM client, 48% considered themselves competent, 64% reported no graduate BDSM training, and 70% disagreed therapy should seek to eliminate BDSM [F14]. A 2025 couple-and-family-therapist survey found generally positive attitudes and moderate self-perceived competence, with higher competence among those reporting at least three hours of BDSM-specific training or relevant credentials/specialties [F32]. Small 2026 interviews found simultaneous themes of education gaps, consent/safety, and trauma [F33]. These are provider perceptions, not client outcomes.

13. **Intersections are visible but usually underpowered.** Australian rural participants were highly sexually and gender diverse [F16]; 84 of 116 BDSM participants in the consent-norm study also identified with another sexual-minority group [F20]; Chilean and Spanish discrimination studies found variation by gender and sexual orientation [F23-F24]; and a 2026 mixed-method study found disclosure and role patterns varied by gender and sexuality [F30]. Most direct therapy samples are too small and too white, educated, community-connected, urban, or sexual-minority-heavy to isolate intersectional effects.

14. **The direct therapy evidence base remains small and weak for outcomes.** The core client evidence consists of one old internet survey, a mixed-mode couple study, five UK-based interviews, and 27 Swedish interviews [F05-F08]. There are no located controlled trials of kink-specific counselling, no comparative effectiveness studies, and no representative Australian study of presenting concerns, disclosure to counsellors, therapy continuation, alliance, or outcomes.

## Detailed findings

### Definitions, heterogeneity, and salience

- The systematic scoping review [F02] found prevalence estimates depended heavily on whether studies asked about fantasy, interest, any lifetime behaviour, recent behaviour, identity, or community participation. Its broad map suggests fantasies are more common than behaviour, but no single pooled prevalence estimate is defensible.
- The Polish grounded-theory study [F27] found participants could identify with a BDSM role only during scenes, extend it into daily life, or understand BDSM as a broader sexuality. Its 12-person sample supports heterogeneity, not a typology prevalence estimate.
- Community participation is not universal. Organised-community members in [F13] described acceptance, self-expression, friendship, safety, education, and conflict. The 2026 survey/interview study [F29] associated greater participation with larger BDSM networks, larger retrospective improvements in life ratings, and more reported benefits, while online spaces served as entry points and in-person events supported deeper ties. Neither source represents people outside organised or online scenes.
- Kink salience varies by context. In [F08], clients raised BDSM because it was directly or indirectly relevant, an important part of life, or a way to test a therapist's response. In [F05-F07], non-disclosure could mean fear of stigma, judgment that kink was irrelevant, or both.

### Why kink enters counselling

- The clearest quantitative result remains [F06]: 74.9% unrelated, 11% tangential, and 12% expressly related among 175 therapy-experienced respondents. Recruitment through BDSM internet groups and retrospective self-report limit generalisation.
- In [F09], 14 highly experienced and largely kink-aware psychotherapists reported that BDSM was usually background or indirectly relevant; relationship concerns were common. This is clinician interpretation from a purposive, all-white US sample, not a client incidence estimate.
- In rural Tasmania [F16], nearly all survey respondents had used a mental-health professional, yet only 7.1% reported doing so for a kink issue. The same study describes service barriers, mental-health concerns, stigma, social support, and kink-related wellbeing as coexisting. Every participant was selected for a pre-existing mental-health condition, so its high distress and suicide-attempt figures must not be treated as kink-population rates.
- No located study provides an Australian population distribution of counselling presenting concerns. The formal sources suggest possible relevance to relationships, identity or shame, disclosure and stigma, consent or abuse, trauma-related meaning, and non-kink mental-health concerns, but do not support ranking these nationally.

### Therapy experiences and continuation

- [F06] recorded incidents rather than unique harmed or helped people: 118 biased/inadequate incidents and 113 sensitive/culturally aware incidents could include multiple reports from one respondent. Negative categories included pathologising, requiring cessation, assuming family or partner abuse, confusing BDSM with abuse, misrepresenting expertise, and making the client educate the clinician.
- [F05] analysed 64 people in 32 heterosexual couples; 60 had therapy experience. Its five categories were termination, prejudice, neutral, knowledgeable/supportive, and non-disclosure. Three women described termination tied to disclosure: one clinician ended therapy and two clients ended it after continuing negative responses. Most themes were not numerically prevalent.
- [F07] found self-censorship functioning as risk management in five kink-identifying adults. The study was UK-based, but accessible records do not establish that all participants were UK residents or that all were therapy clients; it is exploratory evidence about beliefs and experiences around counselling access.
- [F08] found three broad themes: professional stance, therapists' approaches to BDSM, and conversations about BDSM. Negative experiences included judgment/pathologising, visible emotional reactions, using clients to educate the therapist, avoidance, and over-focus; positive accounts included non-judgment and collaborative, client-centred exploration. Only 10 of 27 interviews were fully transcribed, all 27 informed analysis, and one failed recording was represented by participant-approved memory notes.
- No study in this packet compares therapy outcomes after different clinician responses. Termination, disclosure, perceived safety, and retrospective helpfulness are important process signals but not treatment-effect estimates.

### Disclosure, access, and stigma

- Disclosure is neither universally desired nor uniformly avoided. [F06] found 65.1% disclosure and 59.4% had not specifically sought a kink-aware therapist. [F16] found low disclosure to mental-health professionals even though service use was high. [F17] found higher disclosure to current mental-health workers than doctors within a community-recruited Australian healthcare sample.
- The Australian KNOTbound report [F17] provides unusually direct local detail: 118 adults completed an anonymous online survey in April-May 2020; 57 had a current mental-health worker and 40 of those 57 reported disclosure. Of 59 participants describing post-disclosure healthcare, most reported at least one positive or neutral experience and 13 reported one or more negative experiences. It is an ethically approved University of Melbourne summary report, not a peer-reviewed journal article, and KNOTbound supported recruitment.
- The San Francisco study [F18] found both non-disclosure and positive care. Its 115 participants came from town halls, focus groups, and interviews; 44% had visited a provider for a kink-related concern, while only 38% of those with a current primary-care provider disclosed. Fear of stigma and conflation of consensual marks with intimate-partner violence were prominent. The sample was 81% white and 87% community-connected.
- Recent healthcare surveys [F11], [F23], and [F24] show discrimination is not evenly distributed. Race, gender, sexuality, injury, outness, and community support appear in associations, but the studies are cross-sectional and cannot show whether disclosure caused discrimination or whether prior discrimination changed disclosure.
- Minority-stress research [F25] found concealment was the most widely endorsed process in a UK-based overlapping-interest sample. For the BDSM subgroup, prejudice events, acceptance concerns, and self-stigma were associated with distress in hierarchical models. Group overlap was heavy and the authors' term “paraphilic interest” is descriptive study terminology, not a diagnosis.

### Pathologisation, provider knowledge, and counterevidence

- DSM and ICD sources [F03-F04] provide the clearest formal boundary: atypical interest or consensual practice alone is not equivalent to disorder. The sources do not determine whether a particular person's practice is consensual, safe, non-distressing, or clinically irrelevant.
- [F02], [F12], [F26], and the state-of-science review [F31] weigh against broad deficit explanations. [F26] found higher mean secure attachment, conscientiousness, openness, and wellbeing, and lower insecure attachment, rejection sensitivity, neuroticism, and agreeableness among BDSM practitioners in a Spanish sample. Those are group associations, not a claim that practitioners are uniformly healthier or that BDSM caused the differences.
- Provider attitudes are mixed. In [F14], most respondents did not endorse eliminating BDSM and many accepted it as compatible with a healthy long-term relationship, while only 48% felt competent and 64% reported no graduate BDSM training. [F32] similarly found positive attitudes and only moderate perceived competence. [F33] found both sex-positive/consent-focused and trauma-linked frames among just 10 purposely varied therapists.
- The formal literature more clearly documents pathologisation than over-affirmation. No empirical study located measured a clinician overlooking coercion, abuse, distress, or unsafe practice because of an affirming stance. This absence is a research gap, not evidence that the problem never occurs.

### Consent, coercion, injury, and abuse are separate variables

- [F20] found BDSM community members rated explicit consent as more common in their circles and less disruptive than majority participants. No group difference emerged for how strongly consent should be discussed, and recent-event communication results did not establish superior behaviour across measures. The study therefore supports stronger norms on some dimensions, not universal consent expertise.
- [F21] found 26% of 2,996 self-selected international alt-sex participants reported an alt-sex-context violation, alongside 34% adult sexual assault outside alt-sex and 40% childhood sexual assault. The sample combined kink, BDSM, CNM, swinging, leather, and fetish communities; these are not population rates for BDSM clients.
- [F22] analysed 2,888 US kink practitioners from the same broader research programme; 767 reported a kink-context violation. Among those, 46.1% disclosed only informally, 34.2% to no one, and 19.7% to a formal resource. Type, disclosure, and reporting varied by gender, sexuality, and injury. It should not be counted as independent confirmation of [F21].
- Healthcare injury findings are similarly mixed. [F17] included desired physical consequences, accidental injuries, and at least one account of violence that was not consensual play. [F11] explicitly did not distinguish intended/expected from unintended/unwanted injury. These categories must not be collapsed.

### Australian evidence and transfer limits

- [F12] is the only nationally representative Australian source in the packet. It used data from 2001-2002, ages 16-59, a broad single past-year BDSM item, and landline-era computer-assisted telephone methods. It is useful against a broad psychopathology stereotype but is not a current prevalence estimate and says nothing directly about counselling.
- [F16] is direct mental-health-service evidence but represents rural and remote Tasmanians with pre-existing mental-health conditions. Survey/interview overlap was unknown, recruitment used networks and snowballing, and results cannot be generalised to Australian kink adults or Western Australia.
- [F17] is current enough to describe local healthcare disclosure mechanisms but is community-recruited grey literature. It is not specific to counselling, and the mental-health-worker denominator was 57 rather than the full 118.
- No Perth or Western Australian study was located. No Australian representative study measured counselling use, reasons for care, therapist disclosure, early termination, alliance, or outcomes.

### Intersections and remaining evidence gaps

- Direct therapy samples are small and selected: heterosexual couples in [F05], internet-group respondents in [F06], five adults in [F07], and mostly cisgender women from one Swedish forum in [F08]. Provider evidence is largely US and based on self-report or purposive recruitment [F09], [F14], [F32-F33].
- Sexual- and gender-minority overlap is common, but most studies cannot separate kink-related effects from LGBTQIA+, relationship-structure, race, disability, income, geography, or other marginalisation. [F23-F25] and [F30] provide intersectional associations but not causal decomposition.
- Racial and cultural diversity is limited. Many large samples are disproportionately white; [F18] was 81% white, [F14] 93.6% European-American, [F30] about 85% white, and [F11] reported limited racial/ethnic diversity. Chilean and Spanish studies improve geographic breadth but remain online convenience samples.
- No Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander-specific evidence was identified. Evidence is also thin for culturally and linguistically diverse Australians, disabled and chronically ill clients, older adults, asexual or nonsexual kink, sex workers, low-income clients, parents/caregivers, people outside organised communities, and people who conceal kink from researchers or clinicians.
- No controlled therapy trial, validated kink-specific counselling outcome measure, comparative modality study, or longitudinal Australian client study was located. Provider labels such as kink-aware, kink-friendly, kink-allied, affirmative, competent, and experienced are not standardised or independently verified.

## Formal source register

All records below were checked on **2026-07-16**. `n/a` means the source is guidance or review rather than an original participant study.

### F01 - Sprott et al. (2023)

**Citation/type:** Sprott, R. A., Herbitter, C., Grant, P., Moser, C., & Kleinplatz, P. J. “Clinical Guidelines for Working with Clients Involved in Kink.” *Journal of Sex & Marital Therapy, 49*(8), 978-995. Expert-consensus guideline and narrative synthesis. https://doi.org/10.1080/0092623X.2023.2232801  
**Population/method:** No client sample. Twenty experienced clinicians/researchers used a two-year iterative process (2018-2020), literature review, drafting, stakeholder feedback, and revision to develop 23 guidelines.  
**Result used:** The guidance addresses stigma, disclosure, consent, abuse discernment, assessment, and avoiding automatic pathologisation.  
**Limits:** No client outcomes, effect sizes, comparison group, reproducible evidence grading, or formal consensus metric. Retained only as consensus guidance.

### F02 - Brown, Barker, and Rahman (2020)

**Citation/type:** Brown, A., Barker, E. D., & Rahman, Q. “A Systematic Scoping Review of the Prevalence, Etiological, Psychological, and Interpersonal Factors Associated with BDSM.” *Journal of Sex Research, 57*(6), 781-811. Systematic scoping review. https://doi.org/10.1080/00224499.2019.1665619  
**Coverage/method:** Embase, MEDLINE, PsycARTICLES, and PsycINFO searched 26 February 2019 and updated 27 June 2019; 3,915 records initially, 3,100 after deduplication, 133 full texts, and 60 final publications.  
**Result used:** Definitions and estimates varied; fantasies were much more common than behavioural participation; broad psychopathology models had little support; samples were often white, educated, younger, Western, and self-selected.  
**Limits:** Scoping rather than meta-analytic; qualitative studies excluded; English-only; predominantly post-2000; no pooled prevalence.

### F03 - American Psychiatric Association (2013)

**Citation/type:** American Psychiatric Association. *Highlights of Changes from DSM-IV-TR to DSM-5*, pp. 18-19. Official diagnostic-change summary. https://www.psychiatry.org/File%20Library/Psychiatrists/Practice/DSM/APA_DSM_Changes_from_DSM-IV-TR_-to_DSM-5.pdf  
**Population/method:** n/a.  
**Result used:** A paraphilia alone is not automatically a disorder or indication for intervention. Disorder status requires current distress/impairment or satisfaction involving personal harm or risk of harm to others.  
**Limits:** High-level 2013 summary, not the full DSM-5-TR and not audience research. “Harm or risk of harm” is broader than non-consent.

### F04 - World Health Organization (2024)

**Citation/type:** World Health Organization. *Clinical Descriptions and Diagnostic Requirements for ICD-11 Mental, Behavioural and Neurodevelopmental Disorders*. Official diagnostic manual. https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240077263  
**Population/method:** n/a.  
**Result used:** The consenting-individual/solitary-behaviour category requires marked distress not merely arising from rejection or fear of rejection, or significant injury/death risk; ICD-11 removed standalone ICD-10 fetishism and sadomasochism categories and separates non-consensual interests.  
**Limits:** Classification context only; consensual kink and diagnostic paraphilias are not interchangeable.

### F05 - Hoff and Sprott (2009)

**Citation/type:** Hoff, G., & Sprott, R. A. “Therapy Experiences of Clients with BDSM Sexualities: Listening to a Stigmatized Sexuality.” *Electronic Journal of Human Sexuality, 12*. Mixed-mode qualitative content analysis. https://www.researchgate.net/publication/283327343_Therapy_experiences_of_clients_with_BDSM_sexualities_Listening_to_a_stigmatized_sexuality  
**Population/recruitment/n:** Thirty-two committed heterosexual couples (64 people) with consensual erotic BDSM preferences, recruited through US and German BDSM organisations; 60 people had therapy experience.  
**Method/timeframe:** Four US couple interviews in 2002 plus 28 US/German open-ended couple surveys in 2005-2006; recursive/reflexive content analysis.  
**Result used:** Five categories: termination, prejudice, neutral, knowledgeable/supportive, and non-disclosure. Three women described termination tied directly to disclosure.  
**Limits:** Old, self-selected, community-connected, heterosexual couples; mixed languages/modes; not prevalence research.

### F06 - Kolmes, Stock, and Moser (2006)

**Citation/type:** Kolmes, K., Stock, W., & Moser, C. “Investigating Bias in Psychotherapy with BDSM Clients.” *Journal of Homosexuality, 50*(2-3), 301-324. Retrospective internet survey with open-text analysis. https://doi.org/10.1300/J082v50n02_15  
**Population/recruitment/n:** Adults aged 18+ with at least two years of offline BDSM, recruited through BDSM internet groups; 197 responded, 175 had prior psychological care. Seventeen professionals supplied usable responses but were too few for inference.  
**Result used:** Presenting issue unrelated 74.9%, tangential 11%, expressly related 12%; 65.1% disclosed; 59.4% had not sought a kink-aware clinician; 118 biased/inadequate incidents and 113 sensitive/culturally aware incidents.  
**Limits:** Convenience sample, old, retrospective, no response denominator, geography not established, incident counts are not person counts.

### F07 - Nevard (2019/2021)

**Citation/type:** Nevard, I. “Counselling and the Kink Community: A Thematic Analysis.” *British Journal of Guidance & Counselling, 49*(4), 617-628. Qualitative thematic analysis. https://doi.org/10.1080/03069885.2019.1703899  
**Population/recruitment/n:** Five kink-identifying adults in a UK-based study; semi-structured interviews, with related records describing snowball recruitment through intermediaries.  
**Result used:** Self-censorship functioned as risk management against social stigma, medicalisation, and conflation of BDSM with abuse/self-harm.  
**Limits:** Very small sample; participant residence and whether every person had personally used counselling are unclear in accessible records. Retained only as exploratory thematic evidence.

### F08 - Lantto and Lundberg (2022)

**Citation/type:** Lantto, R., & Lundberg, T. “(Un)desirable Approaches in Therapy with Swedish Individuals Practicing BDSM.” *Psychology & Sexuality, 13*(3), 742-755. Qualitative interview study. https://doi.org/10.1080/19419899.2021.1918230  
**Population/recruitment/n:** Twenty-seven Swedish-speaking adults aged 27-65 with BDSM and therapy experience in which BDSM was mentioned; recruited on Darkside; 41 expressed interest.  
**Method:** Semi-structured telephone/in-person interviews, 30-100 minutes; inductive thematic analysis.  
**Result used:** Professional stance, therapist approach, and conversation management separated desirable from undesirable experiences; both positive and negative accounts occurred.  
**Limits:** Self-selected single-forum sample, mostly cis women; only 10 interviews fully transcribed, all 27 informed analysis, and one recording was replaced with participant-approved notes.

### F09 - Lawrence and Love-Crowell (2008)

**Citation/type:** Lawrence, A. A., & Love-Crowell, J. “Psychotherapists' Experience with Clients Who Engage in Consensual Sadomasochism.” *Journal of Sex & Marital Therapy, 34*(1), 67-85. Qualitative clinician study. https://doi.org/10.1080/00926230701620936  
**Population/recruitment/n:** Fourteen licensed US psychotherapists with substantial BDSM-client experience, recruited from a Seattle sex-positive referral list and Kink Aware Professionals; all white, nine with personal BDSM experience.  
**Method/result:** Recorded/transcribed semi-structured interviews and thematic content analysis. BDSM was usually background or indirectly relevant, relationship concerns were common, and 10 stressed adequate knowledge.  
**Limits:** Tiny, purposive, highly experienced/kink-aware sample; clinician perceptions, not client outcomes.

### F10 - Dunkley and Brotto (2018)

**Citation/type:** Dunkley, C. R., & Brotto, L. A. “Clinical Considerations in Treating BDSM Practitioners: A Review.” *Journal of Sex & Marital Therapy, 44*(7), 701-712. Narrative clinical review. https://doi.org/10.1080/0092623X.2018.1451792  
**Population/method:** n/a; no reproducible database strategy, eligibility criteria, search dates, or quality appraisal.  
**Result used:** Synthesises evidence about clinical salience, countertransference, non-disclosure, cultural competence, relationship dynamics, and distinguishing kink from abuse/pathology.  
**Limits:** Not systematic or meta-analytic; mixes heterogeneous research, books, community sources, and guidance. Not independent empirical evidence.

### F11 - Jansen et al. (2024)

**Citation/type:** Jansen, K. L., Fried, A. L., Goetz, C., & Kang, S. “Mistrust and Missed Opportunities: BDSM Practitioner Experiences in Healthcare.” *Journal of Sexual Medicine, 21*(11), 1047-1053. Cross-sectional online survey. https://doi.org/10.1093/jsxmed/qdae125  
**Population/recruitment/n:** 301 English-reading adults identifying with the BDSM community and reporting BDSM play, recruited via FetLife, Reddit, Instagram, and the NCSF newsletter; participant geography not established.  
**Measures/results:** Injuries/disclosure, healthcare discrimination, avoidance, and mistrust. Thirty-eight percent reported at least one discrimination experience, 21% withheld a concern, 48.1% avoided sexual-health questions, and 32% concealed injuries; mistrust predicted concealment.  
**Limits:** Healthcare, not counselling; convenience/self-report/cross-sectional; disproportionately white/male; injury items did not separate intended from unintended injury.

### F12 - Richters et al. (2008)

**Citation/type:** Richters, J., de Visser, R. O., Rissel, C. E., Grulich, A. E., & Smith, A. M. A. “Demographic and Psychosocial Features of Participants in BDSM: Data from a National Survey.” *Journal of Sexual Medicine, 5*(7), 1660-1668. National survey analysis. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1743-6109.2008.00795.x  
**Population/recruitment/n:** Australian Study of Health and Relationships; random national computer-assisted telephone sample aged 16-59, `N=19,307`, 73.1% response, census weighting. Data collected 2001-2002.  
**Result used:** 1.8% of sexually active respondents reported past-year BDSM (2.2% men, 1.3% women); no higher coercion, unhappiness/anxiety, or sexual difficulty; participating men had lower distress (`OR=.33`, 95% CI `.14-.80`).  
**Limits:** Old, age-capped, broad single item, cross-sectional, univariate, relatively small BDSM subgroup.

### F13 - Graham et al. (2016)

**Citation/type:** Graham, B. C., Butler, S. E., McGraw, R., Cannes, S. M., & Smith, J. “Member Perspectives on the Role of BDSM Communities.” *Journal of Sex Research, 53*(8), 895-909. Nominal-group workshop study. https://doi.org/10.1080/00224499.2015.1067758  
**Population/recruitment/n:** Forty-eight organised-community members across seven 90-minute workshops in one large US metropolitan area, recruited through events and leader word of mouth.  
**Method/result:** Participants generated 133 unique terms coded into 15 categories and three broad themes; acceptance, self-expression, friendship, safety, education, and conflict appeared.  
**Limits:** Organised/offline members only, one metropolitan area, race not collected, no lesbian participants despite attempted subgroup representation; inventories are not prevalence estimates.

### F14 - Kelsey et al. (2013)

## Audit decisions and exclusions

- **Retained with correction:** [F05] is 64 individuals in 32 couples, 60 with therapy experience; it is not simply a 32-person study. [F07] is a UK-based five-person study, not securely five UK therapy clients. [F08] must disclose partial transcription. [F11] does not support labelling all participants US residents. [F15] reproduces Kelsey's 76% as 66% and is not empirical.
- **Retained but relabelled:** [F01], [F03], [F04], [F10], [F15], and [F31] are guidance, classification, or narrative synthesis rather than participant research. [F17] is ethically approved Australian grey literature rather than peer-reviewed evidence. [F19] is community-leader Delphi evidence, not a client-outcome study.
- **Added as highest-value new direct evidence:** rural Tasmanian mental-health/service use [F16]; Australian healthcare disclosure [F17]; community-engaged healthcare [F18]; community-leader Delphi [F19]; consent norms and violations [F20-F22]; 2025-2026 discrimination/minority-stress studies [F23-F25]; current counter-pathology, identity, community, gender/disclosure, and provider evidence [F26-F33].
- **Not promoted to the core register:** NCSF's *In Their Own Words* collection contains more than 550 open comments, including mental-health comments, from a 2008 advocacy survey. Its age, self-selection, unclear comment denominators, advocacy framing, and overlap with better documented sources make it unsuitable for core formal findings. It may be useful only as historical illustrative material. https://ncsfreedom.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/12/VD-Survey-In-Their-Own-Words.pdf
- **Held outside the core packet despite relevance:** 2026 qualitative studies of gay men who fist document subcultural care networks and rural isolation, but their highly specific practice, gender, sexuality, and healthcare focus make transfer to the broader counselling audience weak. They are useful gap markers rather than general kink-client evidence: https://doi.org/10.1080/19419899.2026.2621753 and https://doi.org/10.1007/s10508-025-03293-1

## Remaining priority gaps

1. Current representative Australian estimates of kink identity, behaviour, community participation, counselling use, and reasons for seeking care.
2. Perth/Western Australian and Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander-specific research.
3. Longitudinal or controlled studies connecting therapist conduct with alliance, disclosure, continuation, symptom change, relationship outcomes, or harm.
4. Empirical study of both sides of clinical error: pathologising consensual kink and failing to recognise coercion, abuse, injury, or distress.
5. Standardised, externally validated measures of provider competence; most evidence uses labels or self-rating.
6. Samples that include people outside organised kink scenes and online forums, people who do not disclose, and people who do not use identity labels.
7. Better powered intersectional work on race, culture, Indigeneity, disability, chronic illness, age, class, sex work, parenting/caregiving, asexual/nonsexual kink, LGBTQIA+ identities, and relationship structure.
8. Separation of desired sensation/marks, accidental injury, consent violation, coercion, intimate-partner violence, self-harm, and paraphilic-disorder criteria in measures and analysis.
