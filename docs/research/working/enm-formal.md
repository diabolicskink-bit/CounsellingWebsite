# Formal evidence packet: ENM and polyamory in counselling

**Status:** Working formal-evidence packet for later dossier integration  
**Evidence checked:** 2026-07-16  
**Finding IDs:** Provisional stable IDs `F01`–`F17`  
**Geographic frame:** Adults in a Perth-based counselling context with online service across Australia. Most direct therapy evidence is from the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, and Poland; the strongest Australian source is a 2025 qualitative disclosure study.

## Research coverage and boundaries

This packet records formal evidence about adults for whom ethical or consensual non-monogamy (ENM/CNM) or polyamory is central, peripheral, or unrelated to the reason for counselling. It covers terminology and heterogeneity; use of counselling; client and provider experiences; disclosure, stigma, and minority stress; relationship-therapy dynamics; relationship satisfaction; intersections; and evidence gaps.

ENM and CNM are used here as broad umbrella terms for relationships in which all affected partners agree that sexual and/or romantic involvement with more than one person is permitted. Polyamory is one form within that umbrella and commonly foregrounds the possibility of multiple loving or intimate relationships. The evidence does not support treating ENM, CNM, polyamory, open relationships, swinging, relationship anarchy, and non-consensual non-monogamy as interchangeable categories.

This is a research record, not clinical, legal, copy, website, or search-strategy guidance. Percentages are kept attached to their study denominators and sampling methods. Cross-sectional associations are not treated as causal effects. Client reports of practice are not treated as direct observations of therapists, and vignette ratings are not treated as observed clinical behaviour.

## Key findings

1. **The category is heterogeneous in both structure and meaning.** In a Portuguese-language convenience sample of 463 lay definitions, polyamory was described through three overlapping dimensions—emotion, behaviour, and potential—and people currently in CNM relationships gave more nuanced definitions, mentioned consent and ethics more often, and were more likely to describe sex as optional rather than necessary [F01]. A US quota sample of 3,438 single adults found 16.8% expressed some desire for polyamory and 10.7% reported some lifetime engagement, but the measures combined several possible meanings of interest and experience and do not estimate current Australian prevalence [F02].

2. **CNM is not necessarily the presenting concern.** The main peer-reviewed therapy study required only that participants had discussed a romantic relationship with a therapist; it did not establish that CNM was the reason for therapy or distinguish individual from relationship therapy [F03]. A supplementary international thesis found both CNM-related reasons—relationship transitions, communication, agreements, boundaries, jealousy, time, new partners, metamour dynamics, and stigma—and reasons explicitly unrelated to CNM [F06]. No located Australian study quantified why ENM/polyamorous adults seek counselling.

3. **Client-reported therapy experiences include both helpful and harmful practice.** Among 249 current CNM adults in the United States and Canada who had discussed relationships in therapy, exemplary and inappropriate therapist practices formed distinct patterns and both predicted therapist-helpfulness ratings and CNM-related premature termination; 11% reported ending therapy early following a negative CNM-related interaction [F03]. Helpful open-text themes included affirming, nonjudgmental, knowledgeable, or willing-to-learn responses; unhelpful themes included judgment, pathologization, dismissiveness, and knowledge deficits [F03].

4. **Small UK evidence reproduces the main therapy concerns.** In an online qualitative questionnaire with 19 UK adults who had disclosed CNM to a mental-health practitioner, 14 described stigmatizing assumptions, 12 described direct pathologization, and 15 described barriers to openness or the therapeutic alliance [F04]. The sample is small, self-selected, disclosure-conditioned, and has no reported demographic profile.

5. **Provider-side evidence shows risk of relationship structure distorting case formulation.** In an experimental Polish vignette study of 324 current and prospective psychotherapists, otherwise parallel couples were described as monogamous, polyamorous, swinging, or having secret affairs. Polyamorous and swinging couples received lower ratings on relationship satisfaction, morality, and cognitive ability than monogamous couples, and participants more often linked unrelated presenting problems to sexual or emotional non-exclusivity [F05]. The sample was trainee-heavy and the vignettes measured judgments and proposed formulations, not actual care or outcomes.

6. **Disclosure is usually selective and healthcare reactions are mixed.** In interviews with 32 Australian non-monogamous adults, 30 reported selective disclosure; all described actual or anticipated rejection, but just under half also reported at least one positive healthcare response. Twenty-seven considered openness important for adequate care or rapport, particularly in mental healthcare, and almost half had looked for non-monogamy-aware practitioners [F07]. The sample was metropolitan, tertiary educated, entirely sexual-minority, largely polyamorous or relationship-anarchist, and relatively open about non-monogamy.

7. **Stigma is associated with distress, but the evidence does not show that CNM itself causes psychopathology.** Two CNM community studies identified discomfort/disapproval, resource loss or threatening behaviour, character devaluation, and relationship devaluation as recurring stigma forms; in a separate sample, experienced stigma was positively associated with psychological distress, with partial statistical mediation through anticipated and internalized stigma [F09]. A study of 1,176 polyamorous US adults also found CNM minority stress positively associated with depression/anxiety distress [F10]. Both designs were cross-sectional and self-report.

8. **Relationship satisfaction evidence is mixed at study level and null overall.** A 2025 meta-analysis of 35 studies (`N = 24,489`) found no overall difference between monogamous and non-monogamous participants in relationship satisfaction (`k = 29`, Hedges' `g = -0.05`, 95% CI `[-0.20, 0.10]`, `p = .496`) or sexual satisfaction (`k = 17`, `g = 0.06`, 95% CI `[-0.07, 0.18]`, `p = .393`) [F13]. This does not establish equivalence on every outcome, for every ENM form, or for every individual.

9. **The formal therapy evidence base remains small relative to the broader CNM literature.** A 2024 scoping review identified 209 peer-reviewed empirical CNM/polyamory studies across many topics, but only six were published in *Sexual and Relationship Therapy* and the field remained dominated by psychology/psychotherapy-affiliated authors and cross-sectional relationship-outcome work [F14]. The strongest direct therapy studies are convenience samples, retrospective client reports, small qualitative studies, a vignette experiment, and one gray-literature thesis; no controlled trials of ENM-specific counselling or representative Australian therapy studies were located.

## Detailed findings

### Definitions, identity, behaviour, and relationship configuration

- **Consent separates CNM from infidelity but is not always present in lay definitions.** The formal umbrella definition centres agreement or knowledge among affected partners. In the Portuguese lay-definition study, many responses focused on plural love or relationships without mentioning consent; CNM participants mentioned consent and ethics more often than monogamous participants unwilling to consider CNM [F01].
- **Polyamory may refer to emotion, behaviour, or capacity.** The same study's thematic map comprised emotion (for example, feelings for multiple people), behaviour (relationships, commitments, or sexual/relational practices), and potential (openness, ability, or permission), alone or in combination [F01]. This allows someone to use polyamory as a capacity or identity while single or while not currently maintaining multiple partnerships.
- **Sex is not definitionally central for all participants.** CNM participants in [F01] were more likely to describe sex as optional; monogamous participants unwilling to consider CNM were more likely to describe sex as required. Some definitions excluded casual sex, while others treated polyamory as a broad umbrella.
- **Umbrella labels obscure meaningful subgroups.** The studies variously sampled polyamory, open relationships, swinging, relationship agreements, relationship anarchy, or a self-selected CNM identity. Several evidence streams were polyamory-heavy: 78.71% of [F03]'s sample and 85% of [F08]'s sample identified as polyamorous. Findings cannot automatically be transferred to every ENM form.
- **Interest, prior experience, current practice, and identity are different denominators.** In the US quota sample [F02], 16.8% endorsed at least one polyamory-desire item, 10.7% at least one prior-engagement item, and 6.5% knowing someone who had engaged. These are not point-prevalence estimates of current polyamorous relationships, and the study sampled single US adults rather than partnered adults or Australians.

### Why counselling may be sought

- **Direct peer-reviewed evidence does not provide a population distribution of presenting concerns.** Eligibility for [F03] required current CNM and having discussed romantic relationships in therapy, not having sought therapy because of CNM. The authors did not distinguish individual, couple, or family modalities. [F04] asked about experiences after disclosing CNM but did not quantify presenting concerns.
- **One gray-literature mixed-method study provides the most explicit list.** In an international online sample of 318 adults who were current, former, or interested in CNM, 141 (`44%`) reported prior counselling for CNM-related concerns and 168 (`53%`) reported none; the remainder were missing or did not sum to the full sample [F06]. Open-text reasons included discrimination or stigma; transition into or out of CNM; relationship dynamics; communication; agreements, boundaries, and expectations; cohabitation; jealousy; time allocation; new partners; and metamour relationships. A distinct major theme covered reasons not directly related to CNM [F06].
- **That 44% is not a service-use or need estimate.** [F06] recruited through CNM-related social media, included people merely interested in or formerly practising CNM, excluded respondents reporting distress above 5/10, was highly polyamory- and sexual-minority-heavy, and was an unpublished student thesis.
- **Provider bias can make CNM central even when the vignette problem is not.** In [F05], couple background and presenting problems—depressive/work-burnout symptoms, alcohol misuse, erectile difficulty, or couple conflict—were held constant while relationship structure changed. Participants more often proposed sexual or emotional non-exclusivity as the source of problems in CNM conditions than in monogamous conditions.

### Therapy experiences, alliance, and continuation

- **The largest direct client study used mixed methods.** [F03] began with 577 survey completers; 428 had attended at least one therapy session, and the final analytic sample was 249 current CNM adults who had discussed romantic relationships with a therapist. Participants reported observable practices for their current or most recent therapist, rated helpfulness, identified CNM-related premature termination, and could describe very helpful and very unhelpful practices in open text.
- **Exemplary and inappropriate practices were not simple opposites.** Confirmatory-factor and structural-equation analyses supported two related but separate patterns. More exemplary practice predicted higher helpfulness; more inappropriate practice predicted lower helpfulness and greater likelihood of CNM-related premature termination [F03]. Eleven per cent reported such premature termination.
- **Client-defined helpfulness combined stance, conduct, and knowledge.** Among respondents who supplied helpful open text, the four major themes were affirming (49%), helpful therapy practices or techniques (43%), nonjudgmental (36%), and knowledgeable (17%) [F03]. These percentages use only the open-text responders as denominator and overlap because a response could receive multiple codes.
- **Client-defined harm commonly involved pathologization.** Among unhelpful open-text responders, major themes were judgmental (48%), pathologizing (38%), insufficient knowledge (19%), dismissive (18%), and inappropriate focus (5%) [F03]. Examples included treating CNM as the cause or result of another problem, pressuring a client to end a relationship, assuming monogamy, refusing or avoiding the topic, or expecting the client to provide basic education.
- **Screening for an affirming therapist was common but not universally protective.** In [F03], 114 of the final sample had screened the current or most recent therapist and 135 had not. The screening group reported more exemplary and fewer inappropriate practices and higher helpfulness, but the observational comparison is vulnerable to selection differences and cannot establish that screening caused better care.
- **The UK findings were similar but less generalisable.** In [F04], participants described moral or religious judgment, assumptions of hypersexuality, mania or low self-esteem, and CNM being treated as a phase or as the explanation for an unrelated problem. Having to explain basic terminology or relationship structures could consume session time and impede discussion of the presenting concern. The sample included only people who disclosed CNM, so it cannot describe experiences of nondisclosing clients.

### Provider attitudes, formulation, and competence evidence

- **The Polish vignette experiment isolates relationship-label effects better than retrospective client reports.** All 324 participants evaluated four otherwise matched couples under counterbalanced relationship conditions [F05]. The sample comprised 171 clinical-psychology students, 77 postgraduate sexology students, and 74 people recruited through therapy-training institutions or professional associations; 68 were practising certified psychotherapists with a mean of four years' experience. Calling the whole sample experienced therapists would be inaccurate.
- **Monogamy received a halo effect and secret affairs the strongest negativity.** Polyamorous and swinging couples were rated below monogamous couples on relationship satisfaction, morality, and cognitive competence. The secretly unfaithful condition was evaluated most negatively overall. CNM couples were nevertheless rated as more tolerant and similarly interesting, socially engaging, and goal-oriented in some comparisons [F05].
- **Qualitative formulations introduced unsupported pathology and coercion.** Even where vignettes stated mutual interest and established consensual practice, some participants inferred pressure, weak boundaries, lack of security or intimacy, mental illness, drug misuse, or unmet “true” needs, and proposed changing or ending the CNM structure [F05]. These are coded hypothetical responses, not diagnoses or treatment records.
- **A supplementary provider-interview stream shows variation, not uniform hostility.** In 48 in-depth interviews with Polish sex therapists, some framed CNM as relational fulfilment, a response to desire discrepancy, or more honest than infidelity; many were cautious and sometimes framed it as a cover for power imbalance, emotional withdrawal, or compulsive sexual behaviour [F17]. The available source is a conference abstract and does not report counts for these positions.
- **Professional guidance exists, but it is not outcome evidence.** The American Psychological Association's sexual-minority practice guidelines state that psychologists should strive for knowledge of and respect for diverse relationship structures, with the supporting text explicitly including CNM [F15]. A 2025 peer-reviewed guide integrates CNM research, clinical education, and policy issues [F16]. Both are US-centred normative syntheses rather than trials showing which therapy approach produces better outcomes; the APA document is scoped primarily to sexual-minority clients rather than all ENM adults.

### Disclosure, healthcare experience, stigma, and minority stress

- **Australian disclosure was selective rather than all-or-nothing.** Thirty of 32 interview participants in [F07] described selective disclosure across contexts. More than half anticipated consequences and concealed their non-monogamy in at least some settings; about one-third described the emotional or educational labour of disclosure as influencing nondisclosure. Two-thirds also described openness or integrity as a reason to disclose.
- **Actual and anticipated rejection coexisted with positive responses.** All 32 described actual or anticipated rejection, while just under half reported at least one positive response and roughly one-fifth described disclosure as generally positive [F07]. The study therefore does not support a uniformly negative account.
- **Healthcare disclosure was tied to clinical relevance and rapport.** Twenty-seven of 32 still considered openness important for receiving adequate care or building rapport, particularly in mental healthcare. More than half described multiple instances of visible provider discomfort and more than half described provider stigma; more than one-quarter reported difficulty obtaining desired nonjudgmental STI testing. Just under half had experienced at least one positive provider response, and almost half had sought a non-monogamy-aware provider [F07].
- **An earlier US focus-group study found comparable mechanisms.** Twenty adults from a Midwestern polyamory organisation participated in three focus groups [F08]. Themes were provider ignorance, pressure to educate, inadequate screening, sexual stigma, stigma-avoidance or searching for inclusive providers, and experiences of inclusive care. On an 18-person complete-case comparison, mean trust in providers was lower than the instrument's standardisation sample (`M = 33.90`, `SD = 6.10`; `t(17) = -4.645`, `p < .0001`, Cohen's `d = -1.09`) [F08]. The comparison was not matched and the sample was 85% White and 85% polyamorous.
- **Stigma experiences extend beyond healthcare.** [F09] identified four broad themes in 372 CNM participants: expressions of discomfort or disapproval; loss of resources or threatening behaviour; character devaluation; and relationship devaluation. In a separate sample of 383, experienced stigma was positively associated with psychological distress and the association was partially statistically mediated by anticipated and internalized stigma.
- **Minority stress and resilience findings are associative.** Structural-equation modelling in 1,176 US polyamorous adults found CNM minority stress positively related to depression/anxiety distress [F10]. Mindfulness showed both a direct association and a buffering interaction; cognitive flexibility, positive CNM identity, and community connection were also modelled as resilience factors. The cross-sectional design cannot establish direction of effect or the result of counselling.
- **Internalized negativity is not a unitary construct.** In 339 adults with at least two concurrent CNM partners, personal discomfort with one's own relationship structure was associated with lower agreement satisfaction, global relationship satisfaction, and commitment in both relationships, but not sexual satisfaction. Social discomfort and public identification were not associated with the relationship-quality measures [F12].

### Relationship functioning and mixed evidence

- **The strongest comparative synthesis is null overall.** [F13] searched six bibliographic databases and solicited unpublished data, identifying 35 studies with 24,489 participants. Pooled relationship and sexual satisfaction did not differ significantly by monogamous versus non-monogamous relationship orientation.
- **Study-level results were not uniform.** For relationship satisfaction, most studies were null while some favoured monogamy and some favoured non-monogamy; the same mixed pattern appeared for sexual satisfaction [F13]. Moderator analyses found no significant overall variation by heterosexual versus LGBTQ sample, CNM type, or relationship dimension, but the within-type literatures were small.
- **The meta-analysis addresses satisfaction, not all dimensions of relationship health.** It does not by itself establish equivalence in stability, conflict, coercion, abuse, parenting, economic security, or fit between a person's preferences and their current agreements. Most included primary studies were observational and used self-selected samples [F13].
- **Broader research is substantially larger than therapy-outcome research.** The 209-study scoping review [F14] mapped 67 outcome studies, 28 monogamy-versus-CNM comparisons, 11 studies comparing concurrent CNM partnerships, eight comparing CNM types, and six comparing CNM with non-consensual non-monogamy, alongside work on attitudes, antecedents, relational processes, identity, families, and discourse. That breadth does not resolve the limited direct evidence about counselling processes or outcomes.

### Intersections and evidence gaps

- **Sexual and gender diversity are prominent in convenience samples.** [F03] was 43% bisexual, 62.25% women, and about 82% White. All 32 Australian participants in [F07] reported a sexual identity other than heterosexual and more than one-quarter were outside the gender binary. In the US student sample [F11], CNM students were more likely than comparison groups to be sexual- or gender-minority and younger.
- **Cultural and religious position can compound disclosure risk.** In [F07], participants from minoritized cultural or religious backgrounds described more frequent or intense negative disclosure experiences. The small cell sizes and entirely sexual-minority sample prevent separate estimates for culture, religion, sexuality, and gender.
- **A student comparison found higher distress and adversity but cannot attribute them to CNM.** From the US Healthy Minds Network, 91 CNM college students were compared with random samples in four other relationship-status groups (`N = 455`) [F11]. CNM participants reported more depression, emotional abuse, physical abuse, and sexual assault in several comparisons; adjusted depressive symptoms were higher than among dating and married/partnered students. The cross-sectional data do not identify temporal order, and the greater representation of sexual- and gender-minority students and trauma exposure complicates causal interpretation.
- **Australian evidence is thin.** Of the formal sources in this packet, [F07] is the only Australian primary study focused directly on non-monogamy disclosure and healthcare, and it is qualitative. The satisfaction meta-analysis [F13] includes multinational evidence but does not provide an Australian therapy subgroup. No representative Australian estimate of counselling use, reasons for seeking counselling, nondisclosure to therapists, early termination, or therapy outcomes was located.
- **Underrepresented groups remain hard to distinguish.** The direct therapy samples disproportionately represent White, tertiary-educated, urban, sexual-minority, and polyamorous participants. Evidence is especially limited for heterosexual ENM adults, people outside metropolitan areas, culturally and linguistically diverse Australians, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people, people with disability, lower-income clients, people practising swinging or sexually open arrangements without a polyamorous identity, older adults, parents, and people who do not disclose CNM to a practitioner.
- **No ENM-specific treatment-effect evidence was located.** The packet contains observational client reports, qualitative studies, provider vignettes/interviews, comparative relationship research, reviews, and guidance. It does not contain randomised or controlled studies of a counselling model for ENM/polyamorous clients, nor comparative outcomes for individual versus couple or multi-partner therapy.

## Formal source register

### F01 — Cardoso, Pascoal, and Maiochi (2021)

**Citation/type:** Cardoso, D., Pascoal, P. M., & Maiochi, F. H. (2021). “Defining Polyamory: A Thematic Analysis of Lay People's Definitions.” *Archives of Sexual Behavior, 50*, 1239–1252. Peer-reviewed qualitative study.  
**Population/sample/geography:** Portuguese-language web convenience sample; 609 initial responses, 463 valid definitions after 146 blank or unintelligible responses; age 18–66 (`M = 32.19`, `SD = 10.02`); 60% heterosexual; 54% currently monogamous, 32% not in a relationship, 13% currently non-monogamous; 80% urban. The article describes a Portuguese-language sample but does not support transfer to Australia.  
**Recruitment/method:** Social-media snowball web survey; inductive semantic thematic analysis of answers to “How would you define polyamory?” and secondary comparison of current CNM participants with monogamous participants unwilling to consider CNM.  
**Result used:** Emotion, behaviour, and potential were the main overlapping dimensions. Current CNM respondents gave more nuanced definitions and referred more to interpersonal feelings, ethics, consent, and the optional rather than mandatory role of sex.  
**Limits:** Single short survey response; convenience recruitment; secondary group comparison; few current CNM respondents (`n = 61`); definitions are perceptions, not observed relationships.  
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10508-021-02002-y  
**Checked:** 2026-07-16

### F02 — Moors, Gesselman, and Garcia (2021)

**Citation/type:** Moors, A. C., Gesselman, A. N., & Garcia, J. R. (2021). “Desire, Familiarity, and Engagement in Polyamory: Results From a National Sample of Single Adults in the United States.” *Frontiers in Psychology, 12*, 619640. Peer-reviewed cross-sectional survey.  
**Population/sample/geography:** 3,438 single US adults; age `M = 42.93` (`SD = 17.42`); 57.9% women; 67.2% White; 87.2% heterosexual.  
**Recruitment/method:** ResearchNow opt-in internet panels, financially compensated, with quotas based on US Census demographic distributions and oversampling of gay men and lesbian women; identity and response-quality checks.  
**Result used:** 16.8% endorsed at least one desire item, 10.7% at least one lifetime-engagement item, 6.5% knew someone with polyamory experience, and 14.2% of those not personally interested reported positive attitudes.  
**Limits:** US single-adult quota sample rather than probability sample; self-report; broad multi-item indices; not current prevalence; not Australian; data collection funded by Match, which was involved in the full survey design but not the study analysis or publication decision.  
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.619640  
**Checked:** 2026-07-16

### F03 — Schechinger, Sakaluk, and Moors (2018)

**Citation/type:** Schechinger, H. A., Sakaluk, J. K., & Moors, A. C. (2018). “Harmful and Helpful Therapy Practices with Consensually Non-Monogamous Clients: Toward an Inclusive Framework.” *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology, 86*(11), 879–891. Peer-reviewed mixed-method client study.  
**Population/sample/geography:** 577 survey completers; 428 had at least one therapy session; final `n = 249` current CNM adults in the United States and Canada who had discussed romantic relationships with a therapist. Mean age 36.62 (`SD = 10.40`); 62.25% women; about 82% White; 43% bisexual; 78.71% polyamorous.  
**Recruitment/method:** CNM listservs, organisations, online forums, and social groups; reports about observable practices for a current or most recent therapist, a single helpfulness rating, CNM-related premature termination, and open-text helpful/unhelpful experiences; confirmatory factor analysis and structural-equation modelling plus qualitative coding.  
**Result used:** Exemplary and inappropriate practice were distinct and separately predicted helpfulness and premature termination; 11% reported CNM-related premature termination. Helpful themes: affirming 49%, helpful techniques 43%, nonjudgmental 36%, knowledgeable 17%. Unhelpful themes: judgmental 48%, pathologizing 38%, knowledge deficit 19%, dismissive 18%, inappropriate focus 5%, among the relevant open-text responders.  
**Limits:** Retrospective, self-selected client reports; current-CNM and relationship-discussed eligibility; White and polyamory-heavy; therapy modality not specified; open-text denominators differ from the full sample; associations not causal; not Australia.  
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1037/ccp0000349  
**Author manuscript:** https://digitalcommons.chapman.edu/psychology_articles/131/  
**Checked:** 2026-07-16

### F04 — Swindlehurst, Sweet, and Hoelterhoff (2023)

**Citation/type:** Swindlehurst, J., Sweet, J., & Hoelterhoff, M. (2023). “Room for Growth: A Qualitative Study into the Therapeutic Experiences of Consensually Non-Monogamous Clients in the United Kingdom.” *Journal of Couple & Relationship Therapy*. Peer-reviewed qualitative study.  
**Population/sample/geography:** 19 UK adults who had been part of CNM, accessed UK mental healthcare, and disclosed CNM to a practitioner.  
**Recruitment/method:** Social media and forum recruitment; anonymous online qualitative questionnaire; thematic analysis. Demographics were intentionally not collected to protect anonymity.  
**Result used:** Fourteen participants reported stigmatizing assumptions, 12 direct pathologization, and 15 barriers to openness or the therapeutic alliance; 53% highlighted practitioner exposure or education.  
**Limits:** Very small, self-selected, disclosure-conditioned sample; no demographic profile; questionnaire rather than interview; retrospective reports; UK rather than Australia.  
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1080/15332691.2023.2294856  
**Checked:** 2026-07-16

### F05 — Grunt-Mejer and Łyś (2019/2022 issue)

**Citation/type:** Grunt-Mejer, K., & Łyś, A. (2019 online; 2022 issue). “They Must Be Sick: Consensual Nonmonogamy Through the Eyes of Psychotherapists.” *Sexual and Relationship Therapy, 37*(1), 58–81. Peer-reviewed experimental mixed-method vignette study.  
**Population/sample/geography:** `N = 324` in Poland; 273 women, 46 men, one other; age 19–53 (`M = 28`). Recruitment strata were 171 clinical-psychology students at five universities, 77 postgraduate sexology students at two universities, and 74 people from therapy-training institutions or professional associations. Sixty-eight were practising certified psychotherapists.  
**Recruitment/method:** In-class invitations and emails through therapy schools/societies. Each participant evaluated four hypothetical couples under counterbalanced monogamy, swinging, secret-affair, and polyamory conditions; couple context and four presenting problems were otherwise held constant. Repeated-measures analyses were combined with qualitative coding of causal hypotheses and proposed help.  
**Result used:** CNM couples were rated lower than monogamous couples on relationship satisfaction, morality, and cognitive ability, and non-exclusivity was more often introduced as an explanation for unrelated problems. Secret affairs drew the strongest overall negativity.  
**Limits:** Predominantly young, female, trainee sample; hypothetical judgments rather than clinical behaviour; Polish cultural context; the paper's collective use of “psychotherapists” includes prospective therapists.  
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1080/14681994.2019.1670787  
**Checked:** 2026-07-16

### F06 — Stimpson (2022)

**Citation/type:** Stimpson, N. (2022). “Consensual Non-Monogamy and Views on Counselling.” Adler University thesis. Gray-literature mixed-method study; not a peer-reviewed journal article.  
**Population/sample/geography:** 345 initial international online responses; final `n = 318` after exclusions for age, incomplete responses, and distress above 5/10. Of those reporting relevant demographics, mean age was 35.03; 62% women, 17% men, 13% nonbinary; 75% polyamorous; 86% currently CNM.  
**Recruitment/method:** Snowball recruitment through CNM, polyamory, open-relationship, and swinging social-media spaces; quantitative attitudes plus open-text thematic analysis.  
**Result used:** 141 (`44%`) reported previous counselling for CNM-related concerns and 168 (`53%`) did not. CNM-related and unrelated reasons were both described; relationship/partner/couple therapy attitudes showed a small correlation with CNM acceptance (`rho = .12`, `p = .029`), while individual and family-therapy correlations were nonsignificant.  
**Limits:** Unpublished student thesis; self-selected international sample with unclear country denominators; distress exclusion; includes current, former, and merely interested participants; polyamory- and sexual-minority-heavy; single-researcher qualitative analysis; cannot estimate population service use.  
**URL:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/368636288_Consensual_Non-Monogamy_and_Views_on_Counselling  
**Checked:** 2026-07-16

### F07 — Anderson et al. (2025), Australian disclosure study

**Citation/type:** Anderson, J. R., Bondarchuk-McLaughlin, A., Rosa, S., Goldschlager, K. D., & Hinton Jordan, D. X. (2025). “A Qualitative Exploration of the Experiences of Disclosing Non-Monogamy.” *Archives of Sexual Behavior, 54*, 1481–1495. Peer-reviewed qualitative study.  
**Population/sample/geography:** 32 self-identified non-monogamous Australian adults, age 22–58 (`M = 34.42`, `SD = 9.19`); all tertiary educated; 41% had postgraduate education; all reported a sexuality other than heterosexual; more than one-quarter were outside the gender binary. Participants were mainly metropolitan and mainly polyamorous or relationship-anarchist.  
**Recruitment/method:** Targeted social-media recruitment without payment; 12 face-to-face and 20 Zoom semi-structured interviews; grounded-theory-informed open and selective coding; 21.9% of transcripts double-coded.  
**Result used:** 30/32 selectively disclosed; all described actual or anticipated rejection; 27/32 considered openness important for care or rapport; more than half described visible provider discomfort and more than half provider stigma; just under half described a positive response from at least one provider and almost half had sought a non-monogamy-aware provider.  
**Limits:** Small self-selected qualitative sample; highly educated, metropolitan, sexual-minority, polyamory/relationship-anarchy-heavy, and relatively out; COVID-period context; limited double coding; no Perth-specific results or nondisclosing comparison group.  
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10508-025-03119-0  
**Checked:** 2026-07-16

### F08 — Vaughan et al. (2019)

**Citation/type:** Vaughan, M. D., Jones, P., Taylor, B. A., & Roush, J. (2019). “Healthcare Experiences and Needs of Consensually Non-Monogamous People: Results From a Focus Group Study.” *The Journal of Sexual Medicine, 16*(1), 42–51. Peer-reviewed mixed quantitative/qualitative focus-group study.  
**Population/sample/geography:** 20 adults from a Midwestern US polyamory-themed nonprofit; 85% White, 85% polyamorous; 45% cisgender women, 40% cisgender men, with transgender and gender-diverse participants; ages 18–64.  
**Recruitment/method:** Convenience recruitment at an organisation's education meeting; three 40–70-minute focus groups in 2017; survey and Trust in Health Care Providers scale; two-coder inductive thematic analysis.  
**Result used:** Themes of ignorance, education burden, inadequate screening, sexual stigma, avoidance/provider searching, and inclusive care; 18-person trust comparison was lower than the scale standardisation sample (`d = -1.09`).  
**Limits:** Very small organisational convenience sample; focus-group conformity and recall risks; mostly White/polyamorous; unmatched external standardisation comparison; general healthcare rather than counselling alone; US Midwest rather than Australia.  
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsxm.2018.11.006
