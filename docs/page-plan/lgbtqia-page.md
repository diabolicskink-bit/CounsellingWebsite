# LGBTQIA+ counselling page plan

## Status and scope

This is a visitor-first content plan for the `/inclusion/lgbtqia` service page.
It is not approved public copy, a visual design, an implementation plan, or
authority to change the current route.

For this work, treat the page as completely blank. Ignore all currently
implemented LGBTQIA+ page content, including its hero, headings, body copy,
modules, FAQs, calls to action, and metadata wording. None of that material is
an input to this plan.

Current source is relevant only for facts outside the discarded page content:
the route exists, it remains a directly accessible draft with
`noindex, nofollow`, and it is excluded from production navigation and the
sitemap while under review.

This artifact completes the page-plan stage only. Public copy should be
developed later, one section at a time, in separate working documents.

- His approach is psychodynamic and attachment-informed within an integrative

## Blank-page consequence

Nothing has already been communicated on this route. The new hero must carry
the initial orientation normally inherited from an existing opening:

- what service this is;
- who it is for;
- whose practice it is;
- why LGBTQIA+ context is relevant to the page;
- the page's main judgement; and
- the first useful action.

The hero cannot rely on later copy to repair a vague or purely atmospheric
opening. At the same time, it should not attempt to prove the whole practice
position, list the umbrella, or explain every reason someone might seek
counselling.

## Visitor-hat orientation

### Primary visitor

An adult considering individual counselling who is LGBTQIA+, queer or
questioning; trans or gender-diverse; asexual or aromantic; has an intersex
variation; or uses another relevant description, and for whom this context
affects practitioner choice.

They may be looking specifically for help with identity, sexuality, gender,
relationships, family, faith, discrimination, transition, body or medical
history. They may instead be looking for help with anxiety, low mood, grief,
trauma, shame, burnout, work, or another concern and need to know that their
life will not be misread around it.

The primary visitor is defined by the decision they are making, not by one
identity label or imagined personality.

### Evidence-supported visitor positions

The page should remain useful to materially different positions:

1. **Identity is the subject.** The visitor may be questioning, finding
   language, coming out, recognising something later in life, exploring gender,
   considering transition, or working with sexuality, relationships, intimacy,
   shame, or belonging.
2. **Identity changes the context.** The immediate concern may involve family,
   a partner, faith, work, discrimination, community, medical history, bodily
   autonomy, or the cumulative effects of vigilance and rejection.
3. **Identity is not the presenting concern.** The visitor may be coming for
   anxiety, depression, grief, trauma, burnout, relationship strain, or another
   ordinary counselling concern and does not want sexuality or gender made into
   its automatic cause.
4. **The visitor is not certain or not widely out.** They may not have a settled
   label, may disclose differently across relationships or settings, or may be
   deciding whether this is something they want to discuss at all.
5. **The visitor has reason to be wary of counselling.** A previous provider may
   have avoided the subject, over-focused on it, used the wrong name or
   pronouns, invalidated an identity, expected education, or treated identity as
   pathology.
6. **The umbrella itself may not fit cleanly.** Some people with intersex
   variations identify with LGBTQIA+ language and some do not. First Nations,
   cultural, racial, religious, disability, age, rural, and community contexts
   can also materially change what the visitor needs recognised.

These are page-planning positions, not six audiences to address in equal
modules.

### Primary visitor-hat account

> I have arrived because I am choosing a counsellor, and I need to know whether
> my sexuality, gender, sex characteristics, relationships, or uncertainty can
> be understood without becoming the explanation for everything.
>
> At this point I know only that this is an LGBTQIA+ counselling page. I do not
> yet know what that label changes in the actual work.
>
> I still need to recognise whether people come here with situations like mine,
> see evidence of useful familiarity, understand how much I would have to
> disclose or explain, and know what individual counselling with Joel might
> involve.
>
> I do not want the page to give me a general lesson about LGBTQIA+ identities,
> sort me into a category, presume I am out or certain, speak for a community,
> or ask me to trust words such as `safe`, `inclusive`, or `affirming` without
> showing what they mean in practice.
>
> By the end, I need to be able to decide whether contacting Joel is a sensible
> next step and find the current practical information without searching the
> whole site.

This is an evidence-grounded planning hypothesis, not proposed public copy or
manufactured testimony.

## Visitor-first page journey

The page should answer a developing sequence of questions:

1. **What is this, and could it be relevant to me?**
2. **Do you understand the kind of situation that brought me here?**
3. **Will I have to teach you or correct basic assumptions before we reach the
   reason I came?**
4. **Will sexuality or gender be over-explained, avoided, or given a role I did
   not choose?**
5. **What can individual counselling actually do with this situation?**
6. **Does the practical service fit, and what is the next step?**

The intended movement is:

> identify the service → recognise a situation → see familiarity demonstrated
> → retain room around uncertainty and disclosure → understand the work →
> judge practical fit

This sequence should govern the page. The research categories should not.

## What the research changes about the page

### 1. The page cannot treat LGBTQIA+ people as one client type

Sexual orientation, gender identity and expression, sex characteristics,
asexual or aromantic identity, community location, and intersectional context
are not interchangeable. The page needs a broad entry point without implying a
single experience, vocabulary, desired provider identity, or reason for
counselling.

### 2. Identity may be the subject, context, or beside the presenting concern

Australian evidence shows general concerns and identity-related concerns often
coexisting. The page should not imply that someone needs an LGBTQIA+-specific
crisis to belong here, nor should it minimise situations in which identity,
transition, discrimination, family, faith, medical history, or coming out is
central.

### 3. Over-focus and avoidance are both documented problems

Visitors may have encountered a provider who made identity the cause of every
difficulty or one who became silent, changed the subject, or treated identity
as irrelevant when it mattered. The page's central judgement must preserve a
third position: the amount of attention follows the actual concern and the
person's meaning, not a predetermined rule.

### 4. Disclosure is not automatic

Research and public discussion show selective, delayed, uncertain, and absent
disclosure. Attendance at counselling does not mean someone is out, has chosen
a label, wants to discuss identity, or is ready to make a decision about who
else should know.

### 5. An inclusion label does not demonstrate fluency

The current service landscape repeatedly uses `inclusive`, `friendly`, and
`affirming`. Research also records positive and negative experiences under
broad inclusion claims. This page must demonstrate familiarity through accurate
distinctions, language, questions, and boundaries rather than asking the
adjective to carry credibility.

### 6. Useful familiarity does not require identity matching

Provider-identity preferences differ. Some people value shared identity because
it may reduce explanation; others report useful work with providers who do not
share it. The page should not imply a shared identity or claim that identity
matching is necessary for good work.

### 7. Positive therapy and identity-related problems can coexist

High overall satisfaction and specific provider problems appear in the same
research. The page should not dramatise counselling as broadly unsafe or define
Joel solely against harmful therapists. It should give enough information for
the visitor to judge this practice.

### 8. Specific examples carry more credibility than exhaustive coverage

Bi erasure, trans and nonbinary misgendering, asexual pathologisation, intersex
medical history, conversion-practice harm, racial and cultural context,
religion, and rural community overlap are distinct situations. A few carefully
selected distinctions can show judgement. Listing every subgroup would flatten
the differences and still omit people.

### 9. Access information has decision value

Cost, location, time, provider availability, privacy, and knowing where to go
appear in the evidence. The page cannot solve access constraints, but it should
make confirmed delivery, fee, referral, and enquiry information easy to reach.

## Page directions considered

### Direction 1 — Explain what `affirming` means

**Central question:** What does Joel mean when he says the counselling is
LGBTQIA+ affirming?

**Likely structure:** Affirming definition, therapist commitments, areas of
familiarity, issues supported, FAQs, contact.

**Benefit:** Directly responds to a common service label and could make current
positioning more concrete.

**Problem:** It makes the provider's claim the page's protagonist. It easily
becomes a manifesto about correct therapist conduct, repeats promises under
multiple headings, and asks a wary visitor to evaluate adjectives rather than
recognise their own situation. It would also resemble material already
available on countless provider pages.

**Decision:** Do not use as the page argument. The page should demonstrate
affirming practice without organising itself around the label.

### Direction 2 — Map the LGBTQIA+ umbrella

**Central question:** Which identities and issues does this counselling include?

**Likely structure:** Separate blocks for gay and lesbian clients, bisexual and
pansexual clients, trans and nonbinary clients, asexual and aromantic clients,
intersex clients, intersections, and common concerns.

**Benefit:** High visible coverage and easy retrieval for some identity-specific
searches.

**Problem:** The page becomes a shallow identity directory or public education
resource. It turns bounded research categories into a service taxonomy,
suggests the umbrella is homogeneous, mishandles people who do not identify
with the umbrella, and still cannot represent every relevant life. Equal blocks
would reproduce the model-default issues grid.

**Decision:** Reject. Use exact identity terms selectively where they improve
recognition, not as the page's architecture.

### Direction 3 — Follow the visitor's decision

**Central question:** Can I bring my actual concern here without having to hide,
teach, or be reduced to an identity label?

**Likely structure:** Clear service orientation, recognisable reasons for
arriving, useful familiarity demonstrated through distinctions, room around
uncertainty and disclosure, individual-counselling process, and practical fit.

**Benefit:** Keeps the visitor and their decision central. It can include people
whose identity is central, contextual, uncertain, or unrelated to the current
concern without building separate lanes for each subgroup. It gives the page a
reason to exist beyond an `affirming` badge.

**Risk:** The page could repeat `central, context, or unrelated` in every
section, becoming another balanced explainer. It could also define itself too
heavily against poor therapists.

**Response to the risk:** Assign the relevance judgement to one section. Let
the other sections perform different jobs through lived situations, specific
fluency, counselling process, and practical facts. Mention previous provider
problems only where they explain a real visitor question.

**Decision:** Recommended.

## Communication brief

**Artifact register:** Specialist individual-counselling service page. It should
support recognition, comparison, and a fit decision. It is not community
education, a clinical guideline, a practitioner biography, or an institutional
diversity statement.

**Primary reader:** An LGBTQIA+, queer, questioning, gender-diverse, intersex,
asexual, aromantic, or otherwise sexuality- or gender-diverse adult whose
practitioner choice is affected by this context.

**Arrival context:** Search, the Inclusion hub, Home, a recommendation, or direct
route access. The visitor may be discovering counselling, comparing providers,
checking a specific point of fit, or returning after previous therapy.

**Page job:** Help the visitor decide whether Joel's individual counselling
shows enough relevant familiarity and judgement to be worth a first
conversation, whether or not identity is the presenting concern.

**Main message:** Sexuality, gender, sex characteristics, labels, community, and
related experiences can have the place in counselling that the person's actual
situation warrants. They are neither automatically the problem nor required to
stay in the background.

**Supporting ideas:** Recognisable reasons for seeking counselling; less basic
translation; no automatic assumptions; uncertainty and selective disclosure;
no predetermined coming-out, transition, relationship, or faith outcome;
individual-counselling process; current service facts and enquiry route.

**Speaker and relationship:** Joel speaking as one counsellor to one prospective
client. Use `I` for his actions, questions, judgement, and limits. Use `you`
selectively. Do not speak as or for LGBTQIA+ communities and do not imply shared
identity without owner confirmation.

**What the page should help the visitor judge:** Whether their situation fits
the work, whether the page demonstrates more than an inclusion label, and
whether the practical service is suitable enough to enquire.

**Primary search intent:** Finding LGBTQIA+ counselling or a counsellor with
relevant familiarity, including Perth-based and online Australian options.

**Natural subject language:** `LGBTQIA+ counselling`, `LGBTQIA+ counsellor`,
`queer counselling`, `queer therapist`, `gay`, `lesbian`, `bisexual`,
`pansexual`, `trans`, `nonbinary`, `gender diverse`, `asexual`, `aromantic`,
`intersex`, `questioning`, `sexuality`, `gender`, `coming out`, and `online
counselling`. Use only the terms earned by the content. Do not create a keyword
roll call.

**Intended intellectual reading:** This is individual counselling for adults;
Joel understands that identity can be the subject, context, under exploration,
or not the current concern; the page demonstrates baseline fluency without
claiming complete understanding; and practical next steps are clear.

**Intended relational reading:** The visitor retains authority over their
self-description, disclosure, and decisions. They are addressed as a person
considering counselling, not welcomed into a category, corrected, diagnosed,
or recruited into a predetermined story.

**Plausible adverse readings to guard against:**

- `LGBTQIA+` is being treated as one experience.
- The page recognises only people who are out, partnered, transitioning, or in
  identity-related distress.
- Intersex people are assumed to share an LGBTQIA+ identity.
- Bisexual, asexual, or nonbinary identities are acknowledged only as tokens in
  a list.
- Faith is treated as automatically hostile, or conversion-practice harm is
  conflated with all religious life.
- Social discrimination or medical harm is reinterpreted as an internal
  attachment problem.
- `Affirming` means passive agreement rather than thoughtful therapeutic work.
- The page implies Joel has a lived identity or specialist service he has not
  confirmed.

### Copywriting forecast

**GOOD COPYWRITING:** Begin close to the visitor's fit decision. Use a small
number of recognisable situations and exact distinctions to carry credibility.
Let one section be expansive where the umbrella needs care and keep later
sections more compressed. Use plain, direct sentences and permit asymmetry so
the page reads as a developing judgement rather than five completed service
modules. Stop on useful practical information rather than a summary moral.

**BAD COPYWRITING:** Turn the dossier into a polite catalogue, give each identity
or concern an equal paragraph, and qualify every claim until the page becomes a
shapeless inclusion statement. Over-explain terminology, argue with imagined
bad therapists, or make the visitor read Joel's values before seeing their own
situation.

**MODEL-DEFAULT RISK:** Open with `whoever you are`, stack coming out, identity,
relationships, family, and mental health into balanced lists, repeat `whether
you are ... or ...`, announce a `safe and affirming space`, promise the visitor
can be their `authentic self`, and close with a tidy invitation to `take the
next step on your journey`. Resist this through specific visitor questions,
unequal section proportions, concrete distinctions, limited lists, and no
automatic emotional conclusion.

## Recommended page skeleton

The five sections below describe communication jobs, not a required visual
count. If Sections 2 and 3 cannot remain distinct during actual drafting,
combine them rather than preserving the plan mechanically. The useful
distinction is between **what familiarity changes** and **who decides the role
identity has in the work**.

### Hero — Identify the service and establish the page's main judgement

**Stable section ID:** `hero`

**Working H1 direction:** Search-clear and direct. Lead with `LGBTQIA+
counselling` unless the owner later chooses a different umbrella term.
`Affirming` may appear where useful, but should not be the only idea in the H1
or hero.

**Visitor question:** "Is this individual counselling relevant to someone in my
position, even if sexuality or gender is not the problem I came to solve?"

**Hero job:** Identify Joel, the service, the adult individual-counselling scope,
and the main page judgement. Give the visitor a reason to continue and one
clear enquiry path.

**Content to cover:**

- LGBTQIA+ counselling as the page subject;
- sexuality, gender, sex characteristics, identity, or questioning as possible
  subjects or context rather than an automatic diagnosis;
- individual counselling for adults with Joel;
- Perth and online-across-Australia information once, if it helps search and
  access decisions;
- one primary action, likely enquiry or the initial-consult path, subject to
  current Contact wording.

**Editorial rule:** Make the useful judgement visible early. The hero should
orient and invite continued reading, not make the visitor consume a credentials
case or umbrella definition before the page becomes relevant.

**Must not do:**

- reuse or paraphrase the current LGBTQIA+ hero;
- open with `everyone is welcome`, `be your authentic self`, or a `safe space`;
- list every identity or counselling concern;
- imply that LGBTQIA+ identity is normally the reason for therapy;
- claim shared identity, community membership, complete understanding, or a
  guaranteed experience;
- use prevalence or mental-health statistics to justify the service;
- make a rainbow acronym explainer the first task.

**Handoff to Section 1:** The hero identifies the service. The first section
should let the visitor recognise why this page may be relevant now.

**Fact and research basis:** Product direction; current Home, Contact, and
Working with Joel facts; F01-F10; S01-S12.

### Section 1 — Recognisable reasons this page may be relevant

**Stable section ID:** `recognition`

**Detailed copy development:** [Section 1 conceptual work and draft
variations](lgbtqia-section-recognition.md).

**Working heading direction:** Concrete and visitor-facing, such as `What might
bring you here`. Final wording should be developed later.

**Visitor question:** "Do you understand the kind of situation I am actually
in?"

**Section job:** Create recognition across the three main relationships between
identity and the presenting concern without turning them into three equal
cards or an exhaustive issues list.

**Content to cover:**

- when sexuality, gender, sex characteristics, identity, or questioning is
  itself active: finding language, coming out, later-life recognition, gender
  exploration, dysphoria, transition, intimacy, desire, or belonging;
- when a current difficulty is shaped by context: family or partner response,
  faith or culture, rejection, discrimination, work, community, medical
  history, body or reproductive concerns, secrecy, or grief;
- when the person is coming about anxiety, low mood, trauma, shame,
  self-criticism, burnout, relationships, or something else and wants identity
  understood without it taking over;
- a previous counselling experience that makes provider fit or disclosure a
  live concern, as a secondary recognition rather than the page's main story.

**Editorial rule:** Organise around lived reasons for arriving, not identity
labels. Select examples for different kinds of relevance. One developed
situation can do more than a long sequence of topic nouns.

**Must not do:**

- give each subgroup its own paragraph;
- imply everyone is questioning, closeted, distressed by identity, or seeking
  transition support;
- present anxiety, depression, trauma, or shame as inherent to LGBTQIA+ life;
- collapse family, faith, discrimination, conversion practices, and medical
  history into one generic minority-stress story;
- become a broad `what people bring to therapy` section that duplicates Home;
- repeat the page's main `subject or context` proposition as a slogan after each
  example.

**Handoff to Section 2:** Recognition raises a credibility question: how would
relevant familiarity change what the visitor has to explain or correct?

**Research basis:** F02-F19; W01-W19; the situations table; reasons for entering
counselling; and the dossier's subgroup limitations.

### Section 2 — Demonstrate useful familiarity without making a glossary

**Stable section ID:** `useful-familiarity`

**Working heading direction:** Start from the visitor's effort, not Joel's
expertise. A direction such as `Less explaining before you get to the point`
may be explored later, but it is not approved wording.

**Visitor question:** "Will I have to teach you or correct basic assumptions
before I can talk about why I am here?"

**Section job:** Show what baseline LGBTQIA+ familiarity changes in a
counselling conversation. Establish credibility through two or three precise
distinctions and through curiosity about individual meaning.

**Content to cover:**

- using a visitor's own name, pronouns, relationship language, identity terms,
  and level of specificity without forcing an umbrella label;
- knowing that a current partner does not define a bisexual or pansexual
  person's orientation;
- not treating asexuality or aromantic identity as automatic evidence of
  deficiency, trauma, hormonal problems, or an impossible relationship;
- distinguishing intersex variations from sexual orientation and gender
  identity, while not assuming community identification;
- understanding that trans, nonbinary, gay, lesbian, bi, ace, intersex, queer,
  and questioning people who use the same term can still mean different things;
- Joel asking what the relevant language and experience mean for this person
  rather than treating familiarity as licence to presume.

These are candidate distinctions, not a requirement to publish all of them.
Choose for range, naturalness, and confirmed practice scope during drafting.

**Editorial rule:** Demonstrate knowledge in the language and distinctions
used. Pair baseline fluency with individual curiosity. Keep the visitor's
reduced translation burden central; do not turn the section into an education
test Joel is administering to himself.

**Must not do:**

- claim `I understand completely`, `I know your world`, or guaranteed safety;
- imply that Joel shares an identity or that shared identity is required;
- list terminology for the sake of inclusiveness or SEO;
- correct a visitor's preferred terminology;
- use mistakes by other therapists as the section's main evidence;
- claim competence in intersex, transition, conversion-practice, sex-therapy,
  or medical work beyond what Joel confirms;
- rebuild a therapist-facing `what affirming practice means` presentation.

**Handoff to Section 3:** Familiarity alone does not answer who decides how much
attention identity receives, what is disclosed, or what the work should aim
towards.

**Research basis:** F02, F04-F10, F15-F19; W01-W11, W18-W19; language and
self-description findings; mixed findings about provider identity.

### Section 3 — Leave room around relevance, uncertainty, and disclosure

**Stable section ID:** `relevance-and-disclosure`

**Working heading direction:** A direct statement or question about not having
to decide the role of identity in advance. Avoid a generic `come as you are`
heading.

**Visitor question:** "Will you make this the cause of everything, avoid it, or
pressure me to define or disclose more than I want to?"

**Section job:** Establish the page's central working stance: the amount of
attention given to sexuality, gender, sex characteristics, identity, and
community follows what is actually happening and what becomes useful in the
work. There is no predetermined identity outcome.

**Content to cover:**

- identity as the subject, relevant context, under exploration, or not the
  current concern;
- questioning without requiring an immediate label or conclusion;
- selective disclosure and the fact that someone may be out in some settings
  and not others;
- thinking about whether, when, how, or to whom to come out or disclose;
- no predetermined direction around coming out, transition, relationship form,
  family involvement, or reconciling sexuality or gender with faith or culture;
- the difference between affirming identity and passively agreeing with every
  interpretation, behaviour, or relationship decision;
- the ability to work directly with pressure, control, harm, discrimination,
  or unsafe circumstances when those are actually present.

**Editorial rule:** Develop the stance through one or two live contrasts. The
visitor should understand what remains open, not read a list of therapist
principles.

**Must not do:**

- promise that the client alone controls every topic that may become relevant
  in counselling;
- imply pressure to disclose, come out, choose a label, transition, reconcile,
  forgive, or remain connected to family or community;
- present avoidance as neutrality or affirmation as automatic agreement;
- promise confidentiality or privacy details that remain unresolved under
  `SITE-11`;
- treat social harm, rejection, medical coercion, or conversion-practice harm
  as merely an internal belief to reframe;
- repeat Section 2's terminology examples;
- become a four-card `nothing is decided in advance` manifesto.

**Handoff to Section 4:** Once the visitor understands what is not predetermined,
the next question is what individual counselling may actually do with the
situation.

**Research basis:** F02, F04-F09, F11, F15-F19; W02-W09, W11, W14-W18;
disclosure findings; over-focus and avoidance findings.

### Section 4 — Explain the individual-counselling work from the visitor's side

**Stable section ID:** `individual-counselling`

**Working heading direction:** Plain and practical, such as `What we might work
with`. The heading should not lead with modality labels.

**Visitor question:** "What would individual counselling with Joel actually do
with what I am bringing?"

**Section job:** Connect Joel's psychodynamic and attachment-informed approach
to LGBTQIA+-relevant situations in observable, visitor-facing terms, while
keeping the page distinct from the full Working with Joel explanation.

**Content to cover:**

- beginning with the immediate problem and also asking what has shaped it;
- how earlier relationships, family or cultural messages, rejection, secrecy,
  belonging, medical experiences, or repeated vigilance may continue to affect
  shame, self-criticism, trust, closeness, distance, anger, desire, boundaries,
  and self-protection;
- the possibility that a response once helped someone stay connected or safe
  but now creates strain;
- moving between what needs attention now and recurring patterns that become
  visible over time;
- distinguishing the effects of external stigma or harm from an assumption that
  the problem sits inside the person's identity;
- individual counselling as the actual service, with a clear link to the more
  complete approach page.

**Editorial rule:** Describe what the client and Joel may pay attention to, ask,
or make sense of. Use one or two specific examples. Do not promise resolution
or turn the approach into a fixed sequence.

**Must not do:**

- copy the general psychodynamic, attachment, and integrative explanations from
  Working with Joel;
- imply that minority stress, discrimination, family rejection, conversion
  practices, or medical harm is an attachment style or intrapsychic defect;
- make identity exploration sound like diagnosis or assessment;
- imply medical advice, transition assessment, hormone or surgery letters, sex
  therapy, couples therapy, or family therapy unless those services are
  explicitly confirmed;
- present Joel as treating a community or relationship system rather than the
  individual client;
- promise confidence, authenticity, healing, acceptance, or another outcome.

**Handoff to Section 5:** The visitor now needs the concrete service facts and a
proportionate next step.

**Fact and research basis:** Product direction; current Working with Joel and
Home service facts; F02, F04-F06, F10-F14; W01, W05, W09-W11, W14-W18.

### Section 5 — Practical fit and next step

**Stable section ID:** `practical-fit`

**Working heading direction:** Direct and decision-oriented. Avoid an
inspirational final heading.

**Visitor question:** "Does this service fit what I am looking for, and how do I
contact Joel?"

**Section job:** Close the remaining practical gaps, point to fuller information,
and offer one clear next action without making another inclusion claim.

**Content to cover:**

- individual counselling for adults;
- Perth base and online availability across Australia, stated once;
- the current 50-minute, $120 session fact and no-referral fact if the owner
  wants them visible on the specialist page, otherwise a direct Contact link;
- the current free 15-minute initial consultation, subject to the unresolved
  consult-format details in `SITE-16`;
- links to Working with Joel, Contact/Fees, and enquiry;
- a concise reminder that this is not a crisis service only if the page's final
  action could reasonably be mistaken for urgent support.

**Editorial rule:** Prefer confirmed facts and descriptive links. Reduce effort
without promising emotional ease, suitability, availability, reply time, or a
particular experience.

**Must not do:**

- repeat the page's stance about identity;
- end with `you deserve`, `be your authentic self`, or a motivational summary;
- add a large FAQ for questions the body already answers;
- invent a response-time, availability, consult-format, confidentiality,
  Medicare, private-health, payment, or referral arrangement;
- create competing enquiry calls to action;
- make online delivery a repeated SEO slogan.

**Fact basis:** Current Contact source, current-scope service facts, `SITE-11`,
`SITE-14`, `SITE-15`, and `SITE-16` where unresolved practical wording matters.

agency, and process rather than reproduce those pages.

## Material the page should leave out

- All current LGBTQIA+ page wording and structure.
- Prevalence, service-use, distress, satisfaction, or microaggression statistics
  used as persuasion.
- A general guide to LGBTQIA+ identities, terminology, coming out, transition,
  or minority stress.
- An acronym-by-acronym service directory.
- An `affirming counselling means` manifesto or provider-code-of-conduct list.
- Claims that Joel is safe, fully understanding, non-judgemental, culturally
  competent, or an expert without an approved and appropriately bounded basis.
- Claims of shared identity or lived experience.
- A section arguing that LGBTQIA+ people have worse mental health and therefore
  need counselling.
- A conversion-practices explainer, religious-trauma guide, or medical-trauma
  resource unless the page scope is intentionally expanded and competence is
  confirmed.
- Medical transition information, diagnostic assessment, letters, healthcare
  navigation, or sex-therapy advice unless those services are confirmed.
- A generic `all concerns are welcome` section that could appear on every
  counselling page.
- A broad FAQ assembled from questions already answered in the body.
- Repeated reminders that identity may be central or context.
- A conclusion that turns a fit decision into a journey towards authenticity.

## Open decisions before public drafting

1. Which umbrella term should lead the H1 and metadata: `LGBTQIA+`, `LGBTQ+`,
   `queer`, or a deliberate combination? `LGBTQIA+ counselling` is the working
   direction because it matches the route and current project language.
2. Should `affirming` remain in the page title or metadata, appear once in body
   copy, or be demonstrated without leading? The search landscape uses the term,
   but it is not self-proving.
3. What approved experience, training, supervision, professional development,
   practice history, or owner knowledge grounds public claims of LGBTQIA+
   familiarity? Do not infer lived identity.
4. Does Joel provide any gender-related assessment, support letters, referral
   coordination, or transition-specific service? If not, what boundary and
   referral language is accurate?
5. Does Joel want to name work with conversion-practice survivors, religious
   trauma, intersex medical experiences, or asexual and aromantic concerns
   explicitly? Each requires confirmed competence and careful scope.
6. Is the service strictly ongoing individual counselling, or can a partner or
   family member ever join a session? What public boundary is accurate?
7. How should counselling confidentiality, website privacy, enquiry handling,
   chosen names, and disclosure concerns be described once `SITE-11` is
   resolved?
8. Should the specialist page state the current fee, session length, and
   initial-consult option directly, or keep Contact as the single detailed
   source?
9. What is the exact format and purpose of the free 15-minute consult once
   `SITE-16` is resolved?
10. Should Perth appear in the hero or practical ending, and should `online
    counselling across Australia` appear once on this page or remain in shared
    practical context?
11. How should intersex inclusion be worded so people with intersex variations
    are not presumed to identify with LGBTQIA+ language?
12. Is this page only for LGBTQIA+ or questioning adults themselves, or should
    it also account for non-LGBTQIA+ partners or family members seeking
    individual counselling? Do not centre the latter without an explicit
    service and page-purpose decision.

These decisions affect wording and claims. They do not prevent the page
skeleton from being used now.

## Drafting sequence

1. Confirm the umbrella-language, competence, service-boundary, and practical
   decisions that materially affect the hero and later claims.
2. Draft Section 1 first. Test it with a visitor whose identity is central, one
   whose identity changes the context, and one whose presenting concern is
   unrelated. Remove any example included only for coverage.
3. Draft Section 2 and test whether fluency is demonstrated without producing a
   terminology display or implying shared identity. Use no more distinctions
   than the section can develop naturally.
4. Draft Section 3 as the page's central stance. Test it with a questioning
   visitor, a selectively disclosed visitor, an established LGBTQIA+ visitor
   who does not want over-focus, and a visitor who wants identity engaged
   directly.
5. Draft Section 4 from the individual client's side. Check every sentence for
   a concrete counselling action, question, distinction, or limit. Remove
   generic modality explanation.
6. Draft the hero after Sections 1-4 have established what the page genuinely
   contributes. Keep the H1 search-clear and the support copy selective.
7. Draft the practical ending from confirmed current facts and descriptive
   links. Do not use it to restate the page's warmth or values.
8. Read the whole page from a blank search arrival and from the Inclusion hub.
   Remove repeated `identity can be central or context` language.
9. Align the final title, metadata, H1, opening, headings, links, and natural
   search language. Keep the route `noindex, nofollow` until copy, implementation,
   accessibility, responsive, and launch review are complete.
10. Run the copywriter skill's grounded visitor-reading, speaker, deletion,
    model-glue, inclusion, claim, and read-aloud checks before implementation.

## Acceptance checks for the finished page

A cold reader should be able to answer:

- Is this individual LGBTQIA+ counselling for adults with Joel Griffiths?
- Can I recognise at least one situation close to why I am looking now?
- Can I come about anxiety, grief, trauma, relationships, or another concern
  without making identity the presenting problem?
- Can identity, transition, family, faith, discrimination, intimacy, body, or
  community be central when that is what I need to discuss?
- Does the page show useful familiarity rather than merely claim affirmation?
- Do I understand that shared words can mean different things to different
  people?
- Can I be questioning, selectively out, uncertain about disclosure, or unsure
  how much of this I want to discuss?
- Is there room for active therapeutic work without identity being challenged
  or passively used to explain everything?
- Do I understand what individual counselling may pay attention to?
- Can I distinguish counselling from medical, diagnostic, transition-assessment,
  couples, family, or crisis services where those boundaries apply?
- Can I find current practical information and the enquiry path?

The page should also pass these adversarial checks:

- A gay or lesbian visitor is not treated as the default LGBTQIA+ reader.
- A bisexual or pansexual visitor is not defined by their current partner.
- A trans or nonbinary visitor is not presumed to be transitioning, dysphoric,
  or seeking gender-specific counselling.
- An asexual or aromantic visitor is not treated as deficient, traumatised, or
  incompatible with relationships.
- A person with an intersex variation is not assumed to identify with the
  umbrella or to have a particular gender or sexuality.
- A questioning visitor is not pushed towards a label or outcome.
- A religious or culturally connected visitor does not encounter faith or
  community treated as automatically hostile.
- Previous therapy harm is recognised without the page presenting counselling
  generally as dangerous.
- Social and medical harms are not reduced to internal pathology.
- Specialist fluency comes from distinctions, questions, and boundaries rather
  than a self-awarded adjective.
- The page does not read like training material for therapists.
- Each section changes the visitor's understanding and leaves the next section
  a distinct job.
