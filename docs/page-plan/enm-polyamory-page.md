# ENM and polyamory page plan

## Status and scope

- **Artifact:** Working content-strategy and page-skeleton plan.
- **Route:** `/inclusion/enm-polyamory`.
- **Starting assumption:** The page contains its shared site chrome and existing hero, but no post-hero content.
- **In scope:** Visitor needs, page job, content order, section jobs, research-informed coverage, copy risks, drafting questions, and acceptance checks.
- **Out of scope:** Visual direction, layout, components, interaction design, and implementation.

This plan is supporting project memory. It does not approve public wording or
authorise implementation by itself.

## Authority and sources

Use sources in this order when the page is drafted:

1. Current owner instructions and approved wording.
2. [Public Writing Direction](../project/writing-direction.md).
3. [Product Direction](../project/product-direction.md).
4. Current service facts in source and [Current Project Scope](../project/current-scope.md).
5. The [ENM and polyamory client dossier](../research/audiences/enm-polyamory-clients.md) as supporting evidence.

The dossier records what the evidence found. It does not dictate the page
structure, approve claims, or supply a treatment model. Its statistics should
not be turned into marketing claims merely because they are available.

## What the existing hero already needs to do

This plan treats the hero's current communication job as established, even if
its exact wording remains under owner-led revision. The hero should already:

- identify counselling for ENM, polyamory, open relationships, and related
  forms of non-monogamy;
- establish that familiar language can be used without a basic terminology
  lesson;
- say that Joel starts with what is happening for the person rather than a
  preferred relationship shape;
- name a small number of relevant concerns, currently jealousy, agreements,
  pressure, and uncertainty;
- offer the primary enquiry action.

Post-hero sections should develop and prove those propositions. They should
not restart the page with another definition of ENM or a second general promise
of acceptance.

## Visitor-first starting point

### Primary reader

An adult considering individual counselling with Joel for whom non-monogamy
is the main concern, relevant context, something under exploration, or an
ordinary part of life that may have little to do with the reason for therapy.

### Evidence-supported reader positions

These are situations, not personas. One visitor may occupy more than one.

| Reader position | What may have made the page relevant | Live question the page should answer |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A current ENM or polyamorous person with a relationship difficulty | Jealousy, a changed agreement, a breach, hinge pressure, time or capacity strain, a new partner, a metamour difficulty, grief, or a relationship ending | Does Joel recognise this as a real relationship concern without treating ENM itself as the diagnosis? |
| Someone considering, entering, leaving, or reconsidering non-monogamy | A partner raised opening, the person raised it themselves, an existing arrangement no longer fits, or they do not yet know what they want | Can uncertainty be explored without counselling steering toward either monogamy or non-monogamy? |
| Someone in a mono/poly difference or under relational pressure | Partners want different structures, or apparent agreement does not feel freely chosen | Will reluctance, limits, consent, power, and incompatibility be taken seriously without privileging the person who wants polyamory or the established couple? |
| Someone for whom ENM is context rather than the presenting concern | Anxiety, shame, burnout, grief, trauma, self-criticism, work, family, or another concern brought them to counselling | Can Joel understand the relationship context without making it the explanation for everything? |
| Someone comparing or screening practitioners | Previous education burden, concern about monogamous assumptions, or ordinary provider comparison | Does the page demonstrate enough knowledge and judgement to make an enquiry worth considering? |
| Someone coming alone while several relationships are involved | The difficulty includes partners or metamours who will not be in the room | What can individual counselling usefully do, and where are the limits of the service? |

Research basis: the dossier documents central, contextual, exploratory, and
unrelated presenting concerns; mixed provider experiences; mono/poly competing
goals; selective disclosure; relationship-system questions; and individual,
dyadic, and multi-partner contexts. [F03-F08, F20-F26, W01-W09]

### The page journey in the visitor's words

The following first-person phrasing is editorial shorthand for the page
sequence. It is not proposed public copy and does not claim that every visitor
thinks the same thing.

1. **The hero:** "I know what this page is for, and I do not have to translate
   my relationships into monogamous language before anything useful can
   happen."
2. **Recognition:** "Do you understand the kind of situation I may actually be
   dealing with?"
3. **Judgement and fit:** "Will you assume non-monogamy is the problem, or
   assume it must be protected from criticism?"
4. **Usefulness and format:** "What can individual counselling do when other
   relationships and people are involved?"
5. **Practical decision:** "Is this service a workable fit, and what can I do
   next?"

The page should move through those questions. A visitor should not have to
decode a therapist's conceptual framework before recognising why the section
is relevant.

## What the research changes about the page

| Research finding | Page implication | What the page must not imply |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ENM/CNM is a heterogeneous umbrella, and terminology varies. [F01-F03, F14, W10-W12] | Use several natural, recognisable terms across the page. Let the visitor's own meaning remain open. | That one glossary definition covers every identity, agreement, or relationship form. |
| ENM may be central, contextual, under exploration, or unrelated to the presenting concern. [F03, F06, W04, W05, W09] | Make this the page's main differentiating distinction after initial recognition. | That a specialist page should make non-monogamy the focus of every counselling conversation. |
| Direct client evidence reports both helpful and harmful care, including judgement, pathologising, education burden, dismissiveness, and inappropriate focus. [F03, F04] | Demonstrate familiarity through situations, language, distinctions, and service conduct. | Guaranteed safety, perfect understanding, or a claim that every inexperienced therapist is harmful. |
| Clinical and provider literature distinguishes understanding the person's goals from deciding a relationship form in advance. [F20-F26] | Explain that consent, pressure, agreements, power, feelings, and fit can be examined without a predetermined destination. | Automatic endorsement of every agreement, or monogamy as the default repair. |
| Mono/poly accounts include reports of both monogamy-default and pro-poly bias. [W01, W02, W05-W07, W09] | Make room for reluctance, a wish for monogamy, uncertainty, and incompatibility as well as a wish to continue ENM. | That the polyamorous person's account automatically outranks a monogamous partner's limits, or vice versa. |
| Jealousy can be a real concern without functioning as a verdict on ENM. [F06, W02, W06] | Name the feeling concretely and leave its meaning to be explored. | That jealousy proves non-monogamy has failed or should automatically be overcome. |
| Relationship agreements can be understood differently, change over time, or be broken. Sexual-health conversations may be recurrent and network-aware. [F31-F44] | If this is within Joel's intended scope, use agreement changes, testing, barriers, or risk tolerance as bounded examples rather than a separate educational module. | That partner count determines risk, that an agreement guarantees compliance, or that counselling replaces medical assessment. |
| Australian disclosure is selective and mixed. [F07] | Include disclosure or concealment as a possible counselling concern without assuming it is relevant to everyone. | That every ENM visitor is closeted, fearful, rejected, or obliged to disclose. |
| Stigma is associated with distress, while comparative satisfaction is mixed and null overall. [F09-F13] | Allow stigma and internalised negativity to appear as possible concerns. Do not build a defence of ENM around outcome statistics. | That ENM causes psychopathology, or that research proves one relationship structure is universally healthier. |
| The Australian search landscape uses `polyamory`, `ethical non-monogamy`, `ENM`, `open relationships`, and related terms inconsistently. Acronym-only searching was unreliable in the dated sample. [S01-S09, S16-S34] | Use full natural phrases in important identifying copy. Treat `ENM` as one useful term, not the only retrieval term. | Keyword repetition, a terminology catalogue, or `WA` used as a search shortcut where it can mean Washington State. |

## Directions considered

### 1. Therapist-method page

This direction would explain relationship systems, consent, power, formulation,
and the questions a therapist might ask. It can demonstrate knowledge, but it
places the practitioner and their framework at the centre. The rejected
"Follow the strain" candidate exposed the main failure: the section read like
a slide presented to therapists rather than a prospective client recognising
what counselling might be for.

**Decision:** Reject as the organising direction. Retain only the useful
judgement underneath it, translated into visitor-facing situations and service
conduct.

### 2. Comprehensive issue directory

This direction would catalogue opening, jealousy, agreements, hinge roles,
metamours, hierarchy, disclosure, stigma, grief, sexual health, and other
topics. It gives quick recognition and could support search language, but it
encourages an exhaustive taxonomy, equal treatment of unequal concerns, and a
generic grid of problems.

**Decision:** Use selectively in the first section for recognition, but do not
let the issue list become the page structure.

### 3. Visitor decision journey

This direction begins with recognisable situations, then answers the more
consequential fit question: what role should non-monogamy have in the work? It
then clarifies what individual counselling can and cannot do before handing the
visitor to practical information and enquiry.

**Decision:** Choose this direction. It supports recognition, judgement,
service fit, and action without converting the research dossier into public
education or a therapist-facing framework.

## Communication brief

- **Artifact register:** Specialist service page for an individual counselling
  practice.
- **Page job:** Help a prospective client decide whether Joel understands the
  concern or context they want to bring, whether his way of approaching it is
  relevant, and whether individual counselling is the right format.
- **Main message:** Non-monogamy will be understood accurately and given the
  amount of attention the presenting concern requires; it is neither the
  predetermined problem nor the predetermined answer.
- **Search intent:** Service discovery and practitioner fit for polyamory,
  open relationships, ENM/non-monogamy, counselling, therapy, Perth, online,
  and Australia. Search terms should appear where they identify the service,
  not as a section template.
- **Speaker:** Joel in the first person when describing his actions,
  judgement, boundaries, or invitations. Neutral description may be used for
  situations and practical facts.
- **Intended intellectual reading:** Joel recognises a range of ENM situations,
  can distinguish relationship structure from presenting concern, and offers
  individual counselling rather than ongoing relationship therapy.
- **Intended relational effect:** The copy earns consideration through precise
  recognition, candour, and room for the visitor's own conclusion. It does not
  grant approval, diagnose, argue, or narrate the visitor's private feelings.
- **Plausible adverse reading to prevent:** "This page is screening my
  relationship system for coercion and risk," "this is a pro-poly therapist
  who will not hear my wish for monogamy," or "this claims to work with the
  whole polycule even though the service is individual counselling."

### Copywriting forecast

**GOOD COPYWRITING:** Lead with situations a prospective client can recognise,
then give disproportionate weight to the central/context/exploration
distinction. Use a few exact terms and one or two plain practitioner judgements
instead of a catalogue of credentials or values. Let section lengths differ,
and end on practical choices rather than a summary of acceptance.

**BAD COPYWRITING:** Turn the first section into a dutiful list of every issue
found in the research, make every section equally complete, or write an
abstract account of systems, subsystems, impact, power, and therapeutic task.
Another weak execution would spend most of the page describing harms caused by
other therapists, leaving Joel's actual service underexplained.

**MODEL-DEFAULT RISK:** Generate four balanced cards, repeat polished
`not X, but Y` contrasts, add reassurance after every difficult topic, and
finish with an inspirational invitation. Prevent this by assigning one job to
each section, allowing asymmetry, using concrete examples, and omitting any
closing sentence that only restates warmth.

## Recommended page skeleton

### Hero — Existing orientation and primary action

**Visitor question:** "Am I in the right place?"

**Job:** Identify the subject, establish the basic relationship to the reader,
and offer enquiry without making the rest of the page prove the same point
again.

**Already covered:** Familiar terminology, no predetermined relationship
shape, a few example concerns, and the primary enquiry action.

**Handoff:** The first new section should move directly into recognition.

### Section 1 — Recognisable reasons this page may be relevant

**Detailed copy development:** [Section 1 conceptual work and draft
variations](enm-polyamory-section-1.md).

**Working heading direction:** Concrete and descriptive, such as `What may
bring you here`. Final wording should be drafted later.

**Visitor question:** "Do you understand the kind of situation I am in?"

**Section job:** Let visitors recognise a range of real counselling situations
without making them choose a perfect identity label or read a general lesson
about ENM.

**Content to cover:**

- raising the possibility of opening a relationship, hearing it from a
  partner, moving into ENM, reconsidering it, or wanting to close;
- jealousy, insecurity, comparison, shame, feeling left out, or a loss of
  steadiness that does not match what someone expected of themselves;
- an agreement that was unclear, understood differently, changed without a
  full conversation, or was broken;
- different wants about pace, partners, hierarchy, exclusivity, or relationship
  structure, including pressure and mono/poly incompatibility;
- hinge responsibility, time and capacity strain, metamour difficulties, grief,
  endings, or one relationship affecting another;
- disclosure, concealment, stigma, or the labour of deciding who needs to know,
  as secondary examples rather than a universal ENM story.

If sexual-health agreements are within the intended page scope, a change in
testing, barriers, or risk tolerance may appear as one concrete example. It
should not become medical advice or a standalone educational section.

**Editorial rule:** Select examples for range and recognition; do not give
every topic its own equal paragraph. The section should read as "this kind of
situation belongs here," not "these are the recognised ENM problems."

**Must not do:**

- define ENM or polyamory for the visitor;
- imply that every ENM relationship has several partners, a primary couple, or
  sex outside the relationship;
- treat jealousy, a breach, or pressure as evidence for a predetermined
  relationship outcome;
- become an exhaustive issues grid.

**Handoff to Section 2:** Recognition raises the next question: how much of the
work should actually be about non-monogamy?

**Research basis:** F03, F04, F06, F07, F31-F44, W01-W09, and the situations
table in the dossier.

### Section 2 — Decide what role non-monogamy has in the work

**Working heading direction:** A visitor-facing question or direct statement,
such as `How much of this is about non-monogamy?` Final wording should be
drafted later.

**Visitor question:** "Will you make ENM the problem, or decide in advance that
it must be defended?"

**Section job:** Establish the page's main judgement: ENM can be the subject,
relevant context, something being explored, or unrelated to the presenting
concern. The amount of attention it receives follows the concern rather than
the label.

**Content to cover:**

- when the relationship structure itself is central, including opening,
  closing, fit, agreements, or different wants;
- when ENM changes the context around grief, time, family, disclosure, stigma,
  or another relationship concern;
- when a person genuinely does not know whether non-monogamy fits them;
- when the person is coming about anxiety, burnout, trauma, shame,
  self-criticism, grief, or another concern and ENM is simply part of their
  life;
- jealousy, grief, anger, or insecurity as experiences to understand rather
  than verdicts on a relationship structure;
- consent, pressure, power, safety, and harm as legitimate questions without
  inferring them from an ENM label;
- a wish for monogamy, reluctance to open, or recognition of incompatibility as
  conclusions that must remain available, just as continuing ENM must remain
  available.

**Editorial rule:** State the distinction positively and develop it through
one or two contrasts. Do not rebuild the deleted `What ENM-aware counselling
means here` material as a list of therapist promises.

**Must not do:**

- make Joel's opposition to other therapists the section's subject;
- use `affirming`, `safe`, or `non-judgemental` as self-proving adjectives;
- promise neutrality as emotional distance or automatic approval;
- create four equal modules merely because the research names four possible
  relationships between ENM and the presenting concern.

**Handoff to Section 3:** Once the visitor understands the stance, the next
question is what individual counselling can actually do with the situation.

**Research basis:** F03-F06, F20-F26, W01-W09, especially the mixed findings on
central/contextual concerns and mono/poly goals.

### Section 3 — Individual counselling when other relationships are involved

**Working heading direction:** Plain service language, such as `Coming on your
own` or `Individual counselling with a wider relationship picture`. Final
wording should be drafted after the service boundary is confirmed.

**Visitor question:** "Can I come alone, and what can the work realistically
do if other people are not in the room?"

**Section job:** Clarify the service format and describe useful individual work
without implying that Joel assesses, represents, or treats an entire polycule
through one person's account.

**Content to cover:**

- the normal service is individual counselling;
- the person can bring the full relationship picture, including partners,
  metamours, family, and people who are not in the room;
- counselling can work with the person's own feelings, wants, limits,
  responsibilities, decisions, repeated reactions, and ways of entering or
  avoiding difficult conversations;
- relevant examples may include understanding jealousy, finding the person's
  own response beneath hinge responsibility, preparing a disclosure or
  boundary conversation, thinking after an agreement breach, grieving a
  relationship, or deciding whether a structure or relationship still fits;
- Joel's psychodynamic and attachment-informed approach can be made concrete
  here: how present reactions connect with earlier expectations, losses,
  protections, or ways of relating. The page should explain this from the
  prospective client's side rather than teach the modality;
- individual counselling cannot determine absent people's motives, negotiate
  an agreement for everyone, or substitute for ongoing couples or
  multi-partner relationship therapy.

**Service boundary requiring confirmation:** The Kink and BDSM page currently
says a partner may sometimes join an individual session for a specific purpose,
case by case, but that Vive is not an ongoing couples counselling service. Do
not transfer that wording to the ENM page until Joel confirms that the same
arrangement applies here. If ongoing relationship work is outside scope, say
when a dedicated couples or relationship counsellor may be more appropriate.

**Editorial rule:** Use observable session work and honest limits. This is the
place for Joel's approach to become tangible, not for a therapist-facing
relationship-system model.

**Must not do:**

- imply that several partners can routinely attend;
- imply that work with one person repairs the whole relationship network;
- diagnose absent partners or assign their intentions;
- repeat the entire Working with Joel page.

**Handoff to Section 4:** After understanding the format, the visitor needs a
short practical decision path.

**Research and fact basis:** Current Home source identifies `Individual
counselling`; F03, F14, F25, F26, and W08 show that individual, dyadic, and
multi-partner formats should not be collapsed; current public service facts
must govern the final boundary.

### Section 4 — Practical fit and next step

**Working heading direction:** Practical rather than inspirational, such as
`If you are considering counselling`. Final wording should be drafted later.

**Visitor question:** "Is this workable for me, and where do I go next?"

**Section job:** Give the page a useful ending by connecting subject fit to the
site's existing practical and enquiry paths.

**Content to cover:**

- individual online counselling for adults across Australia, with Joel based
  in Perth;
- a link to `Working with Joel` for the fuller account of his approach;
- a link to `Contact and fees` for the current fee, session length, consult,
  availability, and practical details;
- the enquiry action;
- a brief, accurate lowering of the contact threshold: the first message does
  not need to contain a complete account of every relationship or decision.

The Contact page currently owns the live fee and session details. Prefer
linking to that source over duplicating figures on this page unless the project
chooses and maintains one shared data source.

**Editorial rule:** End with choices, not a generic statement that reaching out
is brave, hopeful, or enough. One bottom action area is sufficient because the
hero already contains an enquiry action.

**Must not do:**

- repeat Australia-wide delivery in several headings or adjacent lines;
- invent availability or suitability;
- add a reassurance paragraph after the useful links;
- default to an FAQ if the preceding sections already answer the real
  questions.

**Fact basis:** Current Home and Contact source, current route behaviour, and
the site's enquiry flow.

## Coverage and non-duplication map

| Page part | Owns | Should not repeat |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hero | Subject identification, basic stance, a few example concerns, primary enquiry | Detailed issue coverage, therapy process, service boundary |
| Section 1 | Recognition through situations | General ENM education, therapist promises, practical details |
| Section 2 | The role ENM should have in the work and freedom from a predetermined outcome | The full issue list, modality explanation, generic claims of inclusion |
| Section 3 | Individual counselling process, page-specific approach, and service limits | A whole-system treatment model, the full Working with Joel page, couples-service claims |
| Section 4 | Practical fit, links, and next action | Another stance statement, another issue list, an inspirational conclusion |

## Material the page should leave out

- A standalone `What ENM-aware counselling means` manifesto.
- A glossary or comprehensive guide to ENM forms.
- Prevalence or relationship-satisfaction statistics used to defend ENM.
- A therapist-facing relationship-system diagram or case-formulation lecture.
- A dedicated veto or hierarchy explainer; these are bounded examples, not the
  centre of the evidence.
- A standalone sexual-health advice section unless the service scope and page
  purpose are deliberately expanded. Time-sensitive medical guidance belongs
  with appropriate health services.
- An exhaustive grid of concerns, identity labels, or relationship
  configurations.
- A generic `any reason is valid` section that could appear on every
  counselling page.
- A large FAQ assembled from questions already answered in the page body.
- Repeated claims that Joel is safe, affirming, non-judgemental, or an expert.

## Open decisions before drafting public copy

1. Is the current hero wording approved, or is only its communication job fixed?
2. Does the partner-joining arrangement described on the Kink and BDSM page
   also apply to ENM-related individual counselling?
3. Should the page explicitly state that ongoing couples and multi-partner
   therapy are outside scope, and what referral language is accurate?
4. Does Joel want sexual-health agreements, testing, barriers, or risk
   tolerance named as possible counselling topics? If so, what is the precise
   counselling-versus-medical boundary?
5. Which umbrella wording should lead after the hero: `non-monogamy`, `ethical
   non-monogamy`, `consensual non-monogamy`, or a deliberate mix? The research
   records real disagreement over these terms.
6. Should Perth appear on this page itself, or remain in shared/home/practical
   content while `across Australia` appears once near the final service facts?
7. Should the bottom practical section state the current fee and session length
   directly, or keep Contact as the single visible source of those details?

## Drafting sequence

1. Confirm the open service and terminology decisions above.
2. Draft Section 1 first and test it for recognition across the evidence-based
   reader positions. Remove any topic included only for completeness.
3. Draft Section 2 as the page's central judgement. Test it cold with a current
   ENM reader, an uncertain reader, a monogamous partner in a mono/poly
   difference, and a visitor whose presenting concern is unrelated to ENM.
4. Draft Section 3 from the individual client's side. Check every sentence for
   an accurate service action, useful example, or limit.
5. Draft the practical ending from current source facts and existing links.
6. Reread the page from the hero downward. Remove repeated claims about
   acceptance, familiarity, and predetermined outcomes.
7. Align the final title, metadata, H1/hero, headings, links, and natural search
   language. The route is currently a `noindex, nofollow` draft, but eventual
   indexing still requires coherent subject identification.
8. Run the grounded visitor-reading, speaker, deletion, model-glue, inclusion,
   claim, and read-aloud checks before implementation.

## Acceptance checks for the finished copy

A cold reader should be able to answer:

- Is this page about individual counselling for ENM, polyamory, open
  relationships, and related non-monogamy contexts?
- Can I recognise at least one situation that resembles why I came?
- Do I understand that Joel will not automatically blame or defend ENM?
- Can uncertainty, a wish for monogamy, or mono/poly incompatibility be heard
  without being treated as failure?
- Can ENM remain background context if I am coming about something else?
- Do I know what individual counselling may work with when other people are
  involved?
- Do I understand the limit between individual counselling and ongoing
  relationship therapy?
- Can I find Joel's approach, current practical details, and the enquiry path?

The copy should also pass these adversarial checks:

- An established ENM reader is not treated as a risk system to be assessed.
- A monogamous or reluctant partner is not treated as an obstacle to a more
  enlightened relationship structure.
- Jealousy, grief, pressure, breach, stigma, and sexual-health concerns are not
  treated as universal ENM experiences.
- Specialist fluency comes from distinctions and examples, not self-awarded
  qualities.
- Each section performs one clear job and changes the visitor's understanding.
- The page does not read like training material for therapists.
