# LGBTQIA+ counselling page — Section 1 copy development

**Status:** Working copy exploration; not approved public copy  
**Page:** LGBTQIA+ counselling  
**Section:** `Recognisable reasons this page may be relevant`  
**Stable section ID:** `recognition`  
**Related plan:** [LGBTQIA+ counselling page plan](lgbtqia-page.md)

## Scope of this document

This document develops the first section after the planned hero. It records the
section's conceptual choices, tests three structurally different copy
directions, and recommends one working draft for later full-page assembly.

It is a copy-development document, not a layout specification. Headings,
paragraphs, and lists below indicate reading structure only. They do not
prescribe cards, columns, icons, or another interface treatment.

The current implemented LGBTQIA+ page remains outside the wording authority for
this work. The related page plan starts from a blank route, and the public hero
has not yet been drafted. This section therefore follows the hero's planned
communication job rather than its current source wording.

## Decision in brief

Section 1 should move through a few recognisable reasons someone might be
looking now:

- sexuality or gender has become an active question or decision;
- another person's response has changed a relationship or someone's place in
  family, faith, or community; and
- the person wants counselling for an ordinary concern that may have little to
  do with being LGBTQIA+.

These should read as one developing piece of prose, not three named categories
or equal modules. The strongest direction begins with a personal question,
moves out to other people's responses, and then widens to concerns for which
identity is not the presenting problem.

That movement gives different visitors a point of recognition without making
the section an identity directory. It also leaves Section 2 with a distinct
job: demonstrating what useful familiarity changes in the conversation.

## The planned hero and what it should already establish

The page plan starts from a blank page, so there is no approved hero copy to
quote. For this section work, the planned hero is assumed to have already:

- identified the page as LGBTQIA+ counselling for adults;
- identified Joel and the individual-counselling service;
- established that sexuality, gender, sex characteristics, identity, or
  questioning can be relevant without becoming an automatic diagnosis; and
- provided the first enquiry path.

Section 1 should not repeat the service label, define the acronym, restate that
the practice is affirming, or explain the whole page's stance. It needs to make
the hero's broad relevance concrete.

Once the hero is drafted, the section must be reread beneath the actual wording.
Any repeated main judgement should be removed during full-page assembly.

## The visitor arriving at this section

The visitor has identified the service but may still be asking:

> Do you understand anything close to the situation that made me look for
> counselling now?

Several evidence-supported positions need to remain visible:

1. Someone finding language for sexuality or gender, considering coming out,
   or revisiting something they thought was settled.
2. Someone secure in their identity but facing a decision, loss, conflict, or
   another person's response.
3. Someone whose relationship, family, faith, work, community, or healthcare
   context has made the current difficulty harder.
4. Someone seeking help with anxiety, grief, trauma, burnout, or a relationship
   concern that is not primarily about identity.
5. Someone comparing practitioners after a previous experience of avoidance,
   over-focus, invalidation, or education burden.
6. Someone who does not use the page's umbrella term neatly, including a person
   with an intersex variation who may not identify as LGBTQIA+.

These are positions from which to test the section, not six lanes to reproduce
in public copy.

### Visitor-hat reading

> I know this page is meant to be relevant to people in lives like mine. I still
> need to see whether the page recognises why I might be looking now. I do not
> want to choose from an identity catalogue or prove that my concern is
> LGBTQIA+-specific before the page includes me. After this section, I need a
> reason to believe the claimed familiarity will reduce misunderstanding rather
> than add another topic I have to manage.

This is a planning hypothesis grounded in the page plan and dossier, not a
claim about every visitor's private response.

## The section's communication job

### Primary job

Turn a broad specialist-service label into a small number of credible reasons a
person may be looking for counselling now.

### Main message

The page is relevant to people dealing directly with sexuality or gender,
people dealing with what happened around it, and people whose reason for
counselling may be something else.

This message should be communicated through situations. It should not be
published as a three-part framework.

### Intended intellectual reading

The visitor can reasonably understand that this counselling is not only for
coming out, questioning, or identity-related distress. Stable identity,
external conflict, and ordinary counselling concerns remain within the page's
view.

### Intended relational reading

The copy recognises possible situations without assigning the visitor a label,
outness status, conflict, diagnosis, or desired outcome. The visitor is not
asked to identify with an entire subgroup before a situation can be relevant.

### What the section should make possible next

Recognition raises a credibility question:

> If my situation is included, what would I have to explain or correct before
> we could talk about it properly?

Section 2 owns that question. Section 1 should not answer it by listing Joel's
knowledge, conduct, terminology, or promises.

## Editorial choices

### 1. Situations rather than identity categories

The research contains distinct findings for gay, lesbian, bisexual,
pansexual, trans, nonbinary, asexual, aromantic, intersex, queer, questioning,
and intersectional populations. Converting those populations into equal public
paragraphs would flatten their differences and make the visitor navigate a
service taxonomy.

The section instead uses situations such as:

- finding words or returning to an old question;
- dealing with another person's response;
- losing ease or belonging in an important relationship or community; and
- seeking counselling for a concern that is not about identity.

These situations can be recognised across different identities without
claiming they mean the same thing for everyone.

### 2. Movement rather than a balanced three-part explainer

The page plan distinguishes identity as subject, context, or beside the
presenting concern. Repeating those labels here would turn a useful planning
judgement into visible architecture and would pre-empt Section 3.

The recommended movement is more ordinary:

1. a question or decision is active;
2. other people's responses have changed the situation; and
3. the reason for counselling may be something else.

The third movement deliberately receives the shortest explanation. It needs
recognition, not another general mental-health section that duplicates Home.

### 3. Established identity remains visible

An opening about finding language or coming out can quietly imply that every
visitor is questioning or newly out. A second sentence needs to keep someone
who knows exactly who they are in view while allowing a current decision to be
unsettled.

### 4. Family, faith, and community are selected carefully

The evidence supports rejection, belonging, disclosure, cultural context,
religious conflict, and conversion-practice harm as distinct situations. The
section should not collapse them into one story in which faith, family, or
community is automatically hostile.

The recommended draft names difficulty remaining connected after other people
respond. It does not explain why, assign blame, or imply that separation is the
preferred outcome.

### 5. The section will not use unconfirmed specialist scope to prove breadth

Transition, gender assessment, conversion-practice recovery, intersex medical
history, and asexual or aromantic concerns all appear in the evidence. Publicly
naming them as reasons to choose this service can imply a more specific scope
than has been confirmed.

The candidates test some broader material, but the recommended draft does not
claim medical, diagnostic, report-writing, sex-therapy, conversion-recovery, or
transition-specific services. Exact breadth remains an owner decision before
approval.

### 6. Ordinary language with selective umbrella language

The section uses `sexuality`, `gender`, `coming out`, and `LGBTQIA+` because
they are recognisable in this page context. It does not attempt to complete the
acronym in body copy or teach terminology. Section 2 can demonstrate more
precise fluency once the owner confirms the intended competence and wording
scope.

## Content priority

### Essential in this section

- A direct identity question or decision, expressed without making uncertainty
  universal.
- Clear room for a visitor who is established in their identity.
- One example of another person's response changing an important part of life.
- Family, faith, or community as possible context without treating any of them
  as inherently hostile.
- A short recognition of ordinary counselling concerns for which being
  LGBTQIA+ is not the presenting problem.

### Useful if it fits naturally

- Later-life recognition.
- Coming out or deciding what to do after disclosure.
- Relationship loss or changed belonging.
- Work, discrimination, body, or healthcare context.
- Previous counselling as a reason that provider fit has become especially
  important.

### Better held elsewhere or left implicit

- A full list of identity labels or subgroup-specific concerns.
- Pronouns, relationship terms, bisexual erasure, asexual pathologisation, and
  intersex distinctions; these are candidate material for Section 2.
- The full subject/context/background judgement; Section 3 owns it.
- Joel's counselling process or psychodynamic and attachment-informed approach;
  Section 4 owns it.
- Practical service facts and enquiry detail; Section 5 owns them.
- Provider-harm examples used mainly to argue that other counselling is unsafe.

## Forecast before drafting

### GOOD outcome

The section moves through a few real reasons for looking without turning them
into a service menu. Sentence lengths vary with the material, the established
LGBTQIA+ visitor remains visible alongside someone questioning, and the final
paragraph gives ordinary concerns enough room without restarting Home. The
prose sounds like a counsellor speaking plainly rather than a policy statement
or community explainer.

### BAD outcome

The section becomes a polite catalogue of coming out, transition, family,
faith, discrimination, relationships, anxiety, and trauma. Each source category
receives equal treatment, so the writing has coverage but no emphasis. Another
weak version would make every example a crisis and inadvertently portray
LGBTQIA+ life as a sequence of rejection and distress.

### MODEL-default outcome to resist

The copy opens with `whether you are questioning or confidently living as your
authentic self`, creates three balanced paragraphs for identity, context, and
mental health, and closes with a reassurance that every part of the visitor is
welcome. The section sounds inclusive but interchangeable because the
composition regularises every reader position and supplies a tidy emotional
conclusion.

Likely model-glue structures to challenge include:

- repeated `you may` or `whether ... or ...` openings;
- equal lists of identity, relationship, family, faith, work, and mental-health
  topics;
- `your identity is only one part of your story`;
- `there is no right way to feel`;
- `you do not need to have it all figured out`;
- `a safe and affirming space`; and
- a final invitation to `be your authentic self`.

The underlying ideas may be relevant, but the public copy should earn them
through selection and detail rather than publish the familiar formulation.

## Angle exploration

Three structurally different directions were developed. They keep the section
job fixed while changing the entry point, movement, and amount of scanning.

### Angle A — From a personal question to the world around it

**Organising idea:** Begin with sexuality or gender becoming active, widen to
another person's response, then make room for an unrelated presenting concern.

**Strength:** Creates forward movement without naming the planning framework.
It includes questioning and established visitors and reads as one section
rather than three service categories.

**Risk:** Family, faith, and community can make the middle paragraph feel more
conflict-heavy than the rest. The draft needs to name the possibility without
making rejection the universal LGBTQIA+ story.

### Angle B — Begin with the reason that is not about identity

**Organising idea:** Lead with anxiety, grief, burnout, or relationship strain,
then turn towards occasions when sexuality or gender is central or inseparable
from the concern.

**Strength:** Immediately corrects the assumption that someone needs an
`LGBTQIA+ problem` to use the service. It gives the ordinary-concern visitor
unusually strong visibility.

**Risk:** It restates the page's central subject/context judgement before the
reader has encountered much concrete material. The balanced turn from
unrelated, to central, to intertwined can also become the exact three-part
explainer the plan warns against.

### Angle C — Direct recognition statements

**Organising idea:** Present short, self-contained situations for rapid
scanning, including previous counselling as a reason to compare providers.

**Strength:** Makes the breadth immediately retrievable and gives a visitor
several direct entry points.

**Risk:** The list can become an identity-and-issues grid, especially when
implemented visually. Equal statement weight also makes unlike situations look
interchangeable and encourages token coverage.

## Candidate A — Question, response, then another reason

### Draft

#### What might bring you here

Maybe you're finding words for your sexuality or gender, thinking about coming
out, or looking again at something you thought you'd settled years ago. You
might also know exactly who you are and be trying to work out what to do next.

Sometimes the difficult part is how other people have responded. An important
relationship has changed, or family, faith or community is now harder to remain
part of.

Or you're looking for counselling because you're anxious, grieving, burnt out
or having trouble in a relationship. Being LGBTQIA+ may have very little to do
with it.

### What this draft is doing

- The first paragraph includes current questioning and later-life recognition
  without treating uncertainty as universal.
- `You might also know exactly who you are` keeps an established visitor from
  being cast as the settled opposite of someone who needs counselling.
- The second paragraph shifts from self-understanding to consequences in
  relationships and belonging. It stays short so conflict does not become the
  page's main portrait of LGBTQIA+ life.
- Family, faith, and community are named as places that may become difficult to
  remain part of, not as institutions the page automatically condemns.
- The final paragraph gives ordinary counselling concerns a clear place without
  expanding them into a duplicate of Home.
- The draft stops on the useful distinction. It does not add a reassurance or
  explain what Joel will do; the next section owns that credibility question.

### Cold visitor reading

**Immediate:** The section recognises several moments in which counselling may
become relevant. It does not require the visitor to select an identity category.

**Intellectual:** Counselling may be relevant while someone is finding language,
after other people respond, or for a concern that has little to do with being
LGBTQIA+.

**Relational:** The copy leaves room for uncertainty and certainty. It does not
tell the visitor what coming out, connection to faith or community, or an
important relationship should become.

**Sceptical or adverse:** The middle paragraph may still make rejection and
threatened belonging feel more representative than they are. A person with an
intersex variation, or an asexual or aromantic visitor, may not find an explicit
point of recognition in this section. That breadth should not be solved with a
token list; it remains a scope and later-section question.

### Main revision pressure

Once the hero is drafted, check whether the final sentence repeats the hero's
main judgement. If it does, keep the ordinary-concern example and remove or
change only the repeated conclusion.

## Candidate B — The ordinary concern first

### Draft

#### It may not be why you came

You may have come to this page because you're anxious, grieving, burnt out or
having trouble in a relationship. Being LGBTQIA+ is part of your life, but it
isn't the problem you came to solve.

Sexuality or gender may instead be exactly what you want to talk about: finding
language, thinking about coming out, or dealing with another person's response.

Sometimes the two are tied together. A family problem can also be about coming
out. Grief can include a community you lost. Anxiety at work can be bound up
with what colleagues know.

### What this draft is doing

- It gives the visitor with a non-identity presenting concern the strongest
  possible opening position.
- It turns towards direct identity work rather than treating it as secondary.
- The final paragraph uses three paired examples to show why `identity` and
  `ordinary concern` do not always separate cleanly.

### Cold visitor reading

**Immediate:** This page is not only for identity questions or crises.

**Intellectual:** Identity may be unrelated, central, or woven into another
problem. The examples make that distinction easy to understand.

**Relational:** A visitor who does not want identity to dominate is addressed
early. Someone looking specifically for identity-related work has to wait until
the second paragraph.

**Sceptical or adverse:** The draft sounds more like a conceptual explanation
than recognition. Its three movements are conspicuously balanced, and the final
paragraph risks turning individual lives into examples that prove the model.

### Main revision pressure

The opening performs work the planned hero and Section 3 may already own. It
would need full-page testing before it could justify leading Section 1.

## Candidate C — Direct situations

### Draft

#### Is any of this close?

- You are finding language for your sexuality or gender, or looking again at a
  label that used to fit.
- You know who you are, but an important relationship has changed.
- You are deciding whether to come out, or dealing with what happened when you
  did.
- Family, faith, or community has become harder to remain part of.
- You want counselling for anxiety, grief, trauma, burnout, or something else
  and do not want being LGBTQIA+ treated as the cause.
- A previous counsellor avoided identity, focused on it too heavily, or left
  you doing the basic explaining.

### What this draft is doing

- It maximises fast retrieval.
- Each line is a situation or decision rather than an identity label.
- It gives previous counselling an explicit place without making that history
  the opening argument.
- The list can be shortened without damaging a prose movement because each item
  is independent.

### Cold visitor reading

**Immediate:** It is easy to scan for a close situation.

**Intellectual:** The page's breadth is visible, including uncertainty,
established identity, external response, ordinary concerns, and provider fit.

**Relational:** `Is any of this close?` allows approximation rather than asking
the visitor to identify with a fixed category.

**Sceptical or adverse:** The equal statements still read like a checklist.
Placed into cards or tiles, they could become the identity-and-issues grid the
page plan rejects. The final item also starts to define the page against other
therapists before Joel has demonstrated his own practice.

### Main revision pressure

This direction would require ruthless selection and a non-modular presentation.
Its main advantage is retrieval, but that does not outweigh the loss of movement
for this section.

## Recommended direction

Candidate A is the strongest basis for Section 1.

It moves through three materially different reasons for looking without naming
them as a framework. The section begins close to a personal question, widens to
relationships and belonging, then makes room for a concern that may have little
to do with identity. That is enough range for recognition without converting
the dossier into public coverage.

Candidate B contains an important emphasis on ordinary concerns, but that idea
already appears in Candidate A's final paragraph without taking over the
section. Candidate C is useful as an inventory for later acceptance testing;
its previous-counselling line should not be imported because it would pull
Section 1 towards Section 2's credibility problem.

## Recommended working draft

### What might bring you here

Maybe you're finding words for your sexuality or gender, thinking about coming
out, or looking again at something you thought you'd settled years ago. You
might also know exactly who you are and be trying to work out what to do next.

Sometimes the difficult part is how other people have responded. An important
relationship has changed, or family, faith or community is now harder to remain
part of.

Or you're looking for counselling because you're anxious, grieving, burnt out
or having trouble in a relationship. Being LGBTQIA+ may have very little to do
with it.

## Sentence-function check

| Copy movement | Function | Why it remains |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Finding words, coming out, or revisiting an old understanding | Recognises an active identity question across different points in life | Gives the section a concrete opening without making one identity label the entry requirement |
| Knowing exactly who you are while deciding what to do | Distinguishes identity certainty from situational certainty | Prevents the opening from implying that established LGBTQIA+ adults have no reason for identity-relevant counselling |
| Other people's response | Turns from internal question to interpersonal consequence | Adds a materially different route into counselling rather than another identity topic |
| Changed relationship or harder belonging | Makes family, faith, and community context concrete | Shows what an external response can alter without prescribing separation or reconciliation |
| Anxiety, grief, burnout, or relationship trouble | Recognises ordinary presenting concerns | Keeps the specialist page relevant when identity is not the reason for counselling |
| `may have very little to do with it` | Limits the role assigned to identity | Stops the ordinary-concern paragraph from quietly turning into another identity explanation |

Each paragraph changes the kind of situation in view. No paragraph exists only
to reassure, summarise, or connect two stronger passages.

## Model-glue and deletion pass

### Structures deliberately avoided

- No `whether you are ... or ...` umbrella catalogue.
- No `safe`, `affirming`, `inclusive`, or `non-judgemental` self-description.
- No claim that the visitor can bring their `whole` or `authentic` self.
- No assurance that they do not need everything figured out.
- No list completing every identity or research category.
- No final sentence saying that every reason is valid or welcome.
- No explanation of why the section matters after the situations have already
  shown its relevance.

### Remaining model-default risks

- The first paragraph uses `Maybe` and `might`. Both mark genuinely different
  visitor possibilities, but the full page should be checked for modal
  accumulation once the hero exists.
- The first and final paragraphs contain short lists. Each list is bounded to a
  distinct job: identity-related moments in the first and ordinary presenting
  concerns in the last. If the page later feels list-heavy, delete an example
  rather than replacing the list with a broader abstraction.
- `Being LGBTQIA+ may have very little to do with it` is a contrast. It remains
  because the contrast corrects a live assumption and ends the paragraph more
  directly than a general statement about identity as context.

### Deletion test

- Removing the first sentence loses questioning, coming out, and later-life
  recognition.
- Removing the second sentence makes the section quietly equate LGBTQIA+
  counselling with uncertainty about identity.
- Removing the middle paragraph loses other people's responses and the effect
  on relationships, faith, family, and community.
- Removing the final paragraph makes the page appear to require an
  identity-related reason for counselling.
- Adding a summary after the final line does not change understanding and should
  be resisted.

## Inclusion and adverse-reading check

### Established gay, lesbian, bisexual, pansexual, or queer visitor

`You might also know exactly who you are` prevents questioning from becoming
the default. The copy does not define orientation through a current partner or
relationship history.

### Questioning or later-life visitor

The opening allows new language and revisiting an old understanding without
requiring a conclusion or presenting later recognition as a revelation story.

### Trans or nonbinary visitor

Gender is named without presuming dysphoria, transition, medical treatment, or
a wish for gender-specific counselling. Transition-specific scope remains an
owner decision.

### Asexual or aromantic visitor

The recommended draft does not explicitly name asexuality, aromantic identity,
desire, or relationship assumptions. That omission avoids token coverage but
places responsibility on Section 2 to demonstrate relevant fluency if the
owner confirms this scope.

### Person with an intersex variation

The draft does not imply that intersex is a sexuality or gender identity and
does not make a medical-history service claim. It also does not provide direct
recognition. The final page's umbrella wording and Section 2 content must be
resolved without presuming LGBTQIA+ identification.

### Visitor connected to faith, culture, family, or community

The draft says connection may become harder after other people respond. It does
not treat faith, culture, family, or community as automatically hostile and
does not rank separation, reconciliation, disclosure, or concealment as the
healthy outcome.

### Visitor whose concern is not identity-related

The final paragraph gives this position a direct statement rather than a vague
`all concerns welcome` assurance. It stays short enough not to duplicate Home.

### Visitor wary after previous counselling

This visitor is not named in the recommended draft. The next section directly
addresses the translation and correction burden, so importing the provider-harm
line here would duplicate that question and make other therapists the page's
subject.

## Research grounding and claim limits

The section draws on the dossier's findings that:

- general and identity-related reasons for counselling can coexist, and
  identity is not always the presenting concern [F04, F06];
- questioning, coming out, later-life recognition, and established identity
  all appear in the evidence [F04, F08, W02, W03, W05, W18];
- family, relationships, faith, community, rejection, discrimination, and
  belonging are distinct possible contexts [F02, F03, F11, F13, F14,
  W14-W17]; and
- anxiety, grief, trauma, burnout, work, and relationship concerns can be the
  reason for counselling while sexuality or gender remains background or
  context [F04, F06, W01, W05, W07, W11, W17].

No prevalence statistic or claim about what is `common`, `typical`, or
universal appears in the draft. The research supports these as represented
situations, not as a distribution of reasons among all LGBTQIA+ clients.

The draft does not claim a specific competence, lived identity, service,
assessment, letter, diagnosis, medical role, or outcome. `LGBTQIA+` remains the
page plan's working umbrella term, not approved final scope wording.

## Handoff into Section 2

The recommended draft ends with a visitor whose reason for counselling may have
very little to do with being LGBTQIA+. That creates a practical credibility
question without stating it:

> Can Joel understand the life around the concern without making me explain or
> correct the basics first?

Section 2 should answer through a few precise distinctions and observable
language choices. It should not repeat the three reasons in Section 1 or begin
with another statement that identity can be central, contextual, or unrelated.

## Read-aloud pass

The recommended draft was checked without its editorial annotations.

- The paragraph openings vary: `Maybe`, `Sometimes`, and `Or`.
- The prose uses contractions and ordinary Australian English.
- No sentence performs a sales cadence or inspirational close.
- The longest sentence is the opening list; the following sentence and middle
  paragraph slow the pace.
- The phrase `harder to remain part of` is deliberately plain. It preserves
  continuing connection as possible rather than assuming departure.
- The last sentence stops without explaining, validating, or inviting.

## Open questions for full-page assembly

1. Does the eventual hero already say that identity may not be the reason for
   counselling? If so, which line should own that judgement?
2. Is `What might bring you here` too close to the ENM page heading, or is the
   shared descriptive language useful across two otherwise different sections?
3. Does the owner want transition, body, medical history, conversion-practice
   harm, asexuality, aromantic identity, or intersex variation named on this
   page? Confirm competence and scope before importing those examples.
4. Should previous counselling appear in Section 1 at all, or is the Section 2
   translation-and-correction question sufficient?
5. Does the final page lead with `LGBTQIA+`, `LGBTQ+`, `queer`, or a deliberate
   mix? Revisit the final sentence after that decision.
6. Once Section 2 is drafted, does the last sentence create a useful handoff or
   merely repeat the next heading?
