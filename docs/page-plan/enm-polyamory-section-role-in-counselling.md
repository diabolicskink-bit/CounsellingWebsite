# ENM and polyamory page — Section 2 copy development

**Status:** Working copy exploration, not approved public copy  
**Page:** ENM and polyamory counselling  
**Section:** `Decide what role non-monogamy has in the work`  
**Stable section ID:** `role-in-counselling`  
**Related plan:** [ENM and polyamory page plan](enm-polyamory-page.md)

## Scope of this document

This document develops the second section after the hero. It recovers the
section's place in the visitor journey, makes the main editorial decisions,
tests three structurally different copy directions, and recommends one working
draft for further development.

It is a copy-development document, not a layout or component specification.
The headings and paragraph breaks below indicate reading structure only.

## Decision in brief

Section 2 should be organised around **how much relevance non-monogamy has to
the reason for counselling**.

Section 1 has already shown what it looks like when non-monogamy is central to
the reason for counselling. Section 2 should not open with more examples of
opening, closing, agreements, or incompatible wants.

The refined section has two copy movements:

1. a short continuation covering what Section 1 has not yet shown:
   non-monogamy may change the context around another concern, or may have
   little to do with why the person came; and
2. a separate position block carrying the main practitioner judgement:
   neither monogamy nor non-monogamy is treated as the better outcome, and Joel
   will not steer the work towards either one.

A wish for monogamy, a wish to continue non-monogamy, uncertainty, difficult
feelings, and genuine incompatibility must all remain possible without one
being treated as the more enlightened answer.

## Where the visitor is in the page

### What the hero has already established

The current hero tells the visitor that:

- the page is about counselling for polyamory, open relationships, and other
  forms of ethical non-monogamy;
- familiar language can be used without a basic terminology lesson;
- Joel starts with what is happening rather than how a relationship should
  look; and
- jealousy, agreements, pressure, and uncertainty can be discussed without
  assuming the structure explains everything.

Section 2 should not repeat that as a general promise. It needs to show the
practical judgement behind it.

### What Section 1 has added

The implemented Section 1 currently covers:

- considering opening or hearing the idea from a partner before knowing what
  to think or how much room there is to say it;
- jealousy, differently understood agreements, events outside an agreement,
  breakups, new relationships, and changes in available attention;
- carrying messages and reactions between relationships while postponing an
  individual response; and
- caring about someone whose idea of a workable relationship does not match
  one's own.

The earlier Section 1 workthrough recommended a final line about what happened,
what the person wants, and whether the way of relating still fits. That line is
not in the implemented section. Section 2 should therefore create its own clean
entry rather than relying on an unpublished handoff. It should still assume
the visitor has just read the implemented situations and avoid restating them.

### Visitor hat at this point

The live question is now:

> You recognise situations like mine. But what will you make of them? Will my
> relationship structure become the explanation, or will you avoid difficult
> questions because you think non-monogamy has to be defended?

That first-person phrasing is editorial shorthand, not a claim that every
visitor thinks the same thing.

Relevant visitor positions include:

| Visitor position | One-line reading at this point |
| --- | --- |
| Someone in established ENM with a relationship difficulty | `I need the actual problem taken seriously without ENM being treated as the diagnosis.` |
| Someone unsure whether non-monogamy fits | `I do not want counselling to confirm either the answer I fear or the answer I think I should want.` |
| Someone who wants monogamy or is reluctant to open | `I need my limit heard without being cast as resistance, insecurity, or lack of growth.` |
| Someone who wants to remain non-monogamous | `I need difficult feelings or a bad agreement examined without monogamy becoming the automatic repair.` |
| Someone coming about another concern | `I need my relationships understood accurately without them taking over counselling that is about anxiety, grief, shame, trauma, burnout, or something else.` |

## The section's communication job

### Primary job

Show that the amount of attention given to non-monogamy follows the presenting
concern rather than the relationship label.

### Main message

Non-monogamy can be relevant context without becoming the subject or assumed
cause of counselling. Neither monogamy nor non-monogamy is the preferred
outcome, and Joel will not steer the work towards either one.

### Intended intellectual reading

The visitor should reasonably understand that Joel can:

- recognise from Section 1 when non-monogamy is already central without
  listing those situations again;
- account for a wider relationship context when it materially changes grief,
  time, family, disclosure, or another concern;
- leave ENM in the background when it is not the reason for counselling; and
- examine feelings, consent, power, harm, or incompatibility without treating
  either monogamy or non-monogamy as the direction counselling should take.

### Intended relational effect

The language should give the visitor room to judge Joel's fit. It should not
grant approval, defend ENM to them, argue with monogamy, or imply that Joel is
screening their relationships for hidden pathology.

### What this section must make possible next

Once the visitor understands the stance, Section 3 can answer a practical
service question:

> What can individual counselling actually do when several relationships and
> people are involved, but only one person is in the room?

Section 2 should not begin explaining the individual-counselling format or its
limits. It only needs to make that next question worth asking.

## Editorial choices

### 1. Relevance rather than therapist identity

The deleted `What ENM-aware counselling means here` direction centred the
therapist's promises and professional positioning. This section should instead
help the visitor locate their reason for coming.

The distinction is not:

> Joel is ENM-aware because he holds these principles.

It is:

> This may be the main thing I need to discuss, part of the context, or not the
> issue at all.

That is more useful to a visitor and less likely to read as a presentation to
other therapists.

### 2. Continue from Section 1 rather than restarting recognition

The research and page plan distinguish central, contextual, exploratory, and
unrelated concerns. Those are useful editorial categories, but they should not
become four equal cards or four definitions.

Section 1 already gives concrete form to central ENM concerns. Repeating
opening, closing, agreements, and different relationship wants at the start of
Section 2 makes the page feel as though it has restarted.

The first copy block should therefore begin with the positions the visitor has
not yet seen: ENM changing the context around another concern, and ENM having
little to do with the concern at all.

Exploration is best treated as a position within the copy rather than as a
fourth category. Someone can be uncertain whether non-monogamy fits while it is
central to the reason they came.

### 3. Give the practitioner position its own block

The section needs to name what Joel does, not primarily what other therapists
get wrong. Reported harms and provider bias inform the copy, but they should not
become its subject.

The strongest material is not a secondary conclusion to the context paragraph.
It should stand as a separate copy block within the same section:

> Neither monogamy nor non-monogamy is treated as the better outcome. Joel will
> not steer the work towards either one.

This is a content grouping decision, not a visual specification. The later
design can decide how the distinction is expressed without changing the copy
hierarchy.

### 4. Make both directional biases visible

The evidence includes reports of monogamy-default care and reports that
poly-friendly therapists privileged a polyamorous partner or treated
monogamy as a lesser conclusion.

The copy should therefore preserve all of the following as possible:

- continuing non-monogamy;
- changing agreements or pace;
- wanting monogamy;
- deciding not to open;
- recognising incompatibility;
- remaining uncertain; or
- discovering that no relationship-structure decision is required.

The section does not need to publish this as a complete outcomes list. It needs
enough range that neither pro-monogamy nor pro-poly bias can be inferred from
the wording.

### 5. Feelings require context, not a verdict

Jealousy, anger, grief, relief, and insecurity can be consequential without
settling whether non-monogamy is right or wrong for a person.

This section should make that distinction once. It should not define every
possible meaning of a feeling or turn the visitor into a case formulation.

### 6. Consent, power, safety, and harm remain ordinary questions

Avoiding monogamous assumptions does not mean avoiding difficult questions.
Consent, pressure, power, safety, harm, and agreements can be addressed directly
when they are present.

They should not be presented as a checklist automatically triggered by an ENM
label. A long list here would recreate the therapist-facing tone the page plan
rejects.

## Content priority

### Essential

- ENM as relevant context and as background, without repeating Section 1's
  central situations.
- Genuine uncertainty about whether non-monogamy fits.
- A separate position block stating that neither monogamy nor non-monogamy is
  treated as the better outcome and Joel will not steer the work towards
  either one.
- Difficult feelings not functioning as automatic votes on relationship
  structure.

### Useful if it fits naturally

- A concrete contextual example such as a breakup affecting more than one
  relationship.
- Family or disclosure decisions involving people who are not in the room.
- A brief indication that consent, pressure, or harm can be named directly.

### Better held elsewhere or left implicit

- A list of harms caused by other therapists.
- A four-part `central / contextual / exploratory / unrelated` explainer.
- A definition of neutral, affirming, ENM-aware, or non-judgemental practice.
- The individual-counselling process and service boundary; Section 3 owns it.
- General education about relationship structures.
- A complete list of possible relationship outcomes.
- Research statistics used to defend ENM or prove one structure is healthier.

## Forecast before drafting

### GOOD outcome

The section trusts Section 1 to own central ENM situations. It moves directly
into context and background, then changes weight: a separate, firmer block
states that neither monogamy nor non-monogamy is the preferred outcome and Joel
will not steer the work towards either. The shift in copy weight follows the
importance of the judgement rather than creating balanced categories.

### BAD outcome

The copy becomes a shapeless statement of neutrality, repeats the hero's claim
that ENM is not the problem, or spends most of its space criticising other
therapists. Another weak execution would list every possible concern and
outcome until the central distinction disappears.

### MODEL-default outcome to resist

A fluent draft is likely to create four balanced categories, use repeated
`sometimes ... sometimes ...` constructions, follow every difficult point with
reassurance, and close with a polished `wherever you are in your journey`
invitation.

The structural prevention is to use one short context/background movement,
separate the practitioner position into its own block, and end on the last
useful distinction rather than a summary.

Likely model glue to challenge includes:

- `Every relationship is unique.`
- `There is no right or wrong answer.`
- `Wherever you are on your journey ...`
- `Together, we can explore ...`
- `Your feelings are valid.`
- `This means the focus stays on you.`
- `A safe and affirming space ...`
- repeated `whether ... or ...` catalogues.

## Angle exploration

### Angle A — The focus follows the reason for counselling

**Organising idea:** Trust Section 1 to establish ENM as the main question.
Move into ENM changing the context or being incidental, then give the
neither-monogamy-nor-non-monogamy position its own block.

**Strength:** It performs the exact job assigned by the page plan, includes the
visitor whose concern is unrelated, and demonstrates Joel's stance through
application rather than a values manifesto.

**Main risk:** The first copy block may become too compressed or abstract after
the overlapping examples are removed. One or two contextual examples need to
keep the distinction concrete.

### Angle B — Start with what the visitor is trying to decide

**Organising idea:** Organise the section around different live decisions:
whether ENM fits, whether a particular agreement or relationship fits, or
whether the relationship structure is relevant at all.

**Strength:** Keeps the visitor's agency and question at the centre. It gives
uncertainty more weight and naturally preserves several possible outcomes.

**Main risk:** A sequence of decisions can sound like an intake triage tool. It
also risks moving too quickly into the possible conclusions instead of first
clarifying the section's main central/context distinction.

### Angle C — A feeling does not decide the relationship

**Organising idea:** Use jealousy, relief, anger, or insecurity to separate a
felt experience from a verdict about relationship structure, then widen to the
role ENM may have in the work.

**Strength:** Gives the section a specific, memorable point of entry and
answers a consequential concern for both established ENM visitors and reluctant
partners.

**Main risk:** It repeats material already present in the hero and Section 1.
It also narrows a broader section around feelings and may sound like Joel is
interpreting those feelings before hearing the person.

## Candidate A — Relevance-led

### Draft

#### How much of this is about non-monogamy?

**Main copy block**

Your relationships do not have to become the subject of counselling just
because they are non-monogamous. They may matter to what is happening when
decisions about family, disclosure or support involve more than one person. Or
you may be coming about anxiety, burnout, shame, grief or something else, with
non-monogamy simply part of the wider picture.

**Separate position block**

Neither monogamy nor non-monogamy is treated as the better outcome. I will not
steer the work towards either one.

A wish for monogamy is not something to work through until it disappears.
Wanting to continue non-monogamy does not make every agreement workable.
Jealousy, anger or insecurity need their own context; none of them decides what
the relationship should become.

### What this draft is doing

- The heading states the visitor's live question rather than naming a therapy
  principle.
- The main copy block assumes Section 1 has already established when ENM is
  central. It begins by removing relationship structure as the default subject,
  then distinguishes relevant context from the wider picture.
- The contextual examples show consequences rather than defining a
  `contextual concern` category.
- The position block receives its own copy weight because it contains the
  section's main practitioner judgement.
- `Neither monogamy nor non-monogamy` names both directional biases more
  cleanly than the earlier line about deciding direction from a label.
- The draft stops on the relationship decision rather than adding a promise of
  comfort or acceptance.

### Cold visitor reading

**Immediate:** The section advances from the situations above rather than
offering another opening, agreement, or relationship-fit paragraph. The
separate position block receives the strongest emphasis.

**Intellectual:** The visitor can distinguish a central concern, a changed
context, and an unrelated concern. They can also reasonably infer that neither
the relationship label nor one difficult feeling supplies the conclusion.

**Relational:** The copy leaves the visitor's preferred outcome open. It does
not ask an established ENM visitor to defend their relationships, and it does
not cast a wish for monogamy as something less developed.

**Sceptical or adverse:** `I will not steer the work` is a clear statement of
conduct rather than a guarantee of perfect neutrality. The following examples
show what the statement includes, but the block should not accumulate more
promises.

### Main revision pressure

The main copy block is intentionally brief. In full-page assembly, test whether
`family, disclosure or support` is the right set of contextual examples. If one
is weak, remove it rather than restoring the overlapping central-concern
paragraph.

## Candidate B — Decision-led

### Draft

#### What are you trying to work out?

You may be unsure whether non-monogamy fits you at all. You may know that it
does, but not whether a particular relationship, agreement or pace still does.
Or the relationship structure may be settled while the actual concern is
anxiety, grief, shame, trauma or burnout.

Those questions need different kinds of attention. Fit, consent, pressure and
incompatible wants can be examined directly when they are part of the problem.
When ENM is background, it does not need to be pulled into the centre.

Counselling does not need a preferred outcome before those questions can be
asked. The answer may be to continue, change an agreement, close, leave, or
recognise that two people's needs do not fit. It may not be a relationship
decision at all.

### What this draft is doing

- It begins with the visitor's uncertainty rather than Joel's stance.
- It distinguishes fit with non-monogamy from fit with one relationship,
  agreement, or pace.
- It brings consent and pressure into view as real questions without making
  them automatic features of ENM.
- It makes possible outcomes visible and explicitly includes the possibility
  that the concern does not require a relationship decision.

### Cold visitor reading

**Immediate:** The section appears to be for someone making or reconsidering a
decision. The opening is highly relevant to an uncertain visitor.

**Intellectual:** The distinction between non-monogamy fitting and a particular
arrangement fitting is useful. The broader central/context/background
distinction is present but receives less emphasis.

**Relational:** The visitor retains agency over the conclusion. The copy does
not tell a reluctant partner to become comfortable or an ENM visitor to close.

**Sceptical or adverse:** The list of possible conclusions can make the section
sound like a relationship-decision service. A visitor coming about anxiety or
grief may still feel secondary despite being named in the first paragraph.

### Main revision pressure

The final paragraph is over-complete. It tries to prove openness by publishing
every plausible direction. Removing outcomes would improve the prose, but
would also weaken this angle's main organising idea.

## Candidate C — Feeling-led

### Draft

#### A feeling does not decide the relationship

Jealousy can be sharp even when you want non-monogamy. Relief at the thought of
closing can sit beside grief. Anger after an agreement changes may be about
secrecy, consent or losing a say.

The useful question is not which relationship structure a feeling votes for.
It is what happened, what the feeling is responding to, and what you can freely
agree to now.

Sometimes that question puts non-monogamy at the centre of the work. Sometimes
the structure only changes the context around time, family, disclosure or a
breakup. Sometimes it has little to do with why you are coming.

### What this draft is doing

- It enters through a concrete issue already recognisable from the hero and
  Section 1.
- It separates emotional intensity from a predetermined relationship outcome.
- It names consent and agency without turning them into an assessment list.
- It arrives at the central/context/background distinction through one
  developed example rather than beginning with categories.

### Cold visitor reading

**Immediate:** The section appears to challenge the idea that jealousy or
another difficult feeling proves a relationship structure is wrong.

**Intellectual:** The visitor can understand that a feeling may relate to what
happened, consent, loss of agency, or another specific concern rather than act
as a vote for monogamy or non-monogamy.

**Relational:** The draft does not minimise difficult feelings. It leaves room
for both closing and continuing without identifying the preferred conclusion.

**Sceptical or adverse:** The opening may feel as though Joel has begun
interpreting jealousy, grief, and anger before hearing the person. The final
paragraph's repeated `Sometimes` also exposes the research categories too
directly.

### Main revision pressure

This angle is narrower than the section job and repeats earlier material. Its
best distinction can strengthen another draft, but it should not organise the
whole section.

## Recommended direction

Candidate A is the strongest basis for Section 2.

It gives the page plan's main distinction the clearest visitor-facing form. It
also avoids making uncertainty the universal story or jealousy the organising
problem. The refined direction now trusts Section 1 to own the central ENM
situations, so it no longer repeats opening, closing, agreements, or different
relationship wants.

The separate position block is the section's centre of gravity. It states the
owner's preferred judgement directly: neither monogamy nor non-monogamy is the
better outcome, and Joel will not steer the work towards either.

Candidate B's distinction between fit with non-monogamy and fit with a
particular agreement is useful, but importing its full outcomes list would make
the recommendation over-complete. Candidate C's separation of feeling from
verdict is retained in a quieter form in Candidate A's final sentence.

## Model-glue and deletion pass

### Material removed or resisted

- No `ENM can be central, contextual, exploratory, or unrelated` sentence is
  used in the public draft. That is useful planning language but reads like a
  taxonomy.
- No `Every situation is different` sentence is needed. The examples already
  demonstrate difference.
- No claim of a `safe`, `affirming`, `neutral`, or `non-judgemental` space is
  used.
- No statement about `meeting you where you are` or `supporting your journey`
  is added.
- No conclusion explains that this approach `keeps you at the centre`; the
  section has already shown how the focus is chosen.
- No statistics or research comparisons are used to defend non-monogamy.

### Deletion test

- Restoring the deleted opening/closing/agreement paragraph would duplicate
  Section 1 without adding a new judgement.
- Removing the main copy block loses both the context and unrelated-concern
  positions.
- Removing the first two sentences of the position block loses the owner's
  clearest statement of conduct.
- Removing the wish-for-monogamy and continue-non-monogamy sentences makes the
  headline position easier to read as abstract neutrality.
- Removing the final feeling sentence loses the distinction between taking a
  feeling seriously and letting it decide the relationship outcome.

## Inclusion and adverse-reading check

### Established ENM visitor with a central concern

Section 1 has already recognised the central concern. The position block makes
continuing ENM a legitimate direction without exempting every agreement from
examination merely because it is non-monogamous.

### Visitor unsure whether non-monogamy fits

The copy does not restate opening and closing, but the position block makes
clear that uncertainty will not be resolved by Joel selecting monogamy or
non-monogamy as the preferred answer.

### Monogamous or reluctant partner

The final paragraph explicitly protects a wish for monogamy from being treated
as reluctance to eliminate. It does not turn that person into the obstacle to
another partner's preferred structure.

### Visitor who wants to continue non-monogamy

The copy does not use jealousy, anger, insecurity, or a difficult agreement as
evidence that monogamy is the repair. It also does not promise that continuing
is the outcome.

### Visitor coming about another concern

The main copy block gives this position explicit space. ENM can remain ordinary
background context rather than a hidden explanation for anxiety, burnout,
shame, grief, or another concern.

### Visitor dealing with consent, pressure, or harm

The draft does not publish a checklist, but neither does it hide difficult
questions behind a claim of affirmation. `Every agreement workable` and the
wish-for-monogamy sentence leave room for consent, pressure, power, safety, and
harm to be addressed when present.

### Solo-poly, relationship-anarchist, or non-hierarchical visitor

The copy does not presume marriage, nesting, a primary dyad, or a shared couple
project. `People who are not in the room` acknowledges a wider picture without
defining one relationship as primary.

## Research grounding and claim limits

The section is grounded in the ENM client dossier's findings that:

- ENM may be central, contextual, exploratory, or unrelated to the presenting
  concern [F03, F04, F06, W04, W05, W09];
- clients and public contributors reported both monogamy-default framing and
  pro-poly bias, particularly where partners wanted different structures
  [W01, W02, W05-W07, W09];
- clinical and provider literature repeatedly distinguishes understanding the
  person's goals from deciding a preferred relationship form in advance
  [F20-F26];
- a relationship label has sometimes changed provider formulation even when
  the presenting concern was held constant [F05, F24]; and
- difficult questions about consent, agreements, power, safety, and harm can
  remain legitimate without being inferred from ENM itself [F20, F21,
  F24-F26].

The public draft does not turn those findings into claims that harmful care is
universal, that ENM knowledge guarantees helpful therapy, or that one
relationship structure has better outcomes. It contains no prevalence or
comparative-satisfaction claim.
