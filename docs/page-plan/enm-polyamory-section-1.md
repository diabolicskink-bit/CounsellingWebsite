# ENM and polyamory page — Section 1 copy development

**Status:** Working copy exploration, not approved public copy  
**Page:** ENM and polyamory counselling  
**Section:** `Recognisable reasons this page may be relevant`  
**Related plan:** [ENM and polyamory page plan](enm-polyamory-page.md)

## Scope of this document

This document works through the conceptual and editorial choices for the first
section after the hero, then tests three actual copy directions and recommends
one for further development.

It is a copy document, not a layout specification. Subheadings and paragraph
breaks below indicate reading structure only. They do not prescribe cards,
columns, icons, accordions, or any other interface treatment.

The drafts are intentionally selective. They are not attempts to fit the whole
research dossier into one section.

## Decision in brief

Section 1 should be organised around **recognisable situations**, not a list of
ENM topics and not a statement about how an ENM-aware therapist behaves.

The strongest direction begins with a moment or change:

- opening has been raised before someone knows what they think;
- an established arrangement no longer feels settled;
- an agreement, feeling, or change in another relationship has altered the
  situation;
- someone is carrying too much between relationships; or
- two people care about each other but do not want the same relationship
  structure.

This gives the visitor somewhere concrete to locate themselves. It also adds
something the hero does not already do.

## What the hero has already established

The current hero already tells the visitor that:

- the page is about counselling in polyamory, open relationships, and other
  forms of ethical non-monogamy;
- they can use their own language;
- the work will begin with what is happening rather than a prescribed
  relationship shape; and
- jealousy, agreements, pressure, and uncertainty can be discussed without
  treating the structure as the whole explanation.

Section 1 should not paraphrase those promises. Its job is to make them real by
showing the kinds of situations in which they matter.

## The visitor arriving at this section

The visitor has probably understood the subject of the page from the hero. They
may still be asking:

> Do you understand anything close to the situation I am actually in?

Several reader positions need to be able to find a point of recognition:

1. Someone considering opening a relationship.
2. Someone whose partner has raised opening before they know what they want.
3. Someone already practising non-monogamy who is dealing with a rupture,
   change, or difficult feeling.
4. Someone carrying responsibility between relationships and running out of
   capacity.
5. Someone facing a mono/poly difference or questioning whether the current
   structure still fits.
6. Someone whose relationships are relevant context, but who does not want
   every difficulty interpreted as an ENM problem.

The section does not have to name every position equally. It does need enough
range that the page does not quietly imply that ENM counselling is only about
opening a previously monogamous couple or managing jealousy.

## The section's communication job

### Primary job

Turn a broad service category into several lived, credible reasons a person
might be reading now.

### What the visitor should understand

> This page includes situations like mine, and I do not need a perfect term or
> a settled view of non-monogamy before my situation makes sense here.

### What the visitor should feel

Not reassured in the abstract, but accurately recognised.

The useful response is closer to:

> Yes, that is near the problem.

than:

> This therapist says all the correct things about ENM.

### What the section should make possible next

Once a visitor recognises their situation, Section 2 can address a different
question: what role should non-monogamy itself have in the work? It may be the
subject, relevant context, something under exploration, or largely incidental.

Section 1 should lead to that distinction without explaining it in full.

## Editorial choices

### 1. Situations rather than categories

`Jealousy`, `agreements`, `hinge pressure`, and `mono/poly tension` are efficient
labels, but a row of labels makes the page read like a service menu. A visitor
is more likely to recognise:

- hearing the idea of opening before knowing what they think;
- discovering that an agreement meant something different to each person;
- carrying a difficult conversation from one relationship into the next; or
- caring about someone whose idea of a workable relationship does not match
  theirs.

The situation contains the topic while preserving its human meaning.

### 2. The moment of relevance rather than an ENM life summary

This section does not need to describe every way a visitor might organise their
relationships. It needs to capture what made counselling relevant now: a
proposal, a change, a feeling, a breach, a loss, an incompatibility, or an
accumulation of strain.

This keeps the copy active and prevents it becoming a glossary.

### 3. Range without completeness

The research supports a much wider range than one section can carry:
transitions into and out of non-monogamy, agreements, jealousy, time,
metamours, hierarchy, sexual-health agreements, disclosure, stigma, grief,
identity questions, and concerns unrelated to ENM.

The draft should select examples that reveal different kinds of difficulty:

- **decision and agency:** opening, pace, pressure, room to say no;
- **felt experience:** jealousy, comparison, shame, feeling left out;
- **trust and shared meaning:** unclear, changed, differently understood, or
  broken agreements;
- **network and capacity:** time, hinge responsibility, one relationship
  affecting another; and
- **fit and incompatibility:** different desired structures or uncertainty
  about continuing.

Disclosure, stigma, metamour relationships, sexual-health agreements, and
grief remain valid possible material. They do not all need to appear here.
Adding them solely for coverage would weaken recognition rather than broaden
it.

### 4. Tension without a predetermined verdict

The situations need enough tension to feel real. The copy should not resolve
that tension by implying that:

- jealousy means non-monogamy is wrong for someone;
- reluctance means someone needs to become more comfortable;
- a broken agreement is simply monogamous thinking;
- continuing ENM is the mature outcome; or
- returning to monogamy is the healthy outcome.

The section recognises the problem. It does not announce the answer.

### 5. Plain language with selective community vocabulary

`Hinge`, `metamour`, and `mono/poly` may be useful recognition terms for some
readers. None should carry the meaning of a sentence by itself.

For example, `hinge pressure` becomes more legible as:

> carrying messages, reactions, and competing needs between relationships

This allows an experienced ENM reader to recognise the dynamic without making
a newer or differently identified visitor learn terminology first.

## Content priority

### Essential in this section

- The proposal or transition into opening, including the position of the person
  hearing the proposal.
- At least one difficult feeling rendered as experience rather than verdict.
- At least one agreement or trust situation.
- At least one example that shows more than one relationship affecting the
  person's capacity.
- A real difference in desired relationship structure or uncertainty about
  fit.

### Useful if the copy can hold it naturally

- A new relationship or breakup changing time and attention elsewhere.
- Shame about having a feeling someone did not expect themselves to have.
- The possibility that harm from an agreement breach concerns secrecy,
  consent, trust, or risk rather than exclusivity.

### Better held elsewhere or left implicit

- A full explanation of what ENM-aware counselling means.
- Detailed discussion of disclosure, stigma, hierarchy, vetoes, or
  sexual-health practices.
- A general assurance that any issue is welcome.
- A comprehensive list of relationship configurations.
- The full distinction between ENM as subject and ENM as context; Section 2 owns
  that judgement.

## Forecast before drafting

### GOOD outcome

The visitor encounters two or three sentences close enough to their lived
situation that the page feels specific without diagnosing them. An established
ENM reader sees more than an `opening up` story. A reluctant partner is visible
without being cast as an obstacle. The section changes the page from a claim of
familiarity into evidence of understanding.

### BAD outcome

The section becomes a directory of familiar problem labels. It presents ENM as
a sequence of crises, implies a standard primary-couple structure, or treats
the visitor as a relationship system for the therapist to formulate. The copy
sounds comprehensive but no sentence feels lived.

### MODEL-default outcome to resist

The copy opens with `Relationships can be complex`, offers a symmetrical set of
cards, says `there is no right or wrong way to feel`, and closes with `you do
not have to navigate it alone`. It is smooth, supportive, and interchangeable
with almost any counselling page.

Model-glue phrases to challenge in this section include:

- `Whether you are ... or ...`
- `It is common to ...`
- `There is no right way ...`
- `All feelings are valid.`
- `You are not alone.`
- `A safe, non-judgemental space ...`
- `You do not need to have it all figured out.`

The underlying ideas may be useful, but the section should earn them through
the situations it names rather than state them as generic reassurance.

## Angle exploration

Three editorial angles are developed below. They are not merely tone
variations; each organises recognition differently.

### Angle A — A change has made the situation harder to hold

**Organising idea:** Move from opening, to an established arrangement changing,
to strain carried between relationships, to a question of fit.

**Strength:** Feels like a human account of why someone searches for counselling
now. It can include several reader positions without turning them into menu
items.

**Risk:** A series of difficulties may still make ENM appear inherently
unstable. Conditional language and the page context need to make clear that
these are reasons for counselling, not a portrait of ENM generally.

### Angle B — A set of direct recognition statements

**Organising idea:** Present short, self-contained situations that can be
scanned quickly.

**Strength:** A visitor can find their situation with very little effort. It is
clear and easy to retrieve.

**Risk:** It can become the exact `issues grid` the plan is trying to avoid. The
shorter and more parallel the statements become, the more the section resembles
a presentation for practitioners rather than a page for a person inside the
situation.

### Angle C — The problem does not have one neat name

**Organising idea:** Contrast an identifiable event with a diffuse experience,
then show that familiar ENM topics can contain different underlying concerns.

**Strength:** Gives uncertain visitors permission to arrive without a finished
formulation and demonstrates nuanced understanding.

**Risk:** Moves quickly from recognition into interpretation. It can steal
Section 2's role or sound like the therapist explaining the visitor to
themselves.

## Candidate A — Change-led prose

### Draft

#### What might bring you here

You may be considering opening a relationship, or hearing the idea from a
partner before you know what you think — or how much room you have to say it.

You may already be non-monogamous and something has shifted. Jealousy feels
sharper than you expected. An agreement means something different to each
person, or something has happened outside it. A breakup or new relationship has
changed the time, attention, or reassurance available elsewhere.

You may be carrying too much between relationships: managing messages,
anticipating reactions, and trying to keep everyone steady while your own
response gets postponed. Or you may care deeply about someone whose idea of a
workable relationship does not match yours.

The question may be what happened, what you want now, or whether the way you
are relating still fits.

### What this draft is doing

- It opens with both sides of an opening conversation rather than treating the
  person proposing ENM as the default visitor.
- `How much room you have to say it` introduces agency and pressure without
  announcing a conclusion about coercion.
- The middle paragraph moves from feeling to agreement to the effect of change
  elsewhere. It demonstrates range without giving each topic a heading.
- The third paragraph renders hinge and capacity strain in plain language.
- The relationship-structure mismatch is expressed as care plus a genuine
  difference, not as one person's resistance to progress.
- The final sentence moves from event, to desire, to fit. That creates a natural
  handoff into Section 2 without explaining Section 2 in advance.

### Cold visitor reading

**Immediate:** The section is plainly about difficult moments in opening and
ongoing non-monogamy. It does not require the visitor to identify with a topic
label.

**Intellectual:** The therapist appears to distinguish between feelings,
agreements, network effects, agency, and structural fit rather than treating
them as one generic `ENM issue`.

**Relational:** The copy does not tell the visitor which relationship outcome
will count as success. Both the person raising opening and the person hearing it
remain legible.

**Sceptical:** The cluster of difficult situations could still be read as a
portrait of ENM as unstable. This is partly inherent to a counselling-reasons
section. The risk is reduced by `may`, by beginning with a decision rather than
a dysfunction, and by avoiding claims that these experiences are inevitable or
typical.

### Main revision pressure

The second paragraph contains three recognitions in quick succession. In a
final page context, its pace and line length should be tested against the hero
and the opening of Section 2. If it feels like a compressed list, one example
should be removed rather than expanded.

## Candidate B — Direct situation statements

### Draft

#### Is one of these close?

Opening has been raised, and you are still finding out what you want.

You agreed in principle, but the pace or the reality feels different from what
you thought you were agreeing to.

Jealousy, comparison, or feeling left out has become harder to carry — perhaps
along with shame that you feel it at all.

An agreement was vague, remembered differently, changed without a full
conversation, or broken.

You are the person between relationships, and each difficult conversation
seems to follow you into the next.

You care about each other, but one of you wants monogamy and the other does not.

These situations do not all point to the same answer. They are different places
the work might begin.

### What this draft is doing

- It maximises fast recognition.
- Each statement contains a situation rather than a one- or two-word issue
  label.
- It includes opening, consent and pace, jealousy and shame, agreements, hinge
  strain, and mono/poly difference.
- The final line prevents the list from being read as a diagnostic sequence.

### Cold visitor reading

**Immediate:** Very easy to scan. A visitor can quickly select the line that is
closest to their situation.

**Intellectual:** The breadth is clear, but the short statements give less sense
of how Joel thinks about the distinctions between them.

**Relational:** `Is one of these close?` leaves room for approximation. The
closing line is accurate but begins to sound like the therapist categorising
possible starting points.

**Sceptical:** The repeated sentence structure and near-equal weighting produce
a checklist effect. In a visual treatment, it would be especially easy for
this copy to become six cards or service tiles. That would recreate the
presentational quality the user has already rejected.

### Main revision pressure

This is the strongest retrieval draft and the weakest composition. It is useful
as an inventory against which to test coverage, but it should not be the lead
direction unless the page later requires extremely compressed scanning copy.

## Candidate C — No single neat description

### Draft

#### It may not fit one neat description

You might know exactly what happened: opening was raised, an agreement was
broken, or one relationship began to reshape another. Or you may only know that
you feel jealous, stretched thin, left out, or no longer sure what you want.

There may not be one useful label for it. Jealousy can sit beside a genuine wish
for non-monogamy. A broken agreement can hurt because of secrecy, trust,
consent, or risk rather than an expectation of exclusivity. A problem with time
can also be a problem with status, reassurance, or capacity.

You do not need to settle which account is the correct one before bringing it
to counselling.

### What this draft is doing

- It recognises both a clear precipitating event and a more diffuse loss of
  steadiness.
- It makes several evidence-based distinctions that demonstrate ENM fluency
  without defining terminology.
- It loosens the visitor's need to arrive with a single diagnosis of the
  problem.

### Cold visitor reading

**Immediate:** The visitor can recognise uncertainty as a legitimate starting
position, not merely a failure to explain themselves.

**Intellectual:** This draft most clearly demonstrates that the same surface
topic can involve different meanings.

**Relational:** It reduces pressure to present a clean account. The final line,
however, is a familiar therapy-page reassurance and could feel more generic
than the distinctions above it.

**Sceptical:** The second paragraph may sound as though the therapist has begun
formulating the problem before hearing the person. It also starts to perform
Section 2's job by explaining what non-monogamy may or may not mean within the
concern.

### Main revision pressure

This angle contains strong material, especially the distinction between a
broken agreement and expected exclusivity. It is better used as a source of
precision for later page sections than as the organising idea for Section 1.

## Recommended direction

Candidate A is the strongest basis for Section 1.

It offers the best balance of recognition, range, and movement. It reads as one
piece of visitor-facing copy rather than an inventory. It also performs the
right amount of interpretation: enough to show that Joel understands the
situation, but not so much that the visitor is made into a case example or
Section 2 loses its purpose.

The useful material from Candidate C should not be imported wholesale. The
agreement distinction can be held for a later draft if a more precise sentence
is needed, but adding it now would make the section heavier and more
explanatory.

## Recommended working draft

### What might bring you here

You may be considering opening a relationship, or hearing the idea from a
partner before you know what you think — or how much room you have to say it.

You may already be non-monogamous and something has shifted. Jealousy feels
sharper than you expected. An agreement means something different to each
person, or something has happened outside it. A breakup or new relationship has
changed the time, attention, or reassurance available elsewhere.

You may be carrying too much between relationships: managing messages,
anticipating reactions, and trying to keep everyone steady while your own
response gets postponed. Or you may care deeply about someone whose idea of a
workable relationship does not match yours.

The question may be what happened, what you want now, or whether the way you
are relating still fits.

## Sentence-function check

| Copy movement | Function | Why it remains |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Considering or hearing opening | Includes both sides of a live decision | Prevents the section defaulting to the proposer or an already-established ENM reader |
| Room to say what you think | Introduces agency and pressure | Makes the opening situation more exact without deciding what happened |
| Something has shifted | Moves into established non-monogamy | Shows the page is not only about opening a couple |
| Jealousy feels sharper | Names embodied experience | Recognisable without treating the feeling as a verdict |
| Agreement differs or is exceeded | Introduces shared meaning and rupture | Distinguishes agreement concerns from a generic fear of non-monogamy |
| Breakup or new relationship affects elsewhere | Shows network effects | Makes multiple relationships materially relevant without diagramming a system |
| Carrying messages and reactions | Renders hinge/capacity pressure | Uses ordinary language and keeps the individual visitor central |
| Different workable structures | Names genuine incompatibility | Leaves monogamy and non-monogamy available without ranking them |
| What happened / what you want / what fits | Opens the next question | Ends in inquiry rather than reassurance or a verdict |

Every paragraph changes the visitor's understanding. No paragraph exists only
to connect two stronger passages.

## Model-glue and deletion pass

### Phrases deliberately not used

- `Relationships can be complicated.` It adds no ENM-specific recognition.
- `You are not alone.` It is ungrounded and shifts from recognition to generic
  reassurance.
- `There is no right or wrong way to feel.` The hero and final page stance can
  do this more credibly through distinctions.
- `You do not need to have it all figured out.` The first and last sentences
  already make uncertainty legible without using the stock phrase.
- `A safe space to explore ...` This is a self-awarded quality rather than
  evidence.
- `Whether you are new to ENM or have been polyamorous for years ...` This
  familiar construction classifies the reader broadly but does not recognise a
  situation.

### Deletion test

- Removing the first paragraph loses the opening conversation and the question
  of agency.
- Removing the second loses established ENM, agreements, feelings, and the
  effect of change across relationships.
- Removing the third loses the individual experience of networked strain and a
  genuine structural mismatch.
- Removing the final line leaves the section as a catalogue of problems rather
  than an opening into reflection.

The section could be shortened by removing one sentence from the second
paragraph. If reduction is needed, the `breakup or new relationship` sentence
is the first candidate because network effects also appear in the following
paragraph. It should be deleted, not replaced with a more general sentence.

## Inclusion and adverse-reading check

### Established ENM reader

The draft includes ongoing non-monogamy, agreements, multiple-relationship
effects, and capacity. It does not make opening a formerly monogamous couple the
whole story.

### Person hearing a request to open

The first paragraph makes uncertainty and room to speak visible. It does not
frame reluctance as a skill deficit.

### Person proposing opening

They remain included through `considering opening a relationship`. The draft
does not presume they are pressuring someone merely because they raised it.

### Mono/poly difference

`A workable relationship does not match yours` treats the difference as real
without casting either person as more developed or more authentic.

### Solo-poly, relationship-anarchist, or non-hierarchical reader

The draft does not presume a primary couple, cohabitation, marriage, hierarchy,
or a shared project of `opening up`. The opening example is one situation among
several rather than the page's universal frame.

### Visitor whose main concern is not ENM

This reader may not yet fully see themselves in Section 1. That is acceptable
only because Section 2 explicitly owns the distinction between non-monogamy as
the subject and as background context. Section 1 should not absorb that section
by adding a generic `or anything else` paragraph.

### Visitor dealing with coercion or harm

The draft makes agency visible but does not use a broad ENM label to infer abuse
or safety. Later copy must retain the ability to name consent, power, safety,
and harm directly when those are the concern.

## Research grounding

The section draws on the ENM client research dossier's findings that:

- ENM may be the presenting concern, relevant context, under exploration, or
  unrelated to the reason for counselling;
- counselling situations can include transitions into or out of CNM,
  communication, agreements, boundaries, expectations, jealousy, time
  allocation, new partners, metamour relationships, stigma, and unrelated
  concerns;
- pressure, mono/poly incompatibility, agreement breaches, hierarchy, risk
  changes, disclosure, grief, and capacity appear as distinct situations rather
  than one universal ENM experience; and
- people use varied language and should not have to adopt a preferred identity
  term before their concern can be understood.

Primary internal references: F03, F04, F06, F07, F31–F44, W01–W09, and the
situations table in
[the ENM and polyamory clients dossier](../research/audiences/enm-polyamory-clients.md).

No prevalence statistic or claim about what is `common` has been used. The
research does not provide a representative distribution of counselling reasons
and the copy should not imply one.

## Handoff into Section 2

The recommended final sentence is designed to carry the reader forward:

> The question may be what happened, what you want now, or whether the way you
> are relating still fits.

Section 2 can then shift from **recognising the situation** to **deciding the
role non-monogamy should have in the work**. It should not begin by summarising
the examples again.

The clean conceptual movement is:

> This may be close to what is happening.  
> Now: how much of the work is actually about non-monogamy?

That second line is a planning statement, not proposed public copy.

## Questions to resolve in the full-page draft

1. Does `ethical non-monogamy` remain the leading umbrella term, or will the
   page deliberately mix `ENM`, `non-monogamy`, and more specific terms?
2. Should `mono/poly` appear visibly later on the page, or is the plain-language
   mismatch sentence enough?
3. Does Joel want a sexual-health agreement or changing-risk example anywhere
   on the page? It has deliberately not been added here without a confirmed
   counselling-versus-medical boundary.
4. Once Section 2 is drafted, does the final sentence of Section 1 create a
   useful handoff or repeat the next heading?
5. In the full page rhythm, is the second paragraph readable as prose, or does
   one example need to be removed?

## Acceptance check for this section

Before the section is treated as ready for implementation, a cold reader should
be able to answer yes to the following:

- I can recognise a situation rather than merely an issue label.
- The page includes both people considering opening and people already living
  non-monogamy.
- The person hearing a request to open has room in the copy.
- Jealousy is present without being used as evidence against non-monogamy.
- An agreement concern is not reduced to a demand for exclusivity.
- Multiple relationships affect the situation without the individual
  disappearing into a system diagram.
- A genuine difference in desired relationship structure remains possible.
- I am not told what the relationship should become.
- The section does not read like training material for therapists.
- The next section still has a distinct and necessary job.
